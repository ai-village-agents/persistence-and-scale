## RESEARCH FOUNDATION

**Primary Source 1:** Miller, G. A. (1956). "The magical number seven, plus or minus two: Some limits on our capacity for processing information." *Psychological Review*, 63(2), 81-97.

**Primary Source 2:** Cowan, N. (2001). "The magical number 4 in short-term memory: A reconsideration of mental storage capacity." *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 24(1), 87-114.

**The Evolution:**
- Miller's 1956 paper proposed a span of seven plus or minus two items when rehearsal and chunking are allowed.
- Cowan's 2001 reconsideration emphasized that true working memory, without rehearsal, averages about four chunks or bindings.
- Together they explain the difference between immediate memory span (with rehearsal) and the core attentional focus of working memory.
- Subsequent meta-analyses confirm that the four-chunk limit holds even when using spatial, verbal, or multimodal stimuli.

**Classic Demonstration:**
- Digit span experiments where sequences of numbers are read aloud at one per second.
- Participants can typically repeat 7±2 digits when they can rehearse or group them into meaningful clusters.
- When rehearsal is prevented (e.g., articulatory suppression) accuracy collapses around four discrete digits.
- Variations using colored squares, tones, or spatial locations replicate the capacity drop without chunking options.

**Replication:**
- More than 65 years of replications across laboratories, cultures, and modalities.
- Cowan's integrated model (embedded-processes) has been validated through neuroimaging, developmental, and aging studies.
- Working memory span tasks (change detection, N-back, complex span) converge on the four-unit capacity when controlling rehearsal.
- Reliability is high; effect sizes are large, making this one of cognitive psychology's most robust findings.

**Key Insight:** Our conscious processing capacity is shockingly limited—without chunking, we can juggle only about four distinct items at once.
