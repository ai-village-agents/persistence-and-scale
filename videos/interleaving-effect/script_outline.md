## RESEARCH FOUNDATION

**Primary Source:** Rohrer, D., & Taylor, K. (2007). "The shuffling of mathematics problems improves learning." *Instructional Science*, 35(6), 481-498.

**Classic Experiment - Math Practice:**
- Group A: Blocked practice (20 problems of type X, then 20 of type Y, then 20 of type Z)
- Group B: Interleaved practice (Problems X, Y, Z mixed randomly across 60 problems)
- Test (1 week later): Group B scored 43% higher despite identical practice time
- Paradox: Blocked practice feels easier during learning, but retention is worse

**Replication:** 20+ years validated across domains (math, sports, music, language learning)

**Key Insight:** Mixing related skills improves long-term retention more than practicing one skill at a time.

## NARRATIVE STRUCTURE (11 FRAMES)

### Frame 1: Hook (0:00-0:18, 18s)
**Visual:** Student practicing basketball - all free throws, then all layups, then all three-pointers (labeled "BLOCKED")
**Text:** "Practice: Free throws → Layups → 3-pointers"
**Narration:** "You're learning basketball. You practice twenty free throws, then twenty layups, then twenty three-pointers. Organized. Logical. Efficient. This is how most people practice. And it's wrong."

### Frame 2: The Better Way (0:18-0:38, 20s)
**Visual:** Same student - free throw, layup, three-pointer, layup, free throw (mixed, labeled "INTERLEAVED")
**Text:** "Practice: Free throw → Layup → 3-pointer → Layup → Free throw (mixed)"
**Narration:** "Here's what works better: mix them up. Free throw, layup, three-pointer, layup, free throw—random order. It feels harder. You'll miss more during practice. But one week later, you'll remember more. This is the interleaving effect."

### Frame 3: The Researchers (0:38-0:55, 17s)
**Visual:** "Rohrer & Bjork (2007)" text with math problems illustrated
**Text:** "Doug Rohrer & Robert Bjork (2007)"
**Narration:** "In 2007, psychologists Doug Rohrer and Robert Bjork tested this with math students. One group practiced problem types in blocks. The other group mixed them randomly. Same practice time. One week later, the mixed group scored forty-three percent higher."

### Frame 4: The Results (0:55-1:12, 17s)
**Visual:** Bar chart - Blocked practice: 20% retention vs Interleaved practice: 63% retention
**Text:** "Blocked: 20% correct | Interleaved: 63% correct (1 week later)"
**Narration:** "Blocked practice: twenty percent correct on the delayed test. Interleaved practice: sixty-three percent. That's not a small improvement. That's the difference between forgetting and remembering. And it happened with the same amount of practice."

### Frame 5: Why It Happens (1:12-1:35, 23s)
**Visual:** Brain with two pathways - "Recognition" (easy, weak) vs "Discrimination" (hard, strong)
**Text:** "Blocked = Recognition | Interleaved = Discrimination"
**Narration:** "Blocked practice trains recognition. You see twenty free throws in a row, your brain memorizes the pattern—repeat, repeat, repeat. Easy. Interleaved practice trains discrimination. Every problem requires you to identify which strategy fits. That extra cognitive load forces deeper encoding, which means stronger memory."

### Frame 6: The Counterintuitive Part (1:35-1:52, 17s)
**Visual:** Two faces - one happy (blocked practice), one frustrated (interleaved practice)
**Text:** "Blocked feels easier | Interleaved feels harder"
**Narration:** "Here's the trap: blocked practice feels better during learning. You make fewer mistakes. Progress feels smooth. Interleaved practice feels frustrating—you stumble more, second-guess yourself. But that struggle is the signal that learning is happening."

### Frame 7: Real-World Example 1 - Language Learning (1:52-2:10, 18s)
**Visual:** Flashcards with vocabulary words - first showing blocked (all verbs, all nouns), then interleaved (verb, noun, adjective, verb)
**Text:** "Blocked: All verbs, all nouns | Interleaved: Verb, noun, adjective, verb"
**Narration:** "Learning Spanish? Don't study all verb conjugations, then all noun genders, then all adjectives. Mix them. A verb, a noun, an adjective, a verb. Force your brain to switch gears. It's harder in the moment, but you'll speak more fluently later."

### Frame 8: Real-World Example 2 - Music (2:10-2:28, 18s)
**Visual:** Piano keyboard with sheet music showing scales, then arpeggios, then chords (blocked vs mixed)
**Text:** "Blocked: Scale, scale, scale | Interleaved: Scale, arpeggio, chord, scale"
**Narration:** "Playing piano? Don't drill scales for thirty minutes, then arpeggios, then chords. Alternate them. One scale, one arpeggio, one chord progression, back to a scale. You'll master them faster because your brain learns to choose the right technique in real time."

### Frame 9: Three Practical Takeaways (2:28-2:45, 17s)
**Visual:** Three boxes with icons
**Text:** "1. NOTICE: Am I blocking by topic?  2. MIX: Shuffle related skills  3. REMEMBER: Struggle = learning"
**Narration:** "Three moves: First, notice when you're practicing one skill in isolation. Second, deliberately shuffle related skills within the same session. Third, remember—if it feels too easy, you're probably not learning as much as you think. Struggle is the signal."

### Frame 10: The Rohrer-Bjork Lesson (2:45-2:55, 10s)
**Visual:** Puzzle pieces being shuffled, then fitting together smoothly
**Text:** "Mix the pieces. Build the picture."
**Narration:** "Rohrer and Bjork showed us: the path to mastery isn't repeating one move until it's perfect. It's mixing moves until your brain learns when to use each one. Shuffle the practice. Strengthen the memory."
