## RESEARCH FOUNDATION

**Primary Source:** Ross, L. (1977). "The intuitive psychologist and his shortcomings: Distortions in the attribution process." In L. Berkowitz (Ed.), *Advances in Experimental Social Psychology* (Vol. 10, pp. 173-220).

**Classic Demonstration - The Quiz Study (Ross, Amabile & Steinmetz, 1977):**
- Participants randomly assigned: "Questioner" (create trivia questions) or "Contestant" (answer them)
- Questioners create obscure questions from personal knowledge
- Contestants struggle to answer (expected, given random assignment)
- Observers rate: Questioners perceived as significantly more knowledgeable
- Reality: Roles were random. Performance reflects situation, not ability.

**Replication:** 45+ years validated, fundamental concept in social psychology

**Key Insight:** We attribute others' behavior to personality while ignoring situational forces. We blame the person, not the context.

## NARRATIVE STRUCTURE (10 FRAMES)

### Frame 1: Hook (0:00-0:20, 20s)
**Visual:** Traffic scene - car cuts you off aggressively
**Text:** "That driver just cut you off. What do you think?"
**Narration:** "A car cuts you off in traffic. What's your first thought? 'What a jerk. Aggressive driver.' Not: 'Maybe they're rushing to the hospital.' Not: 'Maybe they didn't see me.' You assumed it's who they are, not what they're dealing with. This is the fundamental attribution error."

### Frame 2: The Term (0:20-0:35, 15s)
**Visual:** "Fundamental Attribution Error" text with definition
**Text:** "Overestimate personality, Underestimate situation"
**Narration:** "The fundamental attribution error: we overestimate personality traits and underestimate situational pressures when explaining others' behavior. We see the person, not the context."

### Frame 3: The Researcher (0:35-0:50, 15s)
**Visual:** "Lee Ross (1977)" text with experiment illustration
**Text:** "Lee Ross (1977) - The Quiz Study"
**Narration:** "Stanford psychologist Lee Ross coined the term in 1977. His classic demonstration: the quiz study. Participants were randomly assigned roles—questioner or contestant. Questioners created trivia questions. Contestants tried to answer."

### Frame 4: The Setup (0:50-1:08, 18s)
**Visual:** Two people - one labeled "Questioner" (creating questions), one labeled "Contestant" (struggling)
**Text:** "Random assignment → Questioner creates hard questions → Contestant struggles"
**Narration:** "The questioners crafted obscure questions from their personal knowledge. 'What street did I grow up on?' The contestants, of course, couldn't answer. This was by design—the roles guaranteed the outcome. Everyone knew the roles were random."

### Frame 5: The Bias Revealed (1:08-1:25, 17s)
**Visual:** Bar chart - Observers rate Questioner "More knowledgeable" than Contestant
**Text:** "Observers judged: Questioners MORE knowledgeable than Contestants"
**Narration:** "Observers watched and rated the participants. Despite knowing the roles were random, they judged questioners as significantly more knowledgeable than contestants. They ignored the situation—random role assignment—and attributed the performance to personality."

### Frame 6: Why It Happens (1:25-1:45, 20s)
**Visual:** Brain with spotlight on person, situation in shadow
**Text:** "Person = Visible | Situation = Invisible"
**Narration:** "Why? Because people are visible. Behavior jumps out at us. Situations are invisible—context, constraints, pressures fade into the background. So we default to explaining what we see: the person. This is cognitively easier than analyzing the full situational context."

### Frame 7: Real-World Example - Workplace (1:45-2:03, 18s)
**Visual:** Colleague missing deadline, labeled "Lazy?" vs thought bubble showing family emergency
**Text:** "Missed deadline → 'They're lazy' (vs family emergency)"
**Narration:** "A colleague misses a deadline. You think: 'They're lazy. Unreliable.' You don't know they were up all night with a sick child or dealing with a family emergency. You see the behavior—late work—and blame character, not circumstance."

### Frame 8: Three Practical Takeaways (2:03-2:23, 20s)
**Visual:** Three boxes with icons
**Text:** "1. NOTICE: Am I blaming the person?  2. ASK: What situation might explain this?  3. REMEMBER: Context is invisible"
**Narration:** "Three moves: First, notice when you're blaming someone's personality for behavior. Second, ask: 'What situational pressures might explain this?' Third, remember—you're seeing the actor, not the stage. Context is invisible but powerful."

### Frame 9: The Ross Lesson (2:23-2:40, 17s)
**Visual:** Two images side by side - one person highlighted, another with spotlight on surrounding context
**Text:** "See the stage, not just the actor."
**Narration:** "Lee Ross showed us: we're bad intuitive psychologists. We judge people without seeing the stage they're on. Before you conclude someone is rude, lazy, or incompetent, ask what situational forces you're missing. The error is fundamental, but it's not inevitable."
