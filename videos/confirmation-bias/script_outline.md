## RESEARCH FOUNDATION

**Primary Source:** Wason, P. C. (1968). "Reasoning about a rule." *Quarterly Journal of Experimental Psychology*, 20(3), 273-281.

**The 2-4-6 Task:**
- Participants given rule: "Three numbers in ascending order"
- Example: 2-4-6
- Task: Generate triples to discover the rule
- Bias: Most test confirming cases (8-10-12) rather than disconfirming (5-3-1)
- Result: 80% fail to discover rule quickly due to confirmation-seeking

**Replication:** 10+ years validated, foundational to cognitive bias research

**Key Insight:** We seek evidence that confirms our beliefs rather than testing them.

## NARRATIVE STRUCTURE (11 FRAMES)

### Frame 1: Hook (0:00-0:15, 15s)
**Visual:** Dark screen with three glowing numbers: "2 - 4 - 6"
**Text:** "2   4   6" (centered, large)
**Narration:** "Here's a puzzle. I'm thinking of a rule that these three numbers follow. Your job? Figure out the rule by suggesting other triples. Ready?"

### Frame 2: The Trap (0:15-0:30, 15s)
**Visual:** Multiple sets appearing: 8-10-12, 20-22-24, 100-102-104 (all with checkmarks)
**Text:** "8-10-12 ✓  20-22-24 ✓  100-102-104 ✓"
**Narration:** "Most people try triples like 8-10-12, or 20-22-24. All correct! They feel confident. The rule must be 'even numbers increasing by two,' right?"

### Frame 3: The Reveal (0:30-0:50, 20s)
**Visual:** "1-2-3" appears with checkmark. "5-17-23" appears with checkmark. "RULE: Any ascending numbers"
**Text:** "1-2-3 ✓  5-17-23 ✓  RULE: Any ascending numbers"
**Narration:** "Wrong. The actual rule is simpler: any three ascending numbers. 1-2-3 works. 5-17-23 works. You missed it because you only tested cases that confirmed your hunch. This is confirmation bias."

### Frame 4: The Researcher (0:50-1:05, 15s)
**Visual:** Split screen - Left: "Peter Wason, 1968" text, Right: Simple illustration of experiment setup
**Text:** "Peter Wason (1968)"
**Narration:** "In 1968, psychologist Peter Wason discovered this in his famous 2-4-6 task. Eighty percent of participants got stuck testing confirming examples instead of trying to break their theory."

### Frame 5: Why It Happens (1:05-1:25, 20s)
**Visual:** Brain diagram with "Belief" highlighted, arrows pointing to "Confirming Evidence" (thick arrow) vs "Disconfirming Evidence" (thin arrow)
**Text:** "Belief → Confirming Evidence" (thick), "Belief → Disconfirming Evidence" (thin)
**Narration:** "Our brains are wired to seek patterns and protect them. Disconfirming evidence feels threatening. Confirming evidence feels validating. So we naturally gravitate toward tests that say 'yes' rather than 'no.'"

### Frame 6: Real-World Example 1 - Medical Diagnosis (1:25-1:45, 20s)
**Visual:** Doctor icon with checklist, highlighting only symptoms that match initial diagnosis
**Text:** "Initial diagnosis: Flu → Only checks flu symptoms"
**Narration:** "In medicine, confirmation bias can be deadly. A doctor suspects flu and checks fever, cough, fatigue. But what if it's meningitis? Without testing disconfirming evidence, rare but serious conditions get missed."

### Frame 7: Real-World Example 2 - Hiring (1:45-2:00, 15s)
**Visual:** Resume with positive traits highlighted, negative signals grayed out
**Text:** "Impressive resume → Ignores red flags"
**Narration:** "In hiring, you like a candidate's resume and ask questions that showcase their strengths. But you skip the hard probes—gaps in employment, job-hopping—that might reveal problems."

### Frame 8: The Scientific Method Defense (2:00-2:20, 20s)
**Visual:** Scientific method flowchart with "FALSIFICATION" emphasized
**Text:** "Hypothesis → Test to DISPROVE → Survived? Stronger."
**Narration:** "Science fights confirmation bias through falsification. You don't just test if your theory works. You actively try to break it. If it survives, it's stronger. This is why peer review and replication matter—they're institutionalized skepticism."

### Frame 9: Three Practical Takeaways (2:20-2:35, 15s)
**Visual:** Three boxes with icons
**Text:** "1. ASK: What would prove me wrong?  2. TEST: Seek disconfirming evidence  3. INVITE: Challenge from others"
**Narration:** "Three moves: First, ask 'What would prove me wrong?' Second, actively test the opposite. Third, invite people who disagree to poke holes. Treat your beliefs like hypotheses, not identities."

### Frame 10: The Wason Lesson (2:35-2:45, 10s)
**Visual:** Return to "2-4-6" with new examples: "1-2-3 ✓", "10-5-3 ✗"
**Text:** "Test the edges. Break your theory."
**Narration:** "Wason showed us: the fastest path to truth isn't confirming what you believe. It's trying to break it."
