## RESEARCH FOUNDATION

**Primary Source:** Kruger, J., & Dunning, D. (1999). "Unskilled and unaware of it: How difficulties in recognizing one's own incompetence lead to inflated self-assessments." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 77(6), 1121-1134.

**The Classic Study:**
- Participants completed humor, grammar, and logical reasoning tests
- Performance scored objectively; participants estimated their percentile rank
- Lowest quartile averaged poorly yet estimated themselves at the 62nd percentile
- Highest quartile slightly underestimated their own performance despite excelling
- Revealed the gap between actual skill and perceived competence

**Key Finding:** Incompetence creates a double curse: poor performance paired with the metacognitive blindness needed to spot that failure.

**Replication:** Over 25 years of replications across driving, chess, medical diagnosis, academic skills, financial literacy, and workplace performance.

**Key Insight:** The cognitive tools required to perform well are the same tools needed to evaluate performance accurately.

**Secondary Sources & Commentary:**
- Ehrlinger et al. (2008) applied findings to college exam predictions
- Atir et al. (2015) showed overconfidence in domains outside one's expertise
- Cultural replications indicate the effect persists in collectivist and individualist contexts
- Distinguish from impostor syndrome: high performers accurately calibrate or slightly underestimate, but still possess metacognitive skill

**Open Questions:**
- Can metacognitive training meaningfully reduce the effect long-term?
- What role does feedback frequency play in calibrating self-assessment?
- How do digital platforms that provide instant analytics mitigate or reinforce the illusion?

**Text:** "Metacognitive blind spot"
