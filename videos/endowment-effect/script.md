# Video 17: The Endowment Effect
## Script (Target: 2:45-3:00, ~135-140 wpm)

### Frame 1 (5s) - Title + Hook
**Visual:** Title "The Endowment Effect" with ownership icon
**Audio:** "Why do we value things more the moment we own them?"

### Frame 2 (18s) - The Asymmetry
**Visual:** Split screen showing price tags - "Will Pay: $3" vs "Will Sell: $7" for same mug
**Audio:** "In a classic experiment, researchers gave people coffee mugs. Then they asked: How much would you sell it for? The average answer: seven dollars. They asked others who didn't get a mug: How much would you pay? Three dollars. Same mug. Same people. The only difference: ownership."

### Frame 3 (20s) - The Pattern
**Visual:** Multiple examples - concert tickets, used cars, sentimental objects
**Audio:** "This isn't just about mugs. Concert tickets you bought feel more valuable than identical ones you could buy. The car you own seems worth more than the market offers. Objects with history feel irreplaceable even when functionally identical replacements exist. We overvalue what's ours simply because it's ours."

### Frame 4 (18s) - Why It Happens
**Visual:** Two forces diagram - Loss Aversion + Identity
**Audio:** "Two forces drive this. First, loss aversion. Losing something you have feels worse than missing out on gaining it. Second, identity. Once you own something, it becomes part of your story, your space, your sense of self. Letting go isn't just a transaction. It feels like loss."

### Frame 5 (18s) - The Cost
**Visual:** Cluttered space, sunk cost decisions, negotiation deadlock
**Audio:** "The endowment effect has costs. It makes decluttering harder. It keeps us holding onto things we don't use because we owned them first. It creates deadlocks in negotiations when both sides overvalue what they have. It turns sunk costs into anchors."

### Frame 6 (20s) - The Antidote
**Visual:** Question visualization - "If I didn't own this, would I buy it?"
**Audio:** "There's a simple antidote. Ask: If I didn't already own this, would I choose to buy it at the price I'm valuing it? This flips the frame. It bypasses ownership bias. It reveals whether you value the thing itself, or just the fact that it's yours."

### Frame 7 (15s) - Takeaway Part 1
**Visual:** Eye icon with "Notice"
**Audio:** "Three things to try. First, notice when you're resisting letting go of something. Ask yourself: Is this about the object, or about ownership?"

### Frame 8 (15s) - Takeaway Part 2  
**Visual:** Question mark icon with "Ask"
**Audio:** "Second, use the reversal question. If you didn't own it, would you acquire it now? Be honest. The answer reveals whether you're valuing the thing or the attachment."

### Frame 9 (15s) - Takeaway Part 3
**Visual:** Balance/clarity icon
**Audio:** "And third, remember: Ownership changes perception. The moment something becomes yours, you see it differently. Knowing this helps you see more clearly."

**Audio:** [silence or soft outro]
