## RESEARCH FOUNDATION

**Primary Source:** Arkes, H. R., & Blumer, C. (1985). "The psychology of sunk cost." *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*, 35(1), 124-140.

**Classic Experiment - Theater Tickets:**
- Scenario A: You paid $10 for theater ticket, discover play is boring after 15 minutes
- Scenario B: You won free theater ticket, discover play is boring after 15 minutes
- Finding: People in Scenario A significantly more likely to stay (78% vs 42%)
- Rational decision: Past cost irrelevant, future enjoyment is what matters
- Result: We let irrecoverable costs influence forward-looking decisions

**Replication:** 35+ years validated across cultures, domains (business, relationships, projects)

**Key Insight:** We throw good money (or time) after bad because we hate admitting waste.

## NARRATIVE STRUCTURE (11 FRAMES)

### Frame 1: Hook (0:00-0:18, 18s)
**Visual:** Movie theater seat, watch showing time passing (15 minutes), person looking bored
**Text:** "You paid $50. The movie is terrible. Do you stay?"
**Narration:** "You're 15 minutes into a movie. You paid fifty dollars for the ticket. The movie is awful—boring, predictable, painful to watch. Do you stay for two more hours? Most people say yes. Why?"

### Frame 2: The Trap Revealed (0:18-0:38, 20s)
**Visual:** Split screen - Left: "$50 GONE" (grayed out), Right: "2 HOURS" (highlighted)
**Text:** "Past: $50 (sunk) | Future: 2 hours (your choice)"
**Narration:** "Because you paid fifty dollars. But here's the problem: that money is already gone. Staying or leaving doesn't change that. The only choice you have is about the next two hours. This is the sunk cost fallacy."

### Frame 3: The Researchers (0:38-0:55, 17s)
**Visual:** "Arkes & Blumer (1985)" text with experiment setup illustration
**Text:** "Hal Arkes & Catherine Blumer (1985)"
**Narration:** "In 1985, psychologists Hal Arkes and Catherine Blumer ran an experiment with theater tickets. Scenario A: you paid ten dollars. Scenario B: you got it free. In both cases, the play is boring. Who stays longer?"

### Frame 4: The Results (0:55-1:12, 17s)
**Visual:** Bar chart showing 78% (Scenario A - paid) vs 42% (Scenario B - free)
**Text:** "Paid $10: 78% stayed | Free ticket: 42% stayed"
**Narration:** "Scenario A—people who paid—stayed through the boring play seventy-eight percent of the time. Scenario B—free tickets—only forty-two percent stayed. Same terrible play, different behavior. The past cost changed future decisions."

### Frame 5: Why It Happens (1:12-1:32, 20s)
**Visual:** Brain with "LOSS AVERSION" label, arrow pointing to pile of money burning
**Text:** "Admitting waste = Psychological pain"
**Narration:** "We hate waste. Walking out feels like admitting you made a bad decision. The fifty dollars burns brighter in your mind than the two hours you're about to lose. Psychologists call this loss aversion—losses hurt more than equivalent gains feel good."

### Frame 6: Real-World Example 1 - Career (1:32-1:52, 20s)
**Visual:** Person at desk, calendar showing years, diploma on wall
**Text:** "6 years in law school → Hate being a lawyer → Stay anyway"
**Narration:** "You spent six years in law school, passed the bar, and now you hate practicing law. But you think, 'I can't quit—I've invested too much.' So you stay miserable for twenty more years. The six years are sunk. The twenty years ahead are your choice."

### Frame 7: Real-World Example 2 - Projects (1:52-2:10, 18s)
**Visual:** Project dashboard showing "$2M spent, 0% complete"
**Text:** "Project: $2M invested, behind schedule, unlikely to succeed"
**Narration:** "In business, a project is two million dollars over budget and six months behind schedule. Data shows it will likely fail. But the team argues, 'We've invested too much to quit now.' So they throw another million at it. That's sunk cost fallacy compounding."

### Frame 8: The Rational Alternative (2:10-2:28, 18s)
**Visual:** Decision tree with "PAST" grayed out, "FUTURE" highlighted with two paths
**Text:** "Ignore the past. Ask: What maximizes future value?"
**Narration:** "Rational decision-making ignores sunk costs. You ask: 'Given where I am right now, what choice maximizes future value?' Not: 'How do I justify past spending?' The past is a lesson, not a chain."

### Frame 9: Three Practical Takeaways (2:28-2:43, 15s)
**Visual:** Three boxes with icons
**Text:** "1. NOTICE: Am I justifying the past?  2. ASK: What would I do if I started today?  3. REMEMBER: Cut your losses"
**Narration:** "Three moves: First, notice when you're justifying the past instead of optimizing the future. Second, ask: 'If I were starting from scratch today, would I make this same choice?' Third, remember—cutting your losses is often the smartest move."

### Frame 10: The Arkes-Blumer Lesson (2:43-2:50, 7s)
**Visual:** Theater exit door glowing, person walking toward it confidently
**Text:** "The exit is always there."
**Narration:** "Arkes and Blumer showed us: the door is always open. Walking out isn't failure. It's choosing your future over your past."
