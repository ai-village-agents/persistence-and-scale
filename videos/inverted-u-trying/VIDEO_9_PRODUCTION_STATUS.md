### Frame 2: Hook (10 sec, 0:05-0:15)
**Visual:** frame_02_hook.png
**Audio:** "Have you ever tried so hard to do something that you actually got worse at it? Gripped the thread so tightly your hand started shaking? Tried so hard to remember a name that it disappeared completely?"
**Narration Time:** ~10 seconds

### Frame 3: The Curve (15 sec, 0:15-0:30)
**Visual:** inverted_u_curve_simple.png
**Audio:** "There's a pattern here, and it's called the inverted U. At low effort, performance is low. As you try harder, you get better. But past a certain point, trying harder makes you worse. The relationship isn't linear—it's a curve."
**Narration Time:** ~15 seconds

### Frame 4: Example 1 - Thread (20 sec, 0:30-0:50)
**Visual:** frame_04_comparison.png
**Audio:** "Think about threading a needle. When you're relaxed and focused, your hand is steady. But if you tense up and try really hard—maybe you're frustrated, maybe you're in a hurry—your hand starts shaking. The harder you grip, the less control you have. You've moved past the peak of the curve."
**Narration Time:** ~20 seconds

### Frame 5: Example 2 - Remembering (20 sec, 0:50-1:10)
**Visual:** frame_05_remembering.png
**Audio:** "Or remembering something. A name, a word, a fact. When you try gently, it might surface. But when you push really hard, wrack your brain, strain to recall it—nothing. It's only when you stop trying that it pops back in. Again, too much effort pushed you over the peak."
**Narration Time:** ~20 seconds

### Frame 6: Example 3 - Sleep (20 sec, 1:10-1:30)
**Visual:** frame_06_sleep.png
**Audio:** "Or falling asleep. The more you tell yourself 'I have to fall asleep right now,' the more awake you become. Trying to force sleep is the opposite of sleeping. You can't effort your way there. You have to let it happen. The harder you try, the worse it gets."
**Narration Time:** ~20 seconds

### Frame 7: Why It Happens (20 sec, 1:30-1:50)
**Visual:** frame_07_brain_body_minimal.png
**Audio:** "Why does this happen? Because trying too hard activates your stress response. Your muscles tense. Your attention narrows. Your fine motor control suffers. You're now over-aroused, past the optimal zone. What you need isn't more effort—it's less."
**Narration Time:** ~20 seconds

### Frame 8: Recognition (20 sec, 1:50-2:10)
**Visual:** frame_08_recognition_minimal.png
**Audio:** "So how do you recognize when you're over-trying? Notice physical tension—are your shoulders tight, your jaw clenched, your grip too hard? Notice mental strain—are you forcing, pushing, efforting? And notice results—are you getting worse instead of better? These are signs you've crossed the peak."
**Narration Time:** ~20 seconds

### Frame 9: The Shift (25 sec, 2:10-2:35)
**Visual:** frame_09_grip_transformation.png
**Audio:** "When you notice you're over-trying, the solution isn't to try harder. It's to back off. Ask yourself: what would this look like if it were easier? Instead of giving it 110%, try 80%. Instead of gripping, try holding lightly. You're not giving up—you're moving back toward the peak of the curve, where performance is actually highest."
**Narration Time:** ~25 seconds

### Frame 10: Takeaway (20 sec, 2:35-2:55)
**Visual:** frame_10_takeaway_minimal.png ⭐
**Audio:** "Here's what to practice. One: Notice when you're efforting—physical tension, mental strain. Two: Ask, what would this look like if it were easier? And three: Try 80% effort instead of 110%. Ease off, and watch what happens. Sometimes the way to get better is to try a little less."
**Narration Time:** ~20 seconds

### Frame 11: Closing (5 sec, 2:55-3:00)
**Visual:** frame_11_closing.png
**Audio:** "Find your peak, not your limit."
**Narration Time:** ~5 seconds
