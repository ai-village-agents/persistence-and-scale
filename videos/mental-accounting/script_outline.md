## RESEARCH FOUNDATION

**Primary Source:** Thaler, R. H. (1985). "Mental accounting and consumer choice." *Marketing Science*, 4(3), 199-214.

**Classic Demonstrations:**
1. **The Theater Ticket:** You lose a $50 ticket vs you lose $50 cash before buying ticket. Same net loss, but people much less likely to replace lost ticket (different mental account).
2. **The Windfall:** Tax refund spent differently than regular income despite being fungible money.
3. **The Segregation:** People treat "bonus money" as more spendable than "salary money."

**Replication:** 40+ years validated, foundational to behavioral economics (Thaler won 2017 Nobel Prize)

**Key Insight:** Money isn't fungible in our minds. We mentally categorize it into separate "accounts" and spend differently based on the label, not the actual value.

## NARRATIVE STRUCTURE (11 FRAMES)

### Frame 1: Hook (0:00-0:18, 18s)
**Visual:** Two scenarios side by side - Scenario A: Lost $50 theater ticket, Scenario B: Lost $50 cash
**Text:** "You lose a $50 theater ticket. Do you buy another? | You lose $50 cash. Do you still buy the ticket?"
**Narration:** "Scenario A: You bought a fifty-dollar theater ticket. You arrive and realize you lost it. Do you buy another ticket? Most people say no. Scenario B: You planned to buy a fifty-dollar ticket. You arrive and realize you lost fifty dollars cash. Do you still buy the ticket? Most people say yes. Same net loss. Different decision. Why?"

### Frame 2: The Answer (0:18-0:38, 20s)
**Visual:** Two mental "accounts" - "Entertainment Budget" and "General Cash"
**Text:** "Account 1: Theater ticket (-$50) | Account 2: General cash (-$50)"
**Narration:** "Because you mentally separate money into accounts. The lost ticket came from your 'entertainment budget'—buying another feels like spending one hundred dollars on theater. The lost cash? That's from your 'general wallet'—buying the ticket still feels like fifty dollars. Same money, different mental label. This is mental accounting."

### Frame 3: The Researcher (0:38-0:55, 17s)
**Visual:** "Richard Thaler (1985)" text with Nobel Prize icon (2017)
**Text:** "Richard Thaler (1985) - Nobel Prize 2017"
**Narration:** "University of Chicago economist Richard Thaler discovered this in the nineteen-eighties. He showed that we don't treat money as fungible—interchangeable. We create mental budgets and track spending within them. It's irrational in economic terms, but it's how humans actually behave."

### Frame 4: Why It Happens (0:55-1:15, 20s)
**Visual:** Brain with multiple labeled boxes: "Rent," "Food," "Fun," "Savings"
**Text:** "Mental budgets help us control spending"
**Narration:** "Why do we do this? Mental accounting helps us control spending. If all money was one big pool, you might blow your rent on vacation. By mentally earmarking 'rent money' versus 'fun money,' you protect important expenses. It's a cognitive shortcut that usually works—but it creates irrational patterns."

### Frame 5: Real-World Example 1 - Windfalls (1:15-1:35, 20s)
**Visual:** Tax refund check labeled "Windfall" vs paycheck labeled "Earned Income"
**Text:** "Tax refund → 'Free money' → Spend | Paycheck → 'Earned' → Save"
**Narration:** "You get a thousand-dollar tax refund. It feels like 'free money'—you splurge on a vacation. But a thousand-dollar bonus on your paycheck? You save it. It's the same money, but the refund feels like a windfall from a different account. So you spend it differently."

### Frame 6: Real-World Example 2 - Credit Cards (1:35-1:55, 20s)
**Visual:** Credit card vs cash, price tags showing same item
**Text:** "Cash → 'Real money' → Painful | Credit → 'Future account' → Easier"
**Narration:** "You hesitate to spend fifty dollars cash on dinner. But swipe a credit card for the same meal? No problem. Cash feels like it's leaving your 'today account.' Credit? That's your 'future account'—the pain is deferred. Same dinner, same cost, different mental accounting."

### Frame 7: The Dangerous Side (1:55-2:13, 18s)
**Visual:** Debt accumulating in "credit card account" while "savings account" stays small
**Text:** "Savings account: $500 | Credit card debt: $5,000 at 18% interest"
**Narration:** "Mental accounting can be dangerous. You keep five hundred dollars in a savings account earning one percent interest while carrying five thousand in credit card debt at eighteen percent. Economically irrational—pay off the debt! But mentally, the savings account is 'untouchable' and the debt is a 'different problem.'"

### Frame 8: The Opportunity (2:13-2:28, 15s)
**Visual:** Unified pool of money with strategic allocation arrows
**Text:** "All money is fungible → Allocate rationally"
**Narration:** "The opportunity: recognize that all money is fungible. A dollar is a dollar, whether it's salary, bonus, refund, or credit. Once you see the accounts as artificial, you can make smarter choices—like paying off high-interest debt before padding savings."

### Frame 9: Three Practical Takeaways (2:28-2:43, 15s)
**Visual:** Three boxes with icons
**Text:** "1. NOTICE: Am I treating money differently by label?  2. ASK: Would I make this choice if it was all one account?  3. REMEMBER: A dollar = a dollar"
**Narration:** "Three moves: First, notice when you're treating the same dollar differently based on its label. Second, ask: 'Would I make this decision if all my money was one account?' Third, remember—money is fungible. The mental categories are tools, not rules."

### Frame 10: The Thaler Lesson (2:43-2:45, 2s)
**Visual:** Return to theater ticket scenario with both accounts merged
**Text:** "See through the accounts."
**Narration:** "Thaler showed us: we don't just spend money. We spend mental categories. See through them."
