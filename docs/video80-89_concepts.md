## VIDEO 80: "The Pratfall Effect" (Day 485)

**Concept:** Why competent people become more likeable after making mistakes

**Research Foundation:**
- Aronson, Willerman, & Floyd (1966): "The Effect of a Pratfall on Increasing Interpersonal Attractiveness"
- Participants listen to recordings of quiz contestant - high competence or average competence, with or without coffee spill (pratfall)
- High competence + pratfall = most likeable
- Average competence + pratfall = less likeable
- Competent people made more human/approachable by small mistakes
- 55+ years replication

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** Mistakes can increase likeability, but only if you're already seen as competent

**Visual Potential:**
- Likeability ratings with/without pratfall
- Competence x Pratfall interaction graph
- Real-world examples (leaders, celebrities)
- Approachability visualization

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. If you're established as competent, don't hide small mistakes - they make you more relatable
2. If you're still building credibility, minimize visible errors - pratfall effect only works from position of demonstrated competence
3. When someone competent makes minor mistake, your increased liking is the pratfall effect - their competence + humanity creates appeal

**Estimated Duration:** 2:46
**Researcher Diversity:** Aronson (new to channel)

## VIDEO 81: "Cognitive Dissonance" (Day 486)

**Concept:** The discomfort of holding contradictory beliefs, and how we resolve it

**Research Foundation:**
- Festinger & Carlsmith (1959): Classic $1 vs $20 study - insufficient justification creates attitude change
- Participants do boring task, then paid either $1 or $20 to tell next participant it was interesting
- $1 group later rated task as actually interesting (changed attitude to match behavior)
- $20 group rated it boring (had external justification)
- Dissonance reduction drives attitude change
- 65+ years research, foundational social psychology

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** Less payment leads to more attitude change

**Visual Potential:**
- Dissonance visualization (conflicting cognitions)
- $1 vs $20 comparison
- Attitude change mechanisms
- Real-world applications (decision-making, justification)

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. After making difficult decision, notice when you start emphasizing positives and downplaying negatives - that's dissonance reduction
2. When you act inconsistent with beliefs, pay attention to whether you're changing beliefs to match actions (easier than admitting inconsistency)
3. Use for positive change: make small public commitments to desired behaviors - acting creates dissonance that leads to genuine attitude shift

**Estimated Duration:** 2:52
**Researcher Diversity:** Festinger (new to channel, highly influential)

## VIDEO 82: "The Curse of Knowledge" (Day 487)

**Concept:** Once you know something, you can't imagine not knowing it

**Research Foundation:**
- Camerer, Loewenstein, & Weber (1989): "The Curse of Knowledge in Economic Settings"
- Newton (1990): Tapper study - people tap rhythm, overestimate others' ability to recognize song
- Tappers predicted 50% recognition, actual: 2.5%
- Knowledge makes it nearly impossible to adopt naive perspective
- 35+ years research, affects teaching, communication, design
- Keynes earlier reference: "The difficulty lies not in new ideas but in escaping old ones"

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** Knowing something makes you worse at explaining it

**Visual Potential:**
- Tapper study demonstration
- Knowledge gap visualization
- Expert-novice communication failures
- Assumed knowledge examples

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. When explaining something you know well, assume others know less than you think - curse of knowledge makes obvious concepts feel more obvious than they are
2. Test explanations on naive audience before assuming clarity - you can't accurately judge your own explanation clarity
3. When someone explains poorly, they may have curse of knowledge (not lack of caring) - ask basic questions to surface assumed knowledge

**Estimated Duration:** 2:47
**Researcher Diversity:** Camerer, Loewenstein, Newton (new to channel)

## VIDEO 83: "Peak-End Rule" (Day 488)

**Concept:** We judge experiences by their peak moment and ending, not by the average

**Research Foundation:**
- Kahneman et al. (1993): Colonoscopy study - longer procedure with less painful ending remembered as less unpleasant
- Two groups: one ends at peak pain, one continues with lower pain before ending
- Longer procedure with milder ending rated better overall
- Memory judged by peaks and endings, duration neglected
- 30+ years research, affects customer experience, medical procedures, event design

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** Adding mildly unpleasant time at the end makes the whole experience seem better

**Visual Potential:**
- Pain over time graphs
- Peak and end moments highlighted
- Retrospective vs real-time evaluation
- Applications in experience design

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. Design experiences to end strong - people remember endings disproportionately (meetings, presentations, visits)
2. Create peak moments deliberately - one standout moment shapes entire memory more than average quality
3. When recalling experiences, remember your memory is biased by peak-end rule - average moment-to-moment experience may differ from retrospective judgment

**Estimated Duration:** 2:48
**Researcher Diversity:** Already used Kahneman, but different study/different principle - acceptable given foundational importance

## VIDEO 84: "The Benjamin Franklin Effect" (Day 489)

**Concept:** Doing someone a favor makes you like them more (not the reverse)

**Research Foundation:**
- Based on Franklin's autobiography - borrowed book from enemy, enemy became friend
- Jecker & Landy (1969): Experimental validation - participants who did favor for experimenter liked experimenter more
- Cognitive dissonance mechanism: "I did them a favor, therefore I must like them"
- Counterintuitive direction of causality
- 50+ years since experimental confirmation

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** Asking for favors makes people like you (not receiving favors)

**Visual Potential:**
- Franklin anecdote illustration
- Causal direction reversal diagram
- Dissonance reduction mechanism
- Relationship building applications

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. Want someone to like you? Ask them for small favor (not offer favor to them) - creates commitment and triggers self-perception shift
2. When someone asks you for favor, know that agreeing will likely increase your liking of them (not just their liking of you)
3. After doing favor for someone, notice if your attitude toward them improves - that's Benjamin Franklin effect, not them earning your good will

**Estimated Duration:** 2:44
**Researcher Diversity:** Franklin (historical), Jecker & Landy (new to channel)

## VIDEO 85: "The Paradox of Choice" (Day 490)

**Concept:** More options can lead to worse decisions and lower satisfaction

**Research Foundation:**
- Iyengar & Lepper (2000): Jam study - 24 jams vs 6 jams display
- 24 jams: 60% stopped to look, 3% purchased
- 6 jams: 40% stopped to look, 30% purchased
- 10x purchase rate with fewer options
- Schwartz (2004): "The Paradox of Choice" - excessive choice creates paralysis and regret
- 20+ years robust findings

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** More freedom of choice can make you less happy

**Visual Potential:**
- Jam study results visualization
- Choice overload effects
- Decision paralysis illustration
- Satisfaction vs number of options curve

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. When facing too many options, deliberately narrow field before detailed evaluation - paradox of choice creates paralysis when considering all options simultaneously
2. After choosing from many options, you'll be more prone to regret ("what if the option I didn't choose was better?") - knowing this helps accept your choice
3. When designing for others (products, menus, services), curate options rather than maximizing them - fewer good options beats many mediocre ones

**Estimated Duration:** 2:49
**Researcher Diversity:** Iyengar, Schwartz (both new to channel)

## VIDEO 86: "The Hostile Media Effect" (Day 491)

**Concept:** People with strong views see neutral media coverage as biased against their position

**Research Foundation:**
- Vallone, Ross, & Lepper (1985): Pro-Israeli and pro-Palestinian participants watch same news coverage of Beirut massacre
- Both sides rated identical coverage as biased against their position
- Same content seen as hostile by opposing groups
- Mechanism: selective perception, different standard for what "balanced" means
- 40+ years replication, especially relevant in polarized environments

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** Neutral coverage appears biased to everyone

**Visual Potential:**
- Same content, opposite interpretations
- Perception bias visualization
- Selective attention to opposing arguments
- Modern media examples

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. If you think media is biased against your position, consider hostile media effect - you may be selectively noticing opposing arguments while dismissing supportive ones
2. When people with opposite views both claim media is biased against them, that's often evidence the coverage is actually balanced (not that it's biased against everyone)
3. Consume media with awareness that you'll naturally perceive more scrutiny of your own position - doesn't mean bias exists, means you're sensitive to challenges

**Estimated Duration:** 2:51
**Researcher Diversity:** Vallone, Ross, Lepper (new to channel)

## VIDEO 87: "Illusory Truth Effect" (Day 492)

**Concept:** Repeated statements seem more true, regardless of actual truth value

**Research Foundation:**
- Hasher, Goldstein, & Toppino (1977): "Frequency and the Conference of Referential Validity"
- Statements repeated multiple times rated as more true than novel statements
- Effect occurs even when people know statements are false
- Mere exposure → processing fluency → truth feeling
- 45+ years research, critical for misinformation understanding

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** Familiarity feels like truth

**Visual Potential:**
- Truth ratings: first vs repeated exposure
- Processing fluency mechanism
- Propaganda/advertising applications
- Misinformation spread visualization

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. When something "feels true," ask if you've just heard it many times - repetition creates familiarity which feels like validity
2. Be especially skeptical of repeated claims in advertising, political messaging - illusory truth effect is deliberately leveraged to create belief through repetition
3. Don't repeat misinformation even to debunk it - repetition strengthens the false claim's fluency, making it feel more true over time

**Estimated Duration:** 2:46
**Researcher Diversity:** Hasher, Goldstein, Toppino (new to channel)

## VIDEO 88: "The Baader-Meinhof Phenomenon (Frequency Illusion)" (Day 493)

**Concept:** After noticing something once, you suddenly see it everywhere

**Research Foundation:**
- Zwicky (2005): Named and described the pattern
- Selective attention + confirmation bias combination
- Once you notice something, your attention system prioritizes detecting it again
- Feel like frequency increased, but it's your attention that changed
- Not single-study research but well-documented cognitive pattern
- Related to attentional bias research (20+ years)

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** The world didn't change; your attention did

**Visual Potential:**
- Attention spotlight changing
- Before/after noticing visualization
- Selective attention demonstration
- Real-world examples (new word, car model, name)

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. When something suddenly seems everywhere, consider Baader-Meinhof - frequency probably didn't change, your attention did
2. Use this deliberately: want to notice opportunities in domain X? Prime your attention by studying one example - your brain will start detecting similar patterns
3. Be aware in decision-making: if you just learned about risk X, you'll suddenly notice instances of it everywhere (doesn't mean risk increased, means attention shifted)

**Estimated Duration:** 2:44
**Researcher Diversity:** Zwicky (pattern documentation), attentional bias research

## VIDEO 89: "The Spotlight Effect" (Day 494)

**Concept:** We overestimate how much others notice our appearance and behavior

**Research Foundation:**
- Gilovich, Medvec, & Savitsky (2000): "The Spotlight Effect in Social Judgment"
- Participants wear embarrassing t-shirt (Barry Manilow), estimate how many others noticed
- Estimated: 50% would notice. Actual: 25% noticed.
- We think we're in spotlight; others barely paying attention
- 25+ years replication, robust effect

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** Others notice you much less than you think they do

**Visual Potential:**
- Spotlight metaphor
- Estimated vs actual noticing rates
- Embarrassing t-shirt study visualization
- Social anxiety connection

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. When you feel self-conscious (bad hair day, awkward comment, visible mistake), remember spotlight effect - you think everyone noticed, probably <50% of your estimate did
2. This liberates you to take social risks - embarrassment you fear will be much less noticed than you imagine, others focused on themselves
3. Flip side: others' mistakes seem more obvious to them than to you - when someone seems embarrassed about something small, they're probably experiencing spotlight effect

**Estimated Duration:** 2:43
**Researcher Diversity:** Gilovich, Medvec, Savitsky (new to channel)
