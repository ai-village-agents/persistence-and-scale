## Research Authority
- Jack Brehm (1966): "A Theory of Psychological Reactance." Academic Press.
- 55+ years of research validation across multiple domains
- Explains resistance to persuasion, censorship backfire, parenting challenges, mandate opposition
- Effect documented in consumer behavior, health campaigns, politics, education

## Core Concept
**Psychological Reactance:** When people perceive their freedom to choose is threatened or eliminated, they are motivated to restore that freedom—often by doing the opposite of what's demanded. The more forceful the restriction, the stronger the reactance. Explains why "don't touch" signs attract touching, why censorship increases interest, and why hard-sell tactics backfire.

Marketing: aggressive hard-sell tactics—'Buy now or lose out forever!'—trigger reactance, making people less likely to buy."

## Section 3: How to Reduce Reactance (1:40-2:20)
**Visual 6 (Frame 6):** Strategies to minimize reactance
- Title: "REDUCE REACTANCE"
- Four strategies with examples:
  1. **Preserve choice:** "Here are two options—you decide" vs. "Do this"
  2. **Explain reasons:** "Here's why X matters" vs. "Just do X"
  3. **Acknowledge freedom:** "It's your choice, and here's what to consider"
  4. **Reduce forcefulness:** Suggest, don't demand

**Narration:**
"If you want to influence behavior without triggering reactance, preserve choice. Instead of 'Do this,' say 'Here are two good options—you decide.' Explain reasons instead of issuing commands—people are less reactive when they understand why. Explicitly acknowledge their freedom: 'It's your choice, and here's what you might want to consider.' And reduce forcefulness. The harder you push, the harder they push back."

## Section 4: Practical Framework (2:20-2:40)
**Visual 7 (Frame 7):** Three-step action framework
- Title: "APPLY REACTANCE THEORY"
- Three numbered boxes:
  1. **NOTICE** when you're resisting advice—ask "Am I reacting to content or loss of choice?"
  2. **ASK** "Am I creating reactance in others?" (Check for forcefulness)
  3. **REFRAME** demands as options (Preserve autonomy)

**Narration:**
"Here's your framework. First, notice when you're resisting advice or rules. Ask yourself: am I reacting to the content, or to the feeling that my freedom is threatened? Second, ask if you're creating reactance in others. Are you demanding, or are you offering options? Third, reframe demands as choices. Don't say 'You need to exercise.' Say 'Some people find exercise helpful for energy. What do you think?' You preserve autonomy, and they're less likely to resist."
