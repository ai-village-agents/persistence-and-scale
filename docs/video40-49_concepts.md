## Candidate Topics (Ranked by Priority)

### HIGH PRIORITY (Strong Fit)

#### 1. Zeigarnik Effect
**Researcher:** Bluma Zeigarnik (1927)
**Core Finding:** Unfinished tasks remembered ~2x better than completed tasks
**Hook:** "Why you remember the task you didn't finish better than the five you completed"
**Replication:** 90+ years, replicated across cultures (Greist-Bousquet & Schiffman 1992 review)
**Duration Estimate:** 2:45
**Actionable Takeaway:** Use intentional incompletion for memory, strategic closure for mental clarity
**Visual Potential:** HIGH - task lists, completion states, memory recall comparison

#### 2. Choice Overload (Paradox of Choice)
**Researcher:** Sheena Iyengar & Mark Lepper (2000), synthesized by Barry Schwartz
**Core Finding:** 24 jam varieties = 3% purchase rate, 6 varieties = 30% purchase rate (10x difference)
**Hook:** "Why having 24 options makes you 10 times less likely to choose than having 6"
**Replication:** 25+ years, meta-analysis by Chernev et al. (2015) - effect strongest under specific conditions
**Duration Estimate:** 2:50
**Actionable Takeaway:** Curate options, use satisficing vs maximizing, embrace constraints
**Visual Potential:** HIGH - jam study visualization, decision paralysis, option arrays

#### 3. Effort Justification (Cognitive Dissonance)
**Researcher:** Leon Festinger (1959), Aronson & Mills initiation study (1959)
**Core Finding:** Difficult initiation increases group attraction; effort creates value perception
**Hook:** "Why the harder you worked for something, the more you value it—even if it's worthless"
**Replication:** 65+ years, robust across domains (Alessandri et al. 2008)
**Duration Estimate:** 2:45
**Actionable Takeaway:** Recognize sunk effort bias, use strategic effort in design/onboarding
**Visual Potential:** MEDIUM-HIGH - effort-value relationship, hazing/initiation examples

#### 4. Retrieval Practice (Testing Effect Extended)
**Researcher:** Henry Roediger & Jeffrey Karpicke (2006)
**Core Finding:** Testing beats studying even when testing takes same time; 50% better retention after 1 week
**Hook:** "Why testing yourself works better than studying, even when both take the same time"
**Replication:** 15+ years intensive research, builds on 100+ years of testing research
**Duration Estimate:** 2:50
**Actionable Takeaway:** Self-testing as primary study method, flashcards over highlighting
**Visual Potential:** HIGH - study vs. test comparison, retention curves over time
**Note:** Related to Testing Effect (Video 16) but focuses on retrieval practice mechanism

#### 5. False Consensus Effect
**Researcher:** Lee Ross, David Greene, Pamela House (1977)
**Core Finding:** People overestimate agreement with their views by 20-40%
**Hook:** "You think 60% of people agree with you. Actually, it's 35%."
**Replication:** 45+ years, robust across cultures and topics (Mullen et al. 1985 meta-analysis)
**Duration Estimate:** 2:45
**Actionable Takeaway:** Seek disconfirming opinions, test assumptions, pluralistic ignorance awareness
**Visual Potential:** MEDIUM-HIGH - agreement estimation vs. reality, echo chamber visualization

#### 6. Hindsight Bias ("I Knew It All Along")
**Researcher:** Baruch Fischhoff (1975)
**Core Finding:** After learning outcome, people believe they "would have predicted it" (50%+ overestimation)
**Hook:** "After it happens, your brain rewrites history to make it seem obvious"
**Replication:** 50+ years, robust across domains (Roese & Vohs 2012 review)
**Duration Estimate:** 2:45
**Actionable Takeaway:** Record predictions before outcomes, keep decision journals
**Visual Potential:** MEDIUM-HIGH - before/after knowledge state, prediction records

#### 7. Elaborative Interrogation
**Researcher:** Michael Pressley et al. (1987), Dunlosky et al. (2013) meta-analysis
**Core Finding:** Asking "why" during learning improves retention by 20-50% vs. passive reading
**Hook:** "Adding one word to your study routine doubles retention: 'Why?'"
**Replication:** 35+ years, effectiveness varies by prior knowledge (Dunlosky meta-analysis rated moderate-high)
**Duration Estimate:** 2:40
**Actionable Takeaway:** Generate "why" explanations during learning, connect new to known
**Visual Potential:** MEDIUM - question generation, connection mapping, retention comparison

#### 8. Habit Loop (Cue-Routine-Reward)
**Researcher:** Synthesized by Charles Duhigg from Wendy Wood, BJ Fogg, Ann Graybiel neuroscience
**Core Finding:** Habits follow 3-stage loop: Cue → Routine → Reward; changing routine while keeping cue/reward works
**Hook:** "Why you can't 'break' a habit—you can only replace it"
**Replication:** 20+ years neuroscience backing (Graybiel basal ganglia research), 30+ years behavior research
**Duration Estimate:** 2:55
**Actionable Takeaway:** Identify cue and reward, substitute new routine, maintain loop structure
**Visual Potential:** HIGH - loop diagram, habit substitution examples
**Note:** Synthesized research, not single-study origin

#### 9. Representativeness Heuristic
**Researcher:** Amos Tversky & Daniel Kahneman (1974)
**Core Finding:** Judge probability by similarity to stereotype, ignoring base rates (Linda the bank teller problem)
**Hook:** "Why 'Linda is a feminist bank teller' seems more likely than 'Linda is a bank teller'—even though it's mathematically impossible"
**Replication:** 50+ years, robust but debated (conjunction fallacy)
**Duration Estimate:** 2:50
**Actionable Takeaway:** Check base rates first, resist stereotype-based probability judgments
**Visual Potential:** HIGH - Linda problem visualization, probability diagrams
**Note:** This is Kahneman - use strategically (would be 3rd K&T video in 40 total = 7.5%)

#### 10. Base Rate Neglect
**Researcher:** Kahneman & Tversky (1973), also Maya Bar-Hillel (1980)
**Core Finding:** People ignore base rate information in favor of specific case details (cab problem: 85% green cabs, witness 80% reliable, still judge blue cab more likely)
**Hook:** "Why you ignore the 85% fact and trust the 80% witness—and get the wrong answer"
**Replication:** 50+ years, robust across domains
**Duration Estimate:** 2:50
**Actionable Takeaway:** Always start with base rates, then adjust for case-specific info
**Visual Potential:** HIGH - cab problem, medical diagnosis examples
**Note:** Kahneman again - would be 4th K&T if both this and #9 used (10% total)

## MEDIUM PRIORITY (Good Fit, Secondary Options)

#### 11. Disfluency Effect (Desirable Difficulty in Reading)
**Researcher:** Connor Diemand-Yauman, Daniel Oppenheimer, Erikka Vaughan (2011)
**Core Finding:** Difficult-to-read fonts improve retention by 10-15% (desirable difficulty)
**Hook:** "Why reading in Comic Sans might actually help you remember"
**Replication:** 15+ years, some mixed results (Xie et al. 2018 found boundary conditions)
**Duration Estimate:** 2:40
**Actionable Takeaway:** Strategic disfluency for important info, balance difficulty with engagement
**Visual Potential:** MEDIUM - font comparison, retention results
**Risk:** Newer research (2011), replication mixed

#### 12. Goal Gradient Effect
**Researcher:** Clark Hull (1932), modern application by Ran Kivetz et al. (2006)
**Core Finding:** Effort increases as goal approaches (coffee card study: 10-stamp card with 2 free stamps = faster completion than 8-stamp card from zero)
**Hook:** "Why you work harder on step 8 of 10 than step 2 of 10, even though the effort is the same"
**Replication:** 90+ years (Hull), 20+ years modern application
**Duration Estimate:** 2:45
**Actionable Takeaway:** Artificial progress to kickstart motivation, visible progress indicators
**Visual Potential:** MEDIUM-HIGH - progress bars, coffee card study, effort curves

#### 13. Mere Ownership Effect (Endowment Effect Extension)
**Researcher:** Related to Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler (1990), but broader ownership literature
**Core Finding:** Ownership increases valuation even without attachment (mug study: owners demand 2-3x what buyers offer)
**Hook:** "The moment you own something, its value doubles in your mind"
**Replication:** 35+ years, robust
**Duration Estimate:** 2:40
**Actionable Takeaway:** Recognize in negotiations, use free trials strategically, detach for objective value
**Visual Potential:** MEDIUM - mug study, valuation gaps
**Note:** Covered Endowment Effect (Video 17), this extends ownership psychology

#### 14. Serial Position Effect (Combined Primacy + Recency)
**Researcher:** Bennet Murdock Jr. (1962), but also covers earlier work
**Core Finding:** First + last positions remembered best (70% vs 30% for middle)
**Duration Estimate:** 2:50
**Visual Potential:** HIGH
**Note:** Already covered in Video 36 - DUPLICATE, remove from consideration

#### 15. Self-Serving Bias
**Researcher:** Multiple origins, Miller & Ross (1975) review
**Core Finding:** Attribute success to internal factors (skill), failure to external factors (luck) 
**Hook:** "Why your success is skill and your failure is bad luck"
**Replication:** 50+ years, robust across cultures
**Duration Estimate:** 2:45
**Actionable Takeaway:** Own failures for growth, recognize bias in evaluations
**Visual Potential:** MEDIUM - attribution diagrams, success/failure interpretations

## LOWER PRIORITY (Interesting but Issues)

#### 16. Cocktail Party Effect
**Researcher:** Colin Cherry (1953)
**Finding:** Selective attention allows focusing on one conversation in noisy environment
**Issue:** Primarily perceptual psychology, less behavioral/cognitive bias focus
**Duration:** 2:40
**Priority:** LOWER - less aligned with cognitive psychology focus

#### 17. Attentional Blink
**Researcher:** Raymond, Shapiro & Arnell (1992)
**Finding:** Can't detect 2nd target within 200-500ms of first target in rapid serial visual presentation
**Issue:** Perceptual/attention, less everyday relevance
**Duration:** 2:35
**Priority:** LOWER - technical, less immediately actionable

#### 18. Affect Heuristic
**Researcher:** Paul Slovic et al. (2002)
**Finding:** Emotional impressions guide judgments of risk/benefit
**Duration:** 2:50
**Priority:** MEDIUM - good but covered partially in availability heuristic (Video 19)
