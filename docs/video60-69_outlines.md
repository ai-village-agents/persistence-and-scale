# Video 60: Status Quo Bias
**Target Day:** Day 465  
**Concept Focus:** Status Quo Bias (Samuelson & Zeckhauser 1988 investment decisions)  
**Duration:** 2:45

**Research Foundation:** Samuelson, W., & Zeckhauser, R. (1988). Status quo bias in decision making. *Journal of Risk and Uncertainty, 1*(1), 7-59.

## Research Authority
- William Samuelson and Richard Zeckhauser (1988) demonstrated default effects across investment, health, and policy scenarios.
- Three-plus decades of replication in pensions, organ donation, and energy markets (Madrian & Shea 2001; Johnson & Goldstein 2003; Pichert & Katsikopoulos 2008).
- Citations 8,500+; foundational reading in behavioral economics and choice architecture.
- Field experiments across North America and Europe confirm persistence in real financial behavior (e.g., automatic enrollment raising 401(k) participation from 49% to 86%, Madrian & Shea 2001).

## Core Concept
**Status Quo Bias:** People disproportionately stick with defaults—even when superior alternatives are available—because change feels risky, effortful, and regret-prone. In Samuelson and Zeckhauser's investment simulations, assigning just one option as the "status quo" caused 60% of participants to keep it, versus 34% when no default was highlighted.

**Narration:**  
"Your company updates the 401(k) plan. The memo says, 'Do nothing and we’ll keep your old allocation.' Eight months later, most employees never touched it—even though better funds were available. That’s Status Quo Bias, and Samuelson and Zeckhauser showed it warps serious decisions."

**Visual 2 (Frame 2):** Split-screen infographic. Left: "Scenario A (No Default)" list of four investment mixes (Aggressive, Balanced, Conservative, Money Market). Right: "Scenario B (Default Highlighted)" with the Balanced mix boxed in electric blue. Footer: "Participants: Harvard decision-making study, 128 MBA students."

**Narration:**  
"In 1988, Samuelson and Zeckhauser asked Harvard students to allocate $1,000 across four funds. In one scenario, no option was preselected. In another, the balanced fund was labeled ‘status quo.’ Same choices, same payoff charts. The only difference was a blue box around one option."

**Visual 3 (Frame 3):** Bar chart on dark navy background. Bars labeled "No Default: 34% choose Balanced" vs. "With Default: 60% choose Balanced". Additional bars showing alternative funds losing share. Callout text: "Default nearly doubles selection rate."

"When nothing was highlighted, 34% chose the balanced mix. When that mix was framed as ‘your current plan,’ 60% locked it in. Premium information and logic lost to inertia. Samuelson and Zeckhauser repeated this across insurance, health, even a science policy scenario. The bias held."

## Section 2: Why Defaults Dominate (1:10-1:52)
**Visual 4 (Frame 4):** Mechanism diagram. Central box: "Status Quo Bias". Three arrows pointing from surrounding bubbles: "Anticipated regret (What if change backfires?)", "Loss aversion (Defaults feel safer)", "Effort cost (Paperwork + mental load)". Footer note: "Replicated in field studies 1990s-2020s."

**Narration:**  
"Why are defaults so sticky? First, anticipated regret—changing feels risky because if the new choice disappoints, you blame yourself. Second, loss aversion—the current state feels like the safe baseline, so switching threatens potential losses. Third, effort costs—even tiny paperwork or research demands delay action. Those micro-frictions let the status quo win."

**Narration:**  
"The bias isn’t just a lab oddity. Field studies show automatic 401(k) enrollment boosts participation from 49% to 86%. Countries adopting presumed-consent organ donation see rates jump to 90%. Utility customers stick with green energy tariffs when that’s the default. Thirty-five years of data say the same thing: configure the default, control the outcome."

Make good behaviors the default, not the exception."

# Video 61: Mere Measurement Effect
**Target Day:** Day 466  
**Concept Focus:** Mere Measurement Effect (Morwitz & Fitzsimons 1996 intention-behavior shift)  
**Duration:** 2:44

**Research Foundation:** Morwitz, V. G., & Fitzsimons, G. J. (1996). The mere-measurement effect: Why does measuring intentions change behavior? *Journal of Consumer Psychology, 4*(1), 1-14.

## Research Authority
- Vicki Morwitz and Gavan Fitzsimons (1996) documented that asking about intentions increases the likelihood of the measured behavior.
- Replications across retail, voting, and health behaviors for 25+ years (Spangenberg 1997; Dholakia 2010; Fitzsimons & Morwitz 2020).
- Meta-analyses (Wood et al. 2016) confirm small-to-moderate effect sizes when questions activate self-prediction.
- Widely used by market researchers, civic campaigns, and wellness programs to nudge action ethically.

## Core Concept
**Mere Measurement Effect:** Simply asking people about their plans nudges them to follow through. Morwitz and Fitzsimons found that surveying consumers about a catalog purchase boosted actual purchases by nearly 35%, because answering self-predictive questions activates commitment and accessibility.

## Hook / Opening (0:00-0:18)
**Visual 1 (Frame 1):** Dark navy 1920x1080 background with headline "A QUESTION THAT CHANGES BEHAVIOR" in white. Right side: animated-style clipboard with survey question "Do you plan to buy from the Spring catalog?" highlighted in electric blue; progress bar showing "Survey Complete".

**Narration:**  
"One catalog company ran a simple survey: 'Do you plan to buy from us this season?' Customers who answered were 35% more likely to place an order. The survey didn’t offer discounts. It just asked. That’s the Mere Measurement Effect."

## Section 1: Original Study Design (0:18-0:58)
**Visual 2 (Frame 2):** Diagram showing experimental groups. Left box: "Control – No Survey (n=2,000 customers)". Right box: "Surveyed – Asked about purchase intent (n=2,000 customers)". Arrows point to outcome boxes showing actual purchase rates. All elements on dark navy with white outlines, electric blue highlights.

**Narration:**  
"Morwitz and Fitzsimons split 4,000 catalog customers. Half received no contact. Half got a one-minute phone call asking, 'Do you plan to buy in the next six months? How likely on a 1-to-5 scale?' No coupons, no follow-up."

**Visual 3 (Frame 3):** Bar chart comparing purchase rates: Control 27%, Surveyed 36%. Sub-caption: "+9 percentage points from a single question." Secondary labels cite replication: "Spangenberg 1997: +8 pts for health club attendance."

**Narration:**  
"Six months later, 36% of surveyed customers bought—up from 27% in the control group. Asking about intent sparked action. Spangenberg replicated it with health club memberships. Asking, 'How likely are you to exercise more?' boosted actual workouts the next month."

## Section 2: Why Intent Questions Work (0:58-1:40)
**Visual 4 (Frame 4):** Mechanism infographic with three nodes: "Self-Prediction (I said I would)", "Increased Accessibility (Goal top-of-mind)", "Cognitive Dissonance (Need to align behavior)". Arrows converge on central node "Action Probability ↑".

**Narration:**  
"Why does a survey change what we do? First, self-prediction. Saying 'Yes, I plan to buy' becomes a micro-commitment you want to honor. Second, accessibility. The goal stays top-of-mind—you start noticing cues that help you act. Third, cognitive dissonance. You want your behavior to match how you answered. So you hit the checkout page."

**Visual 5 (Frame 5):** Timeline panel with three vertical slices: "Day 0: Respond to intent survey", "Week 1: Encounter trigger (catalog arrives)", "Week 4: Action taken". Each slice includes iconography (phone, mailbox, laptop) with brief label text only.

**Narration:**  
"The effect unfolds over time. Day zero: you respond to a survey. Week one: you see the catalog again and recall your answer. Week four: you’ve already decided you’re 'the kind of person who buys,' so you complete the order. Measuring intent quietly closes the loop."

## Research Authority
- Freedman and Fraser’s 1966 field experiments with California homeowners pioneered the technique.
- Replications across charitable giving, public health, and digital marketing for 50+ years (Pliner et al. 1974; Burger 1999; Guéguen 2002).
- Mechanism grounded in consistency theory (Cialdini 1984) and self-perception theory (Bem 1972).
- Quantified lift: compliance with large request jumps from ~17% to ~76% after small agreement.

## Core Concept
**Foot-in-the-Door:** Securing agreement to a small, easy request dramatically increases the odds of compliance with a larger related request. Freedman and Fraser showed that homeowners who first signed a safe-driving petition were four times more likely to later install an enormous roadside billboard promoting safe driving.
