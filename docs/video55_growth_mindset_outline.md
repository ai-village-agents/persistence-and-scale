## Research Authority
- Carol Dweck (2006): "Mindset: The New Psychology of Success." Random House.
- Based on 20+ years of research at Stanford University
- Foundational work in educational and developmental psychology
- 5,000+ citations, widely applied in education, parenting, organizational development
- Distinguishes fixed mindset (ability as static) from growth mindset (ability as malleable)

## Core Concept
**Growth Mindset:** The belief that intelligence, abilities, and talents can be developed through effort, learning, and persistence. Contrasts with fixed mindset (belief that abilities are static traits). Growth mindset predicts greater resilience, higher achievement, and more effective response to failure. Surprisingly, praising "intelligence" creates fixed mindset, while praising "effort" creates growth mindset.

## Section 2: The Praise Study (0:55-1:35)
**Visual 4 (Frame 4):** The landmark praise experiment
- Title: "HOW PRAISE SHAPES MINDSET"
- Experiment with 5th graders, three phases:
  * Phase 1: "All students solve easy puzzles (everyone succeeds)"
  * Phase 2: "Praise delivered"
    - Group A: "You must be smart at this" (intelligence praise)
    - Group B: "You must have worked hard" (effort praise)
  * Phase 3: "Offered choice: challenging puzzles or easy puzzles"

**Visual 5 (Frame 5):** Results of praise study
- Two outcome comparisons:
  * **Intelligence praise (Group A):**
    - 67% chose easy puzzles (avoid challenge)
    - Performance decreased on later tests
    - Reported less enjoyment
  * **Effort praise (Group B, electric blue):**
    - 90% chose challenging puzzles
    - Performance improved on later tests
    - Reported more enjoyment

**Narration:**
"Dweck's team gave fifth graders easy puzzles. Everyone succeeded. Then came the praise. Group A heard 'You must be smart at this.' Group B heard 'You must have worked hard.' Just one sentence. Next, they were offered a choice: challenging puzzles or easy puzzles. 67% of the intelligence-praised kids chose easy puzzles—they wanted to keep looking smart. 90% of the effort-praised kids chose challenging puzzles—they wanted to keep learning. Later tests showed the intelligence-praised group's performance dropped, while the effort-praised group improved. One sentence about intelligence created a fixed mindset. One sentence about effort created a growth mindset."

## Section 3: Why Mindset Matters (1:35-2:10)
**Visual 6 (Frame 6):** Neuroplasticity foundation
- Title: "THE SCIENCE: YOUR BRAIN CAN CHANGE"
- Brain diagram with highlighted connections
- Text points:
  * "Learning creates new neural pathways"
  * "Practice strengthens connections"
  * "'I can't do this yet' vs. 'I can't do this'"
- Bottom: "Neuroplasticity supports growth mindset"

**Narration:**
"Growth mindset isn't just motivational talk—it's grounded in neuroscience. Your brain has neuroplasticity: learning creates new neural pathways, and practice strengthens them. When you struggle with something, you're not revealing a permanent limitation—you're in the process of building new connections. The key word is 'yet.' Not 'I can't do this,' but 'I can't do this yet.' That one word encodes a growth mindset."

## Section 4: Practical Framework (2:10-2:40)
**Visual 7 (Frame 7):** Cultivating growth mindset
- Title: "CULTIVATE GROWTH MINDSET"
- Four strategies with icons:
  1. **Praise process, not traits:** "Great effort" not "You're so smart"
  2. **Embrace failure as data:** "What did I learn?" not "I failed"
  3. **Add 'yet' to limitations:** "I can't do this... yet"
  4. **Celebrate others' success:** Learn from it, don't feel threatened

**Narration:**
"Here's how to build growth mindset in yourself and others. First, praise process, not traits. Say 'You worked hard and found a good strategy,' not 'You're so smart.' Second, reframe failure as data. Ask 'What did I learn?' instead of 'I failed.' Third, add 'yet' to every limitation. 'I can't do this yet' keeps the door open. Fourth, when others succeed, don't feel threatened—learn from them. Their success is proof that growth is possible."
