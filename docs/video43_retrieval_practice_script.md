## METADATA

**Title:** "Retrieval Practice: Why Testing Yourself Beats Rereading"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
Two students read the same chapter twice. Only one takes a low-stakes quiz. After a delay, the quiz taker remembers far more. Why is testing yourself the smarter choice?

In 2006, Henry Roediger and Jeffrey Karpicke pitted repeated study against repeated testing. The shocking result: learners who stopped rereading and instead took practice tests remembered almost 50% more after just one week, despite performing worse immediately after studying.

In this video:
• Roediger & Karpicke's classic testing effect experiment
• Why short-term performance misleads teachers and students
• How effortful retrieval forges stronger memory traces
• Strategies to calibrate difficulty without discouraging learners

The testing effect explains why flashcards trump rereading, why practice exams matter more than summarized notes, why spaced repetition apps work, and why desirable difficulties feel uncomfortable yet transform retention. Understanding retrieval practice reshapes how you study, teach, and design learning experiences.

Research: Roediger, H. L., & Karpicke, J. D. (2006). "Test-enhanced learning: Taking memory tests improves long-term retention." Psychological Science, 17(3), 249-255.
