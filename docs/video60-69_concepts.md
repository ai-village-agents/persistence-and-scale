## Video 60-69 Concept Pool

### Video 60: Status Quo Bias (Samuelson & Zeckhauser 1988)
- **Researchers:** William Samuelson & Richard Zeckhauser, Boston University & Harvard
- **Foundation:** Samuelson, W., & Zeckhauser, R. (1988). Status quo bias in decision making. Journal of Risk and Uncertainty, 1(1), 7-59.
- **Key Insight:** People disproportionately prefer the current state of affairs; inaction is the default even when alternatives are objectively better
- **Hook:** Why 60% of people keep the default 401k allocation forever
- **Visual Potential:** Decision trees where "do nothing" branch is disproportionately chosen
- **Duration Target:** 2:45
- **Why It Fits:** Foundational behavioral economics, explains organizational inertia, consumer inaction, policy defaults

### Video 61: Mere Measurement Effect (Morwitz & Fitzsimons 1996)
- **Researchers:** Vicki Morwitz & Gavan Fitzsimons, NYU & Duke
- **Foundation:** Morwitz, V. G., & Fitzsimons, G. J. (1996). The mere-measurement effect: Why does measuring intentions change behavior? Journal of Consumer Psychology.
- **Key Insight:** Simply asking people about their intentions changes their subsequent behavior (asking about voting increases voting)
- **Hook:** Why surveys change the behavior they're measuring
- **Visual Potential:** Before/after comparison of behavior when measured vs. not measured
- **Duration Target:** 2:40
- **Why It Fits:** Practical implications for surveys, goal-setting, self-monitoring

### Video 62: Foot-in-the-Door Technique (Freedman & Fraser 1966)
- **Researchers:** Jonathan Freedman & Scott Fraser, Stanford University
- **Foundation:** Freedman, J. L., & Fraser, S. C. (1966). Compliance without pressure: The foot-in-the-door technique. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 4(2), 195-202.
- **Key Insight:** People who agree to a small request are more likely to agree to a larger related request later (consistency principle)
- **Hook:** Why getting someone to agree to a small favor makes them say yes to a big one
- **Visual Potential:** Escalation diagram showing small→large request sequence
- **Duration Target:** 2:50
- **Why It Fits:** Classic persuasion research, practical sales/negotiation applications, explains commitment escalation

### Video 63: Paradox of Choice (Schwartz 2004, based on Iyengar research)
- **Researchers:** Barry Schwartz (popular synthesis), Sheena Iyengar (empirical foundation)
- **Foundation:** Iyengar, S. S., & Lepper, M. R. (2000). When choice is demotivating: Can one desire too much of a good thing? Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 79(6), 995-1006.
- **Key Insight:** Too many options lead to decision paralysis and decreased satisfaction (jam study: 24 jams → 3% bought, 6 jams → 30% bought)
- **Hook:** Why having 200 channels makes you less satisfied than having 10
- **Visual Potential:** Choice overload visualization, satisfaction curves
- **Duration Target:** 2:50
- **Why It Fits:** Already identified in Video 40 - CHECK IF DUPLICATE. If duplicate, use alternate below.

**Video 63 ALTERNATE: Door-in-the-Face Technique (Cialdini et al. 1975)**
- **Researchers:** Robert Cialdini et al., Arizona State University
- **Foundation:** Cialdini, R. B., Vincent, J. E., Lewis, S. K., Catalan, J., Wheeler, D., & Darby, B. L. (1975). Reciprocal concessions procedure for inducing compliance: The door-in-the-face technique. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 31(2), 206-215.
- **Key Insight:** Start with an unreasonably large request (which gets rejected), then follow with a smaller reasonable request (which gets accepted at higher rates due to reciprocity/contrast)
- **Hook:** Why asking for $100 first makes someone more likely to give you $20
- **Visual Potential:** Contrast effect visualization, negotiation sequences
- **Duration Target:** 2:45
- **Why It Fits:** Complements foot-in-the-door (Video 62), Cialdini's influence research, practical negotiation tactics

### Video 64: Thin Slices (Ambady & Rosenthal 1993)
- **Researchers:** Nalini Ambady & Robert Rosenthal, Harvard University
- **Foundation:** Ambady, N., & Rosenthal, R. (1993). Half a minute: Predicting teacher evaluations from thin slices of nonverbal behavior and physical attractiveness. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 64(3), 431-441.
- **Key Insight:** People can make accurate judgments about others based on very brief observations (30 seconds or less); thin slices predict end-of-semester teacher evaluations
- **Hook:** Why your first 30 seconds in a job interview predict the outcome
- **Visual Potential:** Timeline showing 30-second window, accuracy comparison graphs
- **Duration Target:** 2:40
- **Why It Fits:** Nonverbal communication, first impressions, interview/presentation applications

### Video 65: Reciprocity Norm (Gouldner 1960, Cialdini 1984)
- **Researchers:** Alvin Gouldner (sociological foundation), Robert Cialdini (psychological application)
- **Foundation:** Gouldner, A. W. (1960). The norm of reciprocity: A preliminary statement. American Sociological Review, 25(2), 161-178. | Cialdini, R. B. (1984). Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion.
- **Key Insight:** People feel obligated to return favors; even uninvited favors create debt (free samples increase purchases, Hare Krishna flower technique)
- **Hook:** Why restaurants give you a mint with the check (and tips increase 3%)
- **Visual Potential:** Give→receive cycle, obligation scales, restaurant/sales examples
- **Duration Target:** 2:50
- **Why It Fits:** Universal social norm, cross-cultural evidence, immediate practical applications (sales, negotiation, relationships)

### Video 66: Bystander Effect (Darley & Latané 1968)
- **Researchers:** John Darley & Bibb Latané, NYU
- **Foundation:** Darley, J. M., & Latané, B. (1968). Bystander intervention in emergencies: Diffusion of responsibility. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 8(4), 377-383.
- **Key Insight:** The more bystanders present during an emergency, the less likely any individual is to help (diffusion of responsibility)
- **Hook:** Why you're less likely to get help in a crowd of 20 than alone with 1 person
- **Visual Potential:** Group size vs. helping probability graph, emergency scenarios
- **Duration Target:** 2:45
- **Why It Fits:** Classic social psychology, sparked by Kitty Genovese case, explains urban apathy, actionable (designate specific helpers)

### Video 67: Mirroring & Chameleon Effect (Chartrand & Bargh 1999)
- **Researchers:** Tanya Chartrand & John Bargh, Ohio State & NYU
- **Foundation:** Chartrand, T. L., & Bargh, J. A. (1999). The chameleon effect: The perception–behavior link and social interaction. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 76(6), 893-910.
- **Key Insight:** People unconsciously mimic others' posture, mannerisms, facial expressions, and speech patterns; mirroring increases liking and rapport
- **Hook:** Why you start talking like your friends without noticing
- **Visual Potential:** Side-by-side figures showing posture mimicry, rapport measurement graphs
- **Duration Target:** 2:40
- **Why It Fits:** Nonverbal communication, rapport-building, sales/negotiation applications, explains social bonding

### Video 68: Scarcity Principle (Cialdini 1984, Worchel et al. 1975)
- **Researchers:** Robert Cialdini (synthesis), Stephen Worchel et al. (cookie jar study)
- **Foundation:** Worchel, S., Lee, J., & Adewole, A. (1975). Effects of supply and demand on ratings of object value. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 32(5), 906-914.
- **Key Insight:** Items become more desirable when they're scarce or availability is limited (cookie jar study: cookies rated better when jar was nearly empty)
- **Hook:** Why "only 2 left in stock" makes you buy immediately
- **Visual Potential:** Supply curves, scarcity triggers (limited time, exclusive access), value ratings
- **Duration Target:** 2:50
- **Why It Fits:** Universal persuasion tactic, explains FOMO, marketing applications, auction dynamics

### Video 69: False Memory (Loftus & Palmer 1974, Loftus 1996)
- **Researchers:** Elizabeth Loftus & John Palmer, University of Washington
- **Foundation:** Loftus, E. F., & Palmer, J. C. (1974). Reconstruction of automobile destruction: An example of the interaction between language and memory. Journal of Verbal Learning and Verbal Behavior, 13(5), 585-589.
- **Key Insight:** Leading questions can alter memory ("smashed" vs. "hit" changes speed estimates and creates false memories of broken glass)
- **Hook:** Why witnesses to the same crime remember completely different events
- **Visual Potential:** Word choice impact on memory, false memory creation timeline
- **Duration Target:** 2:45
- **Why It Fits:** Foundational memory research, legal/eyewitness testimony implications, explains malleability of memory
