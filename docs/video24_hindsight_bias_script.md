## METADATA

**Title:** "Why Everything Makes Sense After It Already Happened"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
After an event occurs, it feels obvious. "I knew that would happen." But before it happened, you didn't predict it. You just remember it as if you did.

Psychologist Baruch Fischhoff demonstrated this powerful bias in the 1970s. After people learned the outcome of an event, they consistently overestimated how predictable it had been beforehand. They couldn't accurately remember what they didn't know before learning the answer.

In this video:
• Fischhoff's classic hindsight bias experiments
• Why outcomes feel inevitable after they occur
• The "I knew it all along" effect
• How hindsight bias distorts memory, learning, and evaluation of decisions

Hindsight bias makes us overconfident in our ability to predict the future. It makes us judge past decisions unfairly. It prevents us from learning from surprise. Understanding hindsight bias is essential for accurate evaluation and genuine learning.

Research: Fischhoff, B. (1975). "Hindsight ≠ foresight: The effect of outcome knowledge on judgment under uncertainty." Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Perception and Performance, 1(3), 288-299.

### FRAME 2: The "I Knew It" Feeling (0:08-0:24, 16 seconds)

  - AFTER: Single path highlighted, "Of course they succeeded—I knew they would!"

**Narration:**

### FRAME 3: Fischhoff's Experiments (0:24-0:42, 18 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Experimental setup displayed:
  - Group 1: "Read scenario + predict outcome" → Prediction: 30% say A
  - Group 2: "Read scenario + told outcome was A" → Estimate: 57% say they would have predicted A
- Label: "Knowing the outcome nearly doubles perceived predictability"

**Narration:**
"In the nineteen seventies, Baruch Fischhoff gave people descriptions of historical events. Some had to predict what happened. Others were told what happened and asked to estimate how predictable it was. People who knew the outcome rated it as almost twice as predictable as those who had to actually predict it. Hindsight made the outcome feel obvious—even though it hadn't been."

### FRAME 4: The Memory Distortion (0:42-0:58, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Brain with two competing memories:
  - ACTUAL memory (faded): "Before: I thought 40% chance"
  - RECONSTRUCTED memory (bright): "I remember thinking: 70% chance"
- Arrow showing: "Knowing outcome → distorts memory of prior belief"
- Text: "You can't accurately remember what you didn't know"

**Narration:**
"This isn't just about estimation. It's a memory distortion. Once you know the outcome, you can't accurately remember what you thought before you knew. Your memory gets rewritten. You genuinely believe you predicted it, even when evidence shows you didn't."

### FRAME 6: The Evaluation Problem (1:16-1:32, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Decision evaluation framework:
  - WRONG approach: "Evaluate decision by outcome"
  - RIGHT approach: "Evaluate decision by information available at the time"
- Examples: Good decision + bad outcome vs Bad decision + good outcome
- Text: "Resulting = judging decisions by outcomes alone"

**Narration:**
"Hindsight bias makes us judge decisions unfairly. We evaluate decisions based on outcomes instead of the information available at the time. A surgeon makes the right call based on all available evidence, but the patient dies—we blame the surgeon. An investor makes a reckless bet that happens to pay off—we call them a genius. We're confusing luck with skill."

---

### FRAME 7: The Overconfidence Problem (1:32-1:48, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Confidence calibration curve:
  - "If everything makes sense in hindsight..."
  - "...you think you can predict the future"
  - Graph showing: Perceived predictive ability (high) vs Actual predictive ability (low)
- Label: "Hindsight bias → overconfidence in foresight"

**Narration:**
"Here's the danger. If everything makes sense after it happens, you start to think you can predict what will happen next. Hindsight bias creates overconfidence in foresight. You think the future is more predictable than it is because the past feels predictable. But the past only feels predictable because you already know what happened."

---

### FRAME 8: The Learning Problem (1:48-2:04, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Learning cycle comparison:
  - BLOCKED by hindsight: "Outcome occurs → 'I knew it' → No surprise → No learning"
  - ENABLED by surprise: "Outcome occurs → 'I didn't expect that!' → Genuine surprise → Real learning"
- Label: "Surprise is the prerequisite for learning"

**Narration:**
"Hindsight bias also blocks learning. If you think you knew it all along, you're not surprised. And surprise is essential for learning. When you're genuinely surprised by an outcome, you update your model of the world. When you think you predicted it, you don't update anything. You stay overconfident and you don't learn."
