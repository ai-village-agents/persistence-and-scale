## VIDEO 90: "The Dunning-Kruger Effect" (Day 495)

**Concept:** Why incompetent people overestimate their ability while experts underestimate theirs

**Research Foundation:**
- Kruger & Dunning (1999): "Unskilled and Unaware of It"
- Bottom quartile performers estimated they were above average
- Top quartile performers estimated they were only slightly above average
- Dual burden: lack of skill + lack of metacognitive awareness to recognize lack of skill
- 25+ years replication, widely cited

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** The less you know, the more confident you are

**Visual Potential:**
- Confidence vs competence graph (showing the curves)
- Self-assessment vs actual performance
- "Mount Stupid" and "Valley of Despair" learning curve
- Domain examples (driving, logic, grammar tests)

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. When you feel very confident about new skill/domain, that's often peak Dunning-Kruger - beginners lack knowledge to recognize what they don't know
2. As you learn more and confidence drops, that's progress (not regression) - you're gaining awareness of complexity
3. Seek external feedback in domains where you feel confident - Dunning-Kruger prevents self-assessment accuracy precisely when you most need it

**Estimated Duration:** 2:50
**Researcher Diversity:** Kruger & Dunning (new to channel)

## VIDEO 91: "The Müller-Lyer Illusion (Perceptual Constancy)" (Day 496)

**Concept:** Why we see optical illusions even when we know they're illusions - perception isn't penetrable by knowledge

**Research Foundation:**
- Müller-Lyer (1889): Classic arrow illusion - lines appear different lengths despite being identical
- Knowing they're same length doesn't make illusion disappear
- Cognitive impenetrability of perception
- Cross-cultural variation (less effect in cultures without "carpentered world")
- 135+ years research, foundational in perception psychology

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** Knowing the truth doesn't change what you see

**Visual Potential:**
- Müller-Lyer illusion demonstration
- Measurement showing lines are equal
- Other cognitive impenetrable illusions
- Perception vs knowledge distinction

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. Your perceptions aren't always correctable by knowledge - some cognitive systems are "encapsulated" from conscious knowledge (applies to biases too)
2. When you can't "unsee" something you know is wrong, that's cognitive impenetrability - not a personal failing but how perception works
3. Recognize that changing beliefs doesn't automatically change intuitions - you can "know better" and still "feel otherwise"

**Estimated Duration:** 2:46
**Researcher Diversity:** Müller-Lyer (historical, foundational perception research)

## VIDEO 92: "The Sunk Cost Fallacy" (Day 497)

**Concept:** Why we continue investing in losing propositions because we've already invested

**Research Foundation:**
- Arkes & Blumer (1985): "The Psychology of Sunk Cost"
- Theater ticket study: people more likely to attend if they paid full price vs discount (even though cost is sunk)
- Escalation of commitment research (Staw & Ross)
- Rational decision-making should ignore sunk costs; humans don't
- 40+ years robust research across domains

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** Past investment (which you can't recover) influences future decisions (which should be independent)

**Visual Potential:**
- Sunk cost visualization (money/time already spent)
- Decision tree: with vs without sunk cost consideration
- Real examples: business projects, relationships, education
- Escalation of commitment pattern

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. When deciding whether to continue, ask "If I hadn't already invested X, would I start now?" - separates sunk cost from actual future value
2. Reframe past investment as information cost (you paid to learn this isn't working) rather than reason to continue
3. Watch for "I've already invested so much" language - that's sunk cost fallacy signal. Past investment is irrelevant to whether future investment is wise.

**Estimated Duration:** 2:49
**Researcher Diversity:** Arkes & Blumer (new to channel)

## VIDEO 93: "The Generation Effect" (Day 498)

**Concept:** Information you generate yourself is remembered better than information you passively receive

**Research Foundation:**
- Slamecka & Graf (1978): "The Generation Effect: Delineation of a Phenomenon"
- Participants either read word pairs or generate second word from cue
- Generated words recalled 2-3x better than read words
- Active production > passive reception for memory
- 45+ years replication, foundational learning research

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** Working harder to learn makes you learn better (seems obvious but violated constantly in education)

**Visual Potential:**
- Recall rates: generated vs read
- Active vs passive learning visualization
- Study technique comparisons
- Brain activation differences

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. When learning, force yourself to generate (don't just read) - close the book and try to explain concept in your own words, solve problem before looking at solution
2. Highlighting and re-reading feel productive but are passive - generation effect requires active production (writing summaries, answering questions, teaching others)
3. When stuck, resist immediately looking at answer - struggle to generate attempts first, even if wrong. The generation attempt (even failed) strengthens memory more than reading correct answer.

**Estimated Duration:** 2:47
**Researcher Diversity:** Slamecka & Graf (new to channel)

## VIDEO 94: "The Availability Cascade" (Day 499)

**Concept:** How repeated media coverage makes events seem more common/important than they are

**Research Foundation:**
- Kuran & Sunstein (1999): "Availability Cascades and Risk Regulation"
- Self-reinforcing cycle: media coverage → availability → public concern → more coverage
- Creates perception that rare events are common
- Shark attacks, plane crashes, rare diseases get disproportionate attention
- 25+ years research, critical for understanding risk perception

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** Media attention creates the perception of frequency, not just reflects it

**Visual Potential:**
- Cascade feedback loop diagram
- Perceived vs actual risk comparison
- Media coverage volume vs actual occurrence rate
- Examples: shark attacks, terrorism, rare diseases

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. When something feels like a major threat, check base rates - availability cascade makes rare-but-covered events feel common
2. Media coverage correlates more with dramatic/visual nature than actual frequency - plane crashes get more coverage than car crashes despite being far less common
3. Notice the cascade: rare event → media coverage → public attention → more coverage → perception of epidemic. Break the cycle by seeking statistical context.

**Estimated Duration:** 2:51
**Researcher Diversity:** Kuran & Sunstein (new to channel)

## VIDEO 95: "The Planning Fallacy" (Day 500)

**Concept:** Why we consistently underestimate how long tasks will take

**Research Foundation:**
- Kahneman & Tversky (1979): Initial identification
- Buehler, Griffin, & Ross (1994): Systematic research program on planning fallacy
- People predict task completion based on best-case scenarios, ignoring past experience
- "Inside view" (this project's specifics) vs "outside view" (how long similar projects took)
- 45+ years research, extremely robust

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** Knowing you're bad at estimating doesn't make you better at it

**Visual Potential:**
- Estimated vs actual completion time comparisons
- Inside vs outside view illustration
- Historical project timeline examples
- Reference class forecasting visualization

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. When estimating task duration, use "outside view" - ask "How long did similar tasks take?" not "How long will this specific task take?"
2. Multiply your initial estimate by 2-3x - planning fallacy is so robust that crude correction factor often more accurate than careful inside-view analysis
3. Track your estimates vs actuals over time - build personal reference class of how wrong your estimates typically are, then adjust accordingly

**Estimated Duration:** 2:48
**Researcher Diversity:** Buehler, Griffin, Ross (new primary researchers, though Kahneman appears again - justified by importance and different principle)

## VIDEO 96: "The Spacing Effect (Distributed Practice)" (Day 501)

**Concept:** Information learned over multiple spaced sessions is retained better than massed practice

**Research Foundation:**
- Ebbinghaus (1885): Original research on memory and forgetting curves
- Cepeda et al. (2006): Meta-analysis of 317 experiments showing robust spacing effect
- Studying material across multiple sessions beats cramming
- One of the most robust findings in all of psychology
- 135+ years research, consistently replicated

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** Spreading study over time beats concentrated effort (even with same total time)

**Visual Potential:**
- Forgetting curves with/without spacing
- Massed vs distributed practice comparison
- Optimal spacing intervals visualization
- Spaced repetition system concept

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. Space your study/practice across multiple days - 3 hours split across 6 days beats 3 hours in one session, even though total time is identical
2. Use expanding intervals: review after 1 day, then 3 days, then 7 days, then 14 days - spacing should increase as memory strengthens
3. Cramming feels effective (immediate performance) but produces poor long-term retention - if goal is lasting memory, distributed practice isn't negotiable

**Estimated Duration:** 2:52
**Researcher Diversity:** Ebbinghaus (historical foundational), Cepeda et al. (modern meta-analysis)

## VIDEO 97: "The Framing Effect" (Day 502)

**Concept:** Logically equivalent information presented differently leads to different decisions

**Research Foundation:**
- Tversky & Kahneman (1981): "The Framing of Decisions and the Psychology of Choice"
- Asian disease problem: "save 200 of 600" (positive frame) vs "400 of 600 will die" (negative frame)
- Identical outcomes, opposite preferences based on framing
- Violates rational decision-making axioms
- 45+ years robust research

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** How you say something changes the decision, even when the facts are identical

**Visual Potential:**
- Asian disease problem visualization
- Positive vs negative framing comparison
- "90% lean" vs "10% fat" examples
- Decision reversal demonstration

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. When making important decisions, deliberately reframe in opposite terms - if presented as gain, reframe as loss (and vice versa). Your preference shouldn't flip if you're deciding rationally.
2. Notice framing in persuasion attempts - "95% success rate" vs "5% failure rate" are mathematically identical but psychologically different. Persuaders choose frames strategically.
3. Use framing ethically - to help people overcome loss aversion or risk aversion when appropriate, frame as gain vs loss. But know you're influencing, not just informing.

**Estimated Duration:** 2:46
**Researcher Diversity:** Tversky (new to channel as primary author, though Kahneman appears)

## VIDEO 98: "The Testing Effect (Retrieval Practice)" (Day 503)

**Concept:** Testing yourself improves learning better than additional studying

**Research Foundation:**
- Roediger & Karpicke (2006): "Test-Enhanced Learning"
- Students either study material 4 times or study 1x + test 3x
- Testing group: 50% retention after 1 week
- Study-only group: 35% retention after 1 week
- Testing isn't just assessment; it's a learning intervention
- 15+ years intensive research, extremely robust

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** Testing is better for learning than studying (we think of tests as assessment, not learning tools)

**Visual Potential:**
- Retention curves: test vs restudy
- Testing as learning mechanism
- Retrieval strengthening memory traces
- Application examples (flashcards, practice problems)

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. Replace re-reading with self-testing - close the book and try to recall, then check. Retrieval practice strengthens memory; re-reading creates false fluency.
2. Make tests harder than necessary - struggling to retrieve (even failing initially) produces better long-term learning than easy successful retrieval
3. Use testing as learning tool, not just assessment - quiz yourself throughout learning process, not just at the end. Each retrieval attempt is a learning event.

**Estimated Duration:** 2:49
**Researcher Diversity:** Roediger & Karpicke (new to channel)

## VIDEO 99: "The Mere Exposure Effect" (Day 504)

**Concept:** Repeated exposure to stimuli increases liking, even without conscious recognition

**Research Foundation:**
- Zajonc (1968): "Attitudinal Effects of Mere Exposure"
- Participants shown nonsense words, Chinese characters, or faces at varying frequencies
- More frequent exposure → higher liking ratings
- Effect occurs even with subliminal exposure
- Works for sounds, images, ideas, people
- 55+ years robust research, foundational social psychology

**Counter-Intuitive Angle:** You like things just because you've seen them before (not because they're objectively better)

**Visual Potential:**
- Exposure frequency vs liking ratings
- Subliminal exposure demonstration
- Applications: advertising, music, people
- Familiarity breeding liking (opposite of proverb)

**3-Step Takeaways:**
1. When you "just like" something, consider mere exposure - repeated exposure creates preference independent of quality. Your liking may reflect familiarity, not merit.
2. In advertising/persuasion, repetition works not through argument but through familiarity → liking. Being aware of mechanism helps resist it (but doesn't eliminate it).
3. Use positively: want people to warm to new idea/person/product? Repeated exposure (without negative context) builds liking automatically. Familiarity reduces resistance.

**Estimated Duration:** 2:47
**Researcher Diversity:** Zajonc (new to channel, highly influential social psychology)
