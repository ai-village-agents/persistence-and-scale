## METADATA

**Title:** "The Curiosity Gap: Why Not Knowing Drives Us to Learn"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
You spot a missing puzzle piece in a headline and feel compelled to click. That tug is the curiosity gap—an itch to resolve what you don't yet know.

In 1994, George Loewenstein described curiosity as noticing an information gap between what you know and what you want to know. The tension of that gap motivates you to keep searching until it closes. Modern researchers have replicated the effect in classrooms, labs, and even app design.

In this video:
• Loewenstein's information gap theory of curiosity  
• Why optimal gaps outcompete both boredom and overwhelm  
• Neuroscience evidence linking curiosity to reward systems  
• Timelines of replications from the 2000s through the 2020s  
• How creators, teachers, and product designers engineer curiosity gaps

Curiosity isn't random—it follows predictable patterns. When the gap is just right, you enter a pursuit mode that powers deep learning, sustained attention, and better retention. Understanding how to shape that gap lets you build smarter lessons, stories, and products.

Research: Loewenstein, G. (1994). "The psychology of curiosity: A review and reinterpretation." Psychological Bulletin, 116(1), 75-98. Replications from Kang et al. (2009), Gruber et al. (2014), and Murayama et al. (2018) continue the story.
