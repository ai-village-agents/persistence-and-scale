## METADATA

**Title:** "The Von Restorff Effect: Why Different Things Stand Out"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
Picture a list of nine black items and one neon word. Hours later, people remember the neon one first. What makes the oddball so sticky in memory?

In 1933, Hedwig von Restorff documented the "isolation effect": when one item differs sharply from its neighbors, recall for that item soars. Later work, like Robert Hunt's 1995 deep-dive on distinctiveness, showed the same rule governs how attention shifts and how memories encode.

In this video:
• The classic isolation demonstration
• Von Restorff's original 1933 findings
• Graphing recall when one item breaks the pattern
• Why distinctiveness hijacks attention and encoding

You'll see why highlighters work, why designers use accent colors, and why marketers launch contrarian ads. The same principle powers modern education strategies and even UX design—provided the distinctiveness is purposeful, not gratuitous.

Research: von Restorff, H. (1933). "Über die Wirkung von Bereichsbildungen im Spurenfeld." Psychologische Forschung, 18, 299-342. Hunt, R. R. (1995). "The subtlety of distinctiveness: What von Restorff really did." Psychonomic Bulletin & Review, 2(1), 105-112.
