## Research Authority
- Pauline Clance & Suzanne Imes (1978): "The impostor phenomenon in high achieving women: Dynamics and therapeutic intervention." Psychotherapy: Theory, Research & Practice, 15(3), 241-247.
- 45+ years of research and clinical validation
- Originally studied in high-achieving women, later found across genders, professions, cultures
- 70% of people experience impostor feelings at some point in their careers (estimated prevalence)

## Core Concept
**Impostor Phenomenon (Impostor Syndrome):** High achievers attribute success to external factors (luck, timing, deceiving others) rather than their own competence, despite objective evidence of skill. They fear being "found out" as frauds. Paradoxically, the more they succeed, the more they feel like impostors—because they discount each achievement.

## Section 2: The Cycle (0:50-1:30)
**Visual 4 (Frame 4):** Impostor cycle diagram
- Circular flow showing self-reinforcing pattern:
  1. "New challenge/task" (top)
  2. → "Anxiety & self-doubt"
  3. → "Over-preparation OR Procrastination"
  4. → "Success achieved"
  5. → "Relief, but..."
  6. → "Discount success: 'Just luck' or 'I over-prepared, that's cheating'"
  7. → Back to "New challenge/task" (cycle repeats)
- Center text: "Cycle reinforces impostor beliefs"

**Narration:**
"Here's the cycle. You face a new challenge. Anxiety and self-doubt flood in. You respond by either over-preparing—staying up all night, triple-checking everything—or procrastinating out of fear. Eventually, you succeed. But instead of thinking 'I'm competent,' you think 'I just got lucky,' or 'I over-prepared, so it doesn't count.' The success doesn't update your self-view. Next challenge arrives, and the cycle repeats. Each success reinforces the impostor belief, because you keep discounting the evidence."

## Section 3: Why High Achievers Are Vulnerable (1:30-2:00)
**Visual 5 (Frame 5):** High achiever vulnerability factors
- Title: "WHY HIGH ACHIEVERS FEEL LIKE IMPOSTORS"
- Three factors with icons:
  1. **High Standards:** "Perfectionistic expectations make any success feel insufficient"
  2. **Selective Comparison:** "Compare their insides (self-doubt) to others' outsides (confident appearance)"
  3. **Rapid Advancement:** "Move into new roles faster than self-concept updates"

**Narration:**
"High achievers are especially vulnerable for three reasons. First, they set perfectionistic standards—so even when they succeed, it feels insufficient. They focus on the one thing they didn't do perfectly. Second, they compare their insides—full of doubt and uncertainty—to everyone else's outsides, which look confident. They don't see other people's internal struggles. Third, they advance quickly into new roles. Their achievements outpace their self-concept. They're objectively qualified, but they still feel like beginners."

## Section 4: Practical Framework (2:00-2:30)
**Visual 6 (Frame 6):** Reframing strategies
- Title: "COUNTERACT IMPOSTOR PHENOMENON"
- Three-step action framework:
  1. **NOTICE** the attribution pattern—are you discounting internal factors?
     Icon: Magnifying glass on thought bubble
  2. **COLLECT** objective evidence of competence (awards, feedback, outcomes)
     Icon: File folder with achievements
  3. **REFRAME** "I got lucky" as "I created conditions for success"
     Icon: Thought bubble transformation

**Narration:**
"You can't eliminate impostor feelings, but you can reframe them. First, notice the pattern. When you succeed, are you immediately attributing it to luck or external factors? Catch that thought. Second, collect objective evidence. Keep a folder of awards, positive feedback, successful outcomes. Your brain won't remember—make it external. Third, reframe the narrative. Instead of 'I got lucky,' say 'I prepared well and created conditions for success.' Instead of 'I fooled them,' say 'They accurately recognized my skills.' You're not lying to yourself—you're correcting a cognitive distortion."
