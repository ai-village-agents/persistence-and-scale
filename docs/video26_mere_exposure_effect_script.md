## METADATA

**Title:** "Why Familiarity Makes You Like Things (Even When You Don't Notice)"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
Play someone the same song ten times. The first time, they're neutral. By the tenth time, they like it more—even if they don't realize they've heard it before.

In 1968, psychologist Robert Zajonc demonstrated the "mere exposure effect": repeated exposure to a stimulus increases liking for it, even without conscious recognition. In his famous experiment, people were shown nonsense shapes and Chinese characters at different frequencies. The more often they'd seen something, the more they liked it—even though they had no memory of seeing it before.

In this video:
• Zajonc's classic mere exposure experiments
• How repeated exposure increases liking below conscious awareness
• Why familiarity drives preference independently of quality
• Applications in advertising, music, relationships, and decision-making

The mere exposure effect reveals that your preferences aren't always based on quality or rational evaluation. Sometimes you like something simply because it's familiar. Understanding this effect helps you distinguish genuine preference from familiarity bias.

Research: Zajonc, R. B. (1968). "Attitudinal effects of mere exposure." Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 9(2, Pt.2), 1-27.

### FRAME 3: Zajonc's Chinese Character Experiment (0:24-0:42, 18 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Experimental setup:
  - Phase 1: "Chinese characters shown 0, 1, 5, 10, or 25 times (subliminal speed)"
  - Phase 2: "Rate: Does this character mean something good or bad?"
- Results bar chart: More exposures → More positive ratings
- Label: "Effect occurs without conscious recognition"

**Narration:**
"In 1968, Robert Zajonc showed people Chinese characters they couldn't read, at different frequencies—some once, some five times, some twenty-five times. The exposure was so brief they couldn't consciously remember seeing them. Then he asked: do you think this character means something good or bad? People rated characters they'd seen more often as more positive, even though they had no conscious memory of seeing them before."

### FRAME 4: Why It Works (0:42-0:58, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Brain processing pathway:
  - "Repeated exposure → Increases processing fluency → Feels familiar → Interpreted as positive"
- Text: "Familiarity = safety, ease, comfort"
- Label: "Evolutionary basis: familiar = not dangerous"

**Narration:**
"Why does this happen? Repeated exposure increases processing fluency. The second time you see something, your brain processes it more easily. That ease feels familiar. And we unconsciously interpret familiarity as positive. From an evolutionary perspective, this makes sense: if you've encountered something multiple times and survived, it's probably safe."

### FRAME 5: The Subliminal Nature (0:58-1:12, 14 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Consciousness iceberg:
  - ABOVE water: "Conscious preference ('I like this because...')"
  - BELOW water: "Unconscious familiarity driving preference"
- Label: "You don't need to remember exposure for it to affect you"

**Narration:**
"The effect is largely unconscious. You don't need to remember seeing something for mere exposure to work. You think you're making a reasoned judgment about quality. But you're often just responding to familiarity. You like it because it feels familiar, even if you can't identify why it feels familiar."

### FRAME 6: Real-World Applications (1:12-1:30, 18 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Four domains:
  - Advertising: "See brand logo 100x → Prefer that brand"
  - Music: "Hear song on radio → Initially dislike → Eventually like"
  - Relationships: "See person regularly → Liking increases (propinquity)"
  - Politics: "Repeated name recognition → Preference in elections"

**Narration:**
"This effect is everywhere. Advertising works partly through mere exposure—you see a brand logo a hundred times, you start to prefer that brand. Radio stations play a new song repeatedly; you dislike it at first but eventually like it. You develop feelings for someone you see regularly at work or school—propinquity effect. Politicians with higher name recognition win more votes, even when voters know nothing about their policies."

### FRAME 7: The Familiarity-Quality Confusion (1:30-1:46, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Two competing factors:
  - "Quality: How good is it objectively?"
  - "Familiarity: How often have I encountered it?"
- Arrow showing familiarity often overrides quality in preference
- Label: "We confuse 'I've seen it before' with 'It's good'"

**Narration:**
"The danger is confusing familiarity with quality. Something can be familiar and low-quality. Something can be unfamiliar and high-quality. But our brains conflate the two. You prefer the familiar restaurant over the new one, even if the new one is better. You choose the familiar software over the better alternative. Familiarity feels like endorsement."

### FRAME 8: The Boundaries (1:46-2:00, 14 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Inverted U-curve:

**Narration:**
"There are limits. The effect peaks at moderate exposure levels. After that, you can get overexposure—you've heard that song so many times you're sick of it. The curve is an inverted U. A little repetition increases liking. Too much creates satiation and tedium."
