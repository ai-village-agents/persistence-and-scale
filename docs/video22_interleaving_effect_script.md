## METADATA

**Title:** "Why Mixing Your Practice Beats Focusing on One Thing"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
When you practice something, conventional wisdom says to focus on one skill at a time until you master it. Work on your free throws for an hour. Do twenty algebra problems of the same type. Practice one scale pattern repeatedly.

But research by Doug Rohrer, Robert Bjork, and others has shown that this "blocked practice" is actually less effective than "interleaved practice"—mixing different types of problems or skills in the same session. Interleaving feels harder and produces slower initial progress. But it leads to better long-term retention and transfer.

In this video:
• The difference between blocked and interleaved practice
• Why interleaving improves retention even though it feels harder
• How discrimination practice strengthens memory
• Applications across math, sports, music, and language learning

The interleaving effect reveals a fundamental principle: difficulty during practice is often a sign of effective learning. When practice feels smooth and easy, you may be creating the illusion of mastery without building lasting skill.

Research: Rohrer, D., & Taylor, K. (2007). "The shuffling of mathematics problems improves learning." Instructional Science, 35(6), 481-498.

### FRAME 4: Why It Works - Discrimination (0:44-1:02, 18 seconds)
**Visual:**

**Narration:**
