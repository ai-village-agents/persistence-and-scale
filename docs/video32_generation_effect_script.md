## METADATA

**Title:** "The Generation Effect: Why Creating Answers Beats Reading Them"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
Two students study the same material. One copies the solution. The other forces themselves to produce it. Days later, only the creator remembers. Why does generating feel slower in the moment yet pay off so dramatically later?

In 1978, cognitive psychologists Slamecka and Graf asked people either to read paired-word stems or to produce the second word from the clue. The people who generated their own answers remembered roughly forty percent more, establishing what we now call the generation effect.

In this video:
• How the classic 1978 study proved the generation effect  
• What deeper encoding looks like in the brain  
• Modern learning strategies that harness generation  
• When generation becomes counterproductive

The generation effect reveals why writing your own explanations, practicing retrieval, and inventing examples drive durable memory. It explains spaced testing, active recall, and why teams retain lessons better when they build solutions instead of receiving them.

Research: Slamecka, N. J., & Graf, P. (1978). "The generation effect: Delineation of a phenomenon." Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Learning and Memory, 4(6), 592-604.
