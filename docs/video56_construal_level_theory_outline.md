## Core Concept
**Construal Level Theory:** Psychological distance (temporal, spatial, social, or hypothetical) changes how you think about events. Distant events are represented abstractly ("why" you do something, values, ideals). Near events are represented concretely ("how" you do it, feasibility, obstacles). Your future self seems disciplined because you're thinking abstractly about values; your present self wants pizza because you're thinking concretely about immediate pleasure.

## Section 2: The Classic Studies (0:55-1:40)
**Visual 4 (Frame 4):** Temporal distance study
- Title: "MOVING APARTMENTS: NEAR VS. FAR"
- Two scenarios:
  * **Help someone move tomorrow (near):**

    - Fewer agree to help
  * **Help someone move next year (far):**

- Result: Same task, distance changes evaluation

**Visual 5 (Frame 5):** Preference reversals graph
- Title: "PREFERENCES SHIFT WITH DISTANCE"
- Two items compared:
  * **Near future**: Prefer high feasibility over high desirability (concrete wins)
  * **Far future**: Prefer high desirability over high feasibility (abstract wins)
- Example: 
  * Near: "Choose convenient local college (feasible)"
  * Far: "Choose prestigious distant college (desirable)"
- Label: "What you choose depends on when you choose"

**Narration:**
"The research shows dramatic shifts. Ask people to help someone move tomorrow, they think about the concrete costs—heavy lifting, early morning, stairs. Fewer agree. Ask them to help someone move next year, they think about the abstract benefits—being a good friend, helping out. More agree. Same task, but distance changes evaluation. Another study: when choosing a college for next year, people prefer the prestigious distant college—abstract desirability wins. When choosing a college they'd attend next week, they prefer the convenient local college—concrete feasibility wins. What you choose depends on when you're choosing."

## Section 3: Real-World Implications (1:40-2:20)
**Visual 6 (Frame 6):** Applications across domains
- Title: "WHERE CONSTRUAL LEVEL SHOWS UP"
- Four examples:
  1. **New Year's Resolutions:** Abstract goals (far) feel achievable; concrete execution (near) reveals obstacles
  2. **Procrastination:** Distant deadline = abstract "I'll handle it"; close deadline = concrete panic
  3. **Moral Licensing:** Distant future good deed justifies present bad behavior (abstract offsets concrete)
  4. **Political Judgments:** Judge distant leaders by values (why), near leaders by effectiveness (how)

**Narration:**
"Construal Level Theory explains so much behavior. New Year's resolutions: on December 31st, 'exercise daily' feels abstract and achievable—it's about health and discipline. On January 2nd, you face concrete obstacles—it's cold, you're tired, the gym is crowded. Procrastination: a deadline three months away feels abstract and manageable. A deadline tomorrow triggers concrete panic. Moral licensing: planning to donate to charity next month (abstract good) makes you feel justified eating dessert now (concrete pleasure). And political judgments: you judge distant leaders by their values and vision—why they lead. You judge local leaders by their effectiveness—how they govern."

## Section 4: Practical Framework (2:20-2:45)
**Visual 7 (Frame 7):** Using construal awareness
- Title: "USE CONSTRUAL LEVEL THEORY"
- Three-step framework:
  1. **NOTICE** construal shift—am I thinking abstract (why) or concrete (how)?
  2. **BRIDGE** the gap—make distant goals concrete, make near obstacles abstract
  3. **PLAN** with both levels—"Why am I doing this?" + "How will I handle obstacles?"

**Narration:**
"Here's how to use this. First, notice the shift. When planning, ask: am I thinking abstractly about why, or concretely about how? Second, bridge the gap. Make distant goals concrete—'exercise daily' becomes 'lay out gym clothes the night before.' Make near obstacles abstract—'I'm tired now' becomes 'this supports my health goals.' Third, plan with both levels. Start with why—your values and goals. Then move to how—specific steps and obstacle plans. Don't let distance deceive you."
