### Video 20: Confirmation Bias (Day 425, May 29)
**Researcher:** Peter Wason (1960s)  
**Study:** Wason Selection Task (1968)  
**Category:** Cognitive Bias  
**Runtime target:** 2:45  
**Quality target:** 4.7/5

**Key points:**
- People seek information that confirms existing beliefs
- Wason's 2-4-6 task demonstrates the bias
- Impacts everything from research to politics
- Counter-intuitive: we think we're objective

**Takeaways:**
1. Notice when you're only seeking confirming evidence
2. Ask "What would prove me wrong?"
3. Actively seek disconfirming information

**Visual approach:** Cards showing hidden/revealed information, graphs of hypothesis testing

### Video 21: Sunk Cost Fallacy (Day 426, May 30)
**Researcher:** Hal Arkes & Catherine Blumer (1985)  
**Study:** "The Psychology of Sunk Cost"  
**Category:** Decision Making  
**Runtime target:** 2:50  
**Quality target:** 4.7/5

**Key points:**
- Past costs shouldn't influence future decisions
- We irrationally escalate commitment
- Theater ticket experiment demonstrates effect
- Emotional investment compounds the fallacy

**Takeaways:**
1. Notice when you're justifying with "already invested"
2. Ask "Would I start this today knowing what I know?"
3. Focus on future costs/benefits, not past

**Visual approach:** Investment bars growing, decision trees, timeline comparisons

### Video 22: Interleaving Effect (Day 427, May 31)
**Researcher:** Doug Rohrer & Robert Bjork (2000s)  
**Study:** Multiple studies on interleaved practice  
**Category:** Learning Science  
**Runtime target:** 2:55  
**Quality target:** 4.7/5

**Key points:**
- Mixing different topics improves retention vs blocking
- Feels harder but produces better learning
- Discrimination practice strengthens memory
- Applies to math, sports, music, languages

**Takeaways:**
1. Mix different problem types in practice sessions
2. Don't mass practice one skill at a time
3. Embrace the feeling of difficulty—it means it's working

**Visual approach:** Calendar showing blocked vs interleaved schedules, performance graphs

### Video 23: Fundamental Attribution Error (Day 428, Jun 1)
**Researcher:** Lee Ross (1977)  
**Study:** "The Intuitive Psychologist"  
**Category:** Social Psychology  
**Runtime target:** 2:40  
**Quality target:** 4.7/5

**Key points:**
- We attribute others' behavior to character, our own to situation
- Classic studies: essay assignments, quiz show paradigm
- Underlies many interpersonal conflicts
- Cultural variations exist

**Takeaways:**
1. When someone behaves badly, consider situational factors
2. Notice the asymmetry (you vs others)
3. Ask "What circumstances might explain this?"

**Visual approach:** Split-screen showing same behavior with different attributions

### Video 24: Mental Accounting (Day 429, Jun 2)
**Researcher:** Richard Thaler (1980s-90s)  
**Study:** Multiple papers on mental accounting  
**Category:** Behavioral Economics  
**Runtime target:** 2:45  
**Quality target:** 4.7/5

**Key points:**
- We treat money differently based on arbitrary categories
- Violates fungibility principle of economics
- Movie ticket vs found $10 experiment
- Impacts savings, spending, investment decisions

**Takeaways:**
1. Notice when you're treating identical money differently
2. Remember money is fungible across categories
3. Make decisions based on total financial picture

**Visual approach:** Envelope budgeting visualization, accounts with barriers

### Video 25: Levels of Processing (Day 430, Jun 3)
**Researcher:** Fergus Craik & Robert Lockhart (1972)  
**Study:** "Levels of Processing: A Framework"  
**Category:** Memory/Cognition  
**Runtime target:** 2:50  
**Quality target:** 4.7/5

**Key points:**
- Deep processing (meaning) beats shallow (appearance/sound)
- Semantic processing creates stronger memories
- Incidental learning can exceed intentional learning
- Explains why elaboration works

**Takeaways:**
1. Ask "What does this mean?" not just "What does this say?"
2. Connect new information to existing knowledge
3. Process information semantically for better retention

**Visual approach:** Pyramid showing processing depths, memory strength bars

### Video 26: Mere Exposure Effect (Day 431, Jun 4)
**Researcher:** Robert Zajonc (1968)  
**Study:** "Attitudinal Effects of Mere Exposure"  
**Category:** Social Psychology  
**Runtime target:** 2:35  
**Quality target:** 4.7/5

**Key points:**
- Repeated exposure increases liking (without awareness)
- Chinese characters experiment demonstrated effect
- Operates below conscious threshold
- Explains advertising effectiveness, relationship formation

**Takeaways:**
1. Notice when familiarity drives preference
2. Be aware exposure ≠ quality
3. Use deliberately: expose yourself to beneficial things

**Visual approach:** Familiarity gradient, repeated symbols becoming more liked

### Video 27: Desirable Difficulties (Day 432, Jun 5)
**Researcher:** Robert Bjork (1994+)  
**Study:** Multiple papers on "desirable difficulties"  
**Category:** Learning Science  
**Runtime target:** 2:55  
**Quality target:** 4.7/5

**Key points:**
- Learning that feels easy often doesn't last
- Difficulty during learning improves long-term retention
- Generation effect, testing effect, spacing all create desirable difficulties
- Disconnect between performance and learning

**Takeaways:**
1. Embrace struggle during learning
2. Don't mistake current ease for future retention
3. Choose harder, more effortful study methods

**Visual approach:** Performance vs learning curves, difficulty-retention relationship

### Video 28: Dunning-Kruger Effect (Day 433, Jun 6)
**Researcher:** David Dunning & Justin Kruger (1999)  
**Study:** "Unskilled and Unaware of It"  
**Category:** Metacognition  
**Runtime target:** 2:45  
**Quality target:** 4.7/5

**Key points:**
- Incompetence includes inability to recognize incompetence
- Skills needed to perform = skills needed to evaluate performance
- Not just "dumb people think they're smart"
- Double burden of the unskilled

**Takeaways:**
1. Seek external feedback, especially when confident
2. Notice the domains where you feel most certain
3. Remember: if it seems easy, you might be missing something

**Visual approach:** Classic confidence-competence curve, skill assessment gaps

### Video 29: Working Memory Limits (Day 434, Jun 7)
**Researcher:** George Miller (1956) & Nelson Cowan (2001)  
**Study:** "The Magical Number Seven" → ~4 chunks  
**Category:** Cognitive Psychology  
**Runtime target:** 2:50  
**Quality target:** 4.7/5

**Key points:**
- Working memory holds ~4 chunks (not 7±2 as originally thought)
- Chunking expands effective capacity
- Explains cognitive load in learning, UI design, problem-solving
- Different from long-term memory capacity

**Takeaways:**
1. Break information into chunks of 3-4 items
2. When overwhelmed, externalize working memory (write it down)
3. Design for limited working memory (presentations, instructions)

**Visual approach:** Memory slots filling up, chunking visualization
