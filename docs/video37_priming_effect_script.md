## METADATA

**Title:** "Priming: How Subtle Cues Shape Your Behavior"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
A screen flashes the word "NURSE" just before you respond "yes" to "doctor." Milliseconds matter: unseen cues nudge your brain to move faster. That's priming—subtle exposure that tunes perception and judgment before you even notice.

In 1971, David Meyer and Roger Schvaneveldt showed that people recognize "nurse" faster when it follows "doctor" than when it follows "bread." Their lexical decision experiments revealed robust semantic priming that has been replicated for decades and across modalities.

This episode:  
• Priming demonstrations you can feel in real time  
• Meyer & Schvaneveldt's pioneering design and findings  
• How spreading activation in semantic networks makes priming work  
• Why some priming claims collapsed under replication scrutiny

Priming helps explain why ad jingles influence shopping, why mood lighting shifts wine ratings, and why everyday cues shape snap judgments. The effect is powerful, but context-dependent; not every priming claim survives exacting tests.

Research: Meyer, D. E., & Schvaneveldt, R. W. (1971). Bargh, J. A., Chen, M., & Burrows, L. (1996).
