## METADATA

**Title:** "The Framing Effect: How Wording Changes Decisions"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
A sudden outbreak threatens 600 people. Frame the plan as lives saved and the majority picks the sure thing. Phrase the exact same numbers as deaths and the majority suddenly prefers the risky gamble. Words alone flip the choice.

In 1981, Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman demonstrated that logically identical options can produce opposite decisions depending on whether outcomes are presented as gains or losses. Their famous "Asian disease problem" revealed the framing effect and helped ignite the heuristics-and-biases revolution in decision science.

In this video:
• The Asian disease classic experiment, step by step
• The original Science paper's gain vs. loss data visualization
• Prospect theory's explanation of loss aversion under uncertainty
• Practical examples in medicine, investing, and marketing

We'll connect the lab findings to forty years of follow-up research, show why context matters for public-facing policy, and give you a three-step checklist to spot and neutralize frames before they nudge you.

Research: Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1981). "The framing of decisions and the psychology of choice." Science, 211(4481), 453-458.

**Visual:**
- Bar chart with two clusters labeled "Gain Frame" and "Loss Frame"

### FRAME 4: The Reversal (0:43-0:58, 15 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Animated cards flipping from "Save" to "Die" wording
- Overlay text: "Risk-averse in gains → Risk-seeking in losses"
- Simple decision tree emphasizing same expected value

**Narration:**
"Framing pushes people toward risk aversion when outcomes sound like gains, and risk seeking when they sound like losses. Prospect theory calls this the reflection effect: we switch preferences simply because of presentation."

### FRAME 5: Why It Works (0:58-1:16, 18 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Prospect theory value curve with steeper slope for losses than gains
- Callouts: "Loss aversion", "Reference point = status quo"
- Annotation arrows showing diminishing sensitivity

**Narration:**
"Losses loom larger than gains. Prospect theory explains that we evaluate options relative to a reference point. Saving two hundred lives feels good, but facing four hundred deaths feels catastrophic. That asymmetry nudges us toward whichever option softens the perceived loss."
