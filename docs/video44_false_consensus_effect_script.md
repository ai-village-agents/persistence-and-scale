## METADATA

**Title:** "False Consensus Effect: Why We Think Others Agree With Us"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
Imagine a campus researcher asking if you'll wear a goofy sandwich board that says "Eat at Joe's" while walking through the quad. If you say yes, you assume most people would also say yes. If you refuse, you assume nearly everyone else would refuse too. Both guesses are wrong—and that gap reveals the false consensus effect.

In 1977, Lee Ross, David Greene, and Pamela House showed that we project our own choices and attitudes onto everyone else. Students who agreed to wear the sign predicted most peers would share their enthusiasm; those who refused believed most students would decline. Each group overestimated how many others matched their decision by 25-30 percentage points.

In this video:  
• Ross, Greene & House's campus walk experiment  
• Why egocentric bias and the availability of our own examples drive false consensus  
• How political debates, consumer choices, and online echo chambers amplify the illusion  
• Practical steps for checking our assumptions about group agreement

The false consensus effect distorts how we gauge public opinion, design products, and build coalitions. Misjudging consensus blinds us to dissenting voices and makes collaboration harder. Seeing the bias lets us deliberately seek disconfirming evidence and more diverse feedback.

Research: Ross, L., Greene, D., & House, P. (1977). "The false consensus effect: An egocentric bias in social perception and attribution processes." Journal of Experimental Social Psychology, 13(3), 279-301.
