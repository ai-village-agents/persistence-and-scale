## Research Authority
- Thomas Gilovich, Victoria Medvec, Kenneth Savitsky (2000): "The spotlight effect in social judgment: An egocentric bias in estimates of the salience of one's own actions and appearance." Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 78(2), 211-222.
- 20+ years of replication in social psychology
- 1,000+ citations, explains social anxiety, public speaking fear, embarrassment responses
- Effect robust across cultures and age groups

## Core Concept
**Spotlight Effect:** People overestimate how much others notice their appearance, actions, and mistakes—typically by a factor of 2x. You think 50% of people noticed your embarrassing moment; reality: 25% noticed. You feel like you're under a spotlight, but most people are focused on themselves, not you.

## Section 2: Why It Happens (0:50-1:25)
**Visual 4 (Frame 4):** Egocentric bias mechanism
- Title: "WHY THE SPOTLIGHT EFFECT EXISTS"
- Center: "YOUR PERSPECTIVE" (large circle)
- Inside circle: "You = center of your experience, 100% attention on yourself"
- Outside circle: Multiple small "OTHERS" circles
- Each "OTHERS" circle has internal focus on "themselves"
- Bottom text: "You're not the center of anyone else's attention"

**Narration:**
"Here's why it happens. You are the center of your own experience. You spend 100% of your time with yourself—your thoughts, your appearance, your mistakes. So naturally, you assume you're equally prominent in everyone else's awareness. But you're not. Every other person is the center of their own experience. They're thinking about their own appearance, their own mistakes, their own worries. You're peripheral to them, just as they're peripheral to you. The spotlight you feel is mostly in your own head."

## Section 3: Real-World Impact (1:25-2:00)
**Visual 5 (Frame 5):** Applications and relief
- Title: "WHERE THE SPOTLIGHT EFFECT SHOWS UP"
- Four examples with icons:
  1. **Public Speaking:** Feel like everyone notices every mistake; they don't
  2. **Social Blunders:** Overestimate how many people saw/remembered your awkward moment
  3. **Appearance Anxiety:** Think everyone notices bad hair day; most don't
  4. **Performance Pressure:** Believe small errors ruin entire presentation; audience barely registers them

**Narration:**
"The Spotlight Effect drives social anxiety. Public speaking: you stumble over a word and think the whole audience noticed. Most didn't. Social blunders: you said something awkward at a party and think everyone will remember. They won't—they're replaying their own awkward moments. Appearance anxiety: you have a bad hair day and feel like everyone's staring. They're not—they're worried about their own appearance. Performance pressure: you make a small error in a presentation and think it ruined everything. The audience barely registered it. You're not under a spotlight. You're under a flashlight you're holding on yourself."

## Section 4: Practical Framework (2:00-2:30)
**Visual 6 (Frame 6):** Spotlight Effect antidote
- Title: "COUNTERACT THE SPOTLIGHT EFFECT"
- Three-step framework:
  1. **NOTICE** when you feel "everyone is watching" → they're not
  2. **HALVE** your estimate → if you think 50% noticed, reality is ~25%
  3. **REMEMBER** others are focused on themselves, not you

**Narration:**
"Here's how to fight it. First, notice when you feel like everyone's watching—that's the Spotlight Effect activating. Second, literally cut your estimate in half. If you think 50% of people noticed, assume reality is 25%. The research supports this. Third, remember that everyone else is the protagonist of their own story. They're not thinking about your mistake—they're thinking about their own. This isn't about being invisible—it's about being realistically visible."
