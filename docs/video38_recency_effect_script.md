## METADATA

**Title:** "The Recency Effect: Why Last Impressions Matter"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
People study a list of words and then recall them. Items from the end of the list dominate recall—unless too much time passes. This video unpacks the recency effect and why the freshest information wins our memory battles.

In 1962, psychologist Bennet Murdock mapped the "serial position curve," showing powerful primacy and recency advantages. Recent work reveals that recency is especially fragile: a brief delay wipes it out, revealing how working memory keeps the newest items alive.

In this video:
• Murdock's classic word-list experiments
• How the serial position curve predicts recall
• Recency vs primacy and when each dominates
• Why working memory fuels the recency bump

We follow the research arc from 1960s lab studies to 21st-century neuroscience and applied settings. You'll see how presenters, negotiators, and storytellers exploit recency, and what happens when a delay erases it. Learn to design messages where the last impression is the lasting impression.

Research: Murdock, B. B. (1962). "The serial position effect of free recall." Journal of Experimental Psychology, 64(5), 482-488. Complementary work: Glanzer & Cunitz (1966); Miller et al. (2012).
