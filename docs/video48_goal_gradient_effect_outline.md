## Research Authority
- Clark Hull (1932): Original discovery in rat maze experiments, foundational behavioral psychology
- Ran Kivetz, Oleg Urminsky, Yuhuang Zheng (2006): Modern application to loyalty programs and human goal pursuit
- Published in Journal of Marketing Research, 300+ citations
- Effect replicated across physical tasks, digital interfaces, loyalty programs, fitness tracking

## Core Concept
**Goal Gradient Effect:** As people get closer to completing a goal, their effort and motivation increase. The final steps receive disproportionate energy compared to the first steps. Originally discovered in rats running mazes faster as they neared food, now used in loyalty cards, progress bars, and habit tracking.

- Both require 10 purchases to complete

**Narration:**
"In 2006, researchers Kivetz, Urminsky, and Zheng tested this with humans. They gave customers two types of coffee loyalty cards. Card A: buy 10, get one free. Card B: buy 12, get one free, but we've given you two stamps to start. Both cards require 10 purchases, but Card B customers finished 20% faster. Why? They felt closer to the goal from day one."

**Visual 5 (Frame 5):** Completion rate graph

**Narration:**
"The data was dramatic. When customers had 8 stamps left, they visited every 9 days. When they had 2 stamps left, they came back every 6 days—30% faster. The closer the finish line, the more motivated they became. The goal gradient effect, now in human behavior."

  * "Near goal" (inner ring, electric blue): "Concrete, imminent, urgent"

**Narration:**

Instead of one finish line in 3 months, create 12 weekly finish lines. You'll trigger the gradient effect 12 times. Third, make progress visible and frequent. Every checkbox, every small win, reinforces the feeling that you're getting closer."
