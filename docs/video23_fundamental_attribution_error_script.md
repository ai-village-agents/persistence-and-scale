## METADATA

**Title:** "Why You Judge Others by Their Character but Yourself by Your Situation"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
Someone cuts you off in traffic. You think: "What a jerk." Later, you cut someone off. You think: "I didn't see them—the sun was in my eyes."

When we explain other people's behavior, we overattribute it to their personality and underweight the situation. When we explain our own behavior, we do the opposite. This asymmetry, named by psychologist Lee Ross in 1977, is called the fundamental attribution error.

In this video:
• Lee Ross's concept of the fundamental attribution error
• Why we overestimate personality and underestimate situation
• The actor-observer asymmetry in attribution
• How this bias shapes judgments of success, failure, and character

This error drives misunderstandings in relationships, unfair judgments at work, and oversimplified narratives about success and failure. Understanding the fundamental attribution error is essential for fair judgment and accurate social perception.

Research: Ross, L. (1977). "The intuitive psychologist and his shortcomings: Distortions in the attribution process." In L. Berkowitz (Ed.), Advances in Experimental Social Psychology, Vol. 10, 173-220.

### FRAME 2: The Attribution Asymmetry (0:08-0:24, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Split screen showing two explanations for same behavior:
  - LEFT: "When OTHERS do something" → Arrow pointing to "We blame their personality/character"
  - RIGHT: "When WE do something" → Arrow pointing to "We blame the situation/circumstances"

**Narration:**
"We have a double standard for explaining behavior. When other people do something negative, we attribute it to their character. They're rude, lazy, incompetent. When we do the same thing, we attribute it to our situation. The alarm didn't go off. The instructions were unclear. Someone else made a mistake."

### FRAME 4: Lee Ross and the FAE (0:38-0:54, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Text: "Lee Ross (1977): The Fundamental Attribution Error"
- Definition displayed: "Overestimating personality, underestimating situation when judging others"

**Narration:**
"Psychologist Lee Ross named this the fundamental attribution error in 1977. Fundamental because it's so pervasive and deep-rooted. We systematically overestimate the role of personality and underestimate the role of situation when explaining other people's behavior. We think we're seeing their true character when we're often just seeing their response to circumstances."

### FRAME 5: Real-World Consequences (0:54-1:12, 18 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Four scenarios:
  - Workplace: "Employee underperforms" → Judge: "They're lazy" vs Reality: "Dealing with family crisis"
  - School: "Student fails test" → Judge: "Not smart" vs Reality: "Works two jobs, no sleep"
  - Relationships: "Partner snaps at you" → Judge: "They're mean" vs Reality: "Stressful day at work"
  - Success: "CEO succeeds" → Judge: "Brilliant leader" vs Reality: "Inherited advantages + luck"

**Narration:**
"This error shapes everything. An employee underperforms: we think they're lazy. We miss that they're dealing with a family crisis. A student fails: we think they're not smart. We miss that they work two jobs and got no sleep. Your partner snaps at you: we think they're mean. We miss that they had a brutal day at work. A CEO succeeds: we think they're brilliant. We miss inherited advantages and luck."

### FRAME 6: The Actor-Observer Asymmetry (1:12-1:28, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Two perspectives on same event:
  - OBSERVER view (looking at someone else): Person silhouette emphasized, context faded
  - ACTOR view (your own perspective): Context emphasized, self less salient
- Labels: "When judging others: person is salient" | "When judging self: situation is salient"

**Narration:**
"Why does this happen? When you observe someone else, their behavior is what's salient. You see them being rude. You don't see their pain, their stress, their constraints. When you're the actor, the situation is salient. You feel the pain, the stress, the constraints. You see all the reasons you had no choice."

---

### FRAME 7: The Visibility Problem (1:28-1:44, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Iceberg metaphor:
  - ABOVE water: "Visible behavior" (small portion)
  - BELOW water: "Invisible situation" (large portion: stress, constraints, history, circumstances)
- Text: "We judge on what we see, not what we don't"

**Narration:**
"We're judging on incomplete information. Behavior is visible. But the situation is often invisible. You don't see someone's chronic pain, their financial stress, their family obligations, the bad news they just received. You only see them being short with you. So you conclude: that's who they are. But you're missing ninety percent of the story."

### FRAME 8: The Success and Failure Narrative (1:44-2:00, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Two success stories:
  - "Others' success" → Arrow: "We attribute to talent/hard work/character"
  - "Our success" → Arrow: "We attribute to effort + situation + luck"
- Two failure stories:
  - "Others' failure" → Arrow: "We attribute to lack of talent/laziness"
  - "Our failure" → Arrow: "We attribute to circumstances/bad luck"

**Narration:**
"This error creates unfair narratives about success and failure. When others succeed, we attribute it to their talent or hard work—we downplay their advantages. When we succeed, we notice both our effort and our luck. When others fail, we think they lacked talent or didn't try hard enough. When we fail, we see all the obstacles and bad luck."

**Visual:**

**Narration:**
"So here's what to do. When someone does something that bothers you, pause and ask: what situation might explain this? Are they stressed? Did something just happen? What constraints might they be facing? Don't assume you're seeing their character."

### FRAME 10: Takeaway 2 & 3, Close (2:12-2:40, 28 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Large number "2" in gold
- Text: "Give others the same situational credit you give yourself"
- Large number "3" in gold
- Text: "Recognize: behavior is person × situation, not person alone"
- Bottom text: "Lee Ross (1977) | The Fundamental Attribution Error"

**Narration:**
"Second, give others the same situational credit you give yourself. You're late because of traffic—maybe they were too. You're irritable because you didn't sleep—maybe they didn't either. Extend the same generosity of interpretation.

Third, remember that behavior is always the result of both person and situation. Not person alone. Not situation alone. The interaction of both. You're not just seeing who someone is. You're seeing who they are in this specific context at this specific moment. That's a much humbler and much more accurate way to judge."
