## METADATA

**Title:** "Why You Can Only Hold 4 Things in Your Mind at Once"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
Try to remember this list: apple, car, justice, seven, Paris, quickly, purple, democracy, hammer. How many can you hold in your mind right now without looking back?

For decades, psychologists thought working memory could hold "seven plus or minus two" items, based on George Miller's famous 1956 paper. But modern research by Nelson Cowan and others has shown the real limit is closer to four chunks. Working memory—your mental workspace—is surprisingly small.

In this video:
• George Miller's "magical number seven" and why it was revised
• Nelson Cowan's research showing ~4 chunk limit
• What "chunking" means and how it expands capacity
• Why working memory limits affect learning, design, and problem-solving

Working memory limits explain why complex instructions overwhelm us, why multitasking fails, why good design matters, and why writing things down helps us think. Understanding these limits is essential for effective learning, communication, and cognitive work.

Research: Miller, G. A. (1956). "The magical number seven, plus or minus two." Psychological Review, 63(2), 81-97. | Cowan, N. (2001). "The magical number 4 in short-term memory." Behavioral and Brain Sciences, 24(1), 87-114.

**Narration:**
"Working memory is different from long-term memory. Long-term memory is vast—you can store thousands of facts, experiences, skills. When you're reading this sentence, doing math in your head, or following directions, you're using working memory. And it's shockingly small."

### FRAME 3: Miller's Magical Number Seven (0:24-0:40, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Historical reference: "George Miller (1956): The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two"
- Visual: 7 slots/boxes representing memory capacity
- Label: "Classic finding: 7±2 items"

**Narration:**
"In 1956, George Miller published a famous paper called 'The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two.' He found that people could hold about seven items in working memory—sometimes as few as five, sometimes as many as nine. For decades, seven became the standard estimate. Phone numbers are seven digits. Lists are often seven items. But more recent research has revised this number downward."

---

### FRAME 4: The Revision: Four Chunks (0:40-0:58, 18 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Updated research: "Nelson Cowan (2001): The Magical Number 4"
- Visual: 4 slots/boxes (compared to previous 7)
- Key distinction: "4 chunks, not 4 items"
- Label: "Modern estimate: ~4 chunks in focus"

**Narration:**
"In 2001, Nelson Cowan reviewed decades of research and argued the real limit is closer to four chunks—not seven. When you control for rehearsal and other memory strategies, working memory holds about four units of information. This is smaller than most people think. Your mental workspace is four slots. Once those slots are full, you can't hold anything else without something dropping out."

---

### FRAME 5: What is a Chunk? (0:58-1:16, 18 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Chunking visualization:
  - INDIVIDUAL ITEMS: "F-B-I-N-A-S-A-U-S-A" (10 items, overwhelming)
  - CHUNKED: "FBI, NASA, USA" (3 chunks, manageable)
- Label: "Chunking = grouping items into meaningful units"

**Narration:**
"But here's the key: the limit is four chunks, not four items. A chunk is a meaningful unit. The letters F, B, I are three items. But if you group them as 'FBI,' that's one chunk. The letters N, A, S, A are four items. As 'NASA,' it's one chunk. Chunking dramatically expands effective capacity. Four chunks can hold four single letters—or four entire concepts if you've learned to chunk them."

### FRAME 6: Why Chunking Matters (1:16-1:32, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Expert vs Novice comparison:
  - NOVICE: Sees chess board as ~20 individual piece positions (overload)
  - EXPERT: Sees chess board as ~4 patterns/configurations (manageable)
- Label: "Expertise = better chunking"

**Narration:**
"This is why expertise matters. A chess novice looks at a board and sees twenty individual pieces—way beyond working memory capacity. A chess expert sees four or five patterns: a king-side attack, a pawn structure, a tactical motif. Same board, but the expert has chunked the information. This is what expertise looks like at the cognitive level: better chunking."

### FRAME 7: When Working Memory Overloads (1:32-1:50, 18 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Four scenarios of overload:
  - Complex instructions: "Step 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7..." → Forget early steps
  - Multitasking: Juggling tasks → All degrade
  - Dense presentations: Too much info on slide → Can't process
  - Problem-solving: Trying to track too many variables → Get lost

**Narration:**
"Working memory overload is everywhere. You're given complex instructions with seven steps—by step five, you've forgotten step one. You try to multitask—both tasks suffer because you're splitting four slots between them. A presentation has ten bullet points on one slide—you can't process it. You're solving a problem with five variables—you lose track. These are all working memory failures."

### FRAME 8: Long-Term vs Working Memory (1:50-2:06, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Clarifying distinction:
  - LONG-TERM MEMORY: "Unlimited capacity | Can store millions of facts"
  - WORKING MEMORY: "~4 chunks | Only what's active right now"
- Arrow: Information moves from working → long-term through encoding

**Narration:**
"It's important to distinguish: long-term memory has essentially unlimited capacity. You can learn thousands of facts, skills, experiences. The bottleneck is working memory—the narrow gateway through which everything must pass to enter long-term memory. You can only think about four things at once. To get information into long-term memory, it must first pass through this tiny workspace."

### FRAME 9: Takeaway 1 (2:06-2:19, 13 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Large number "1" in gold
- Text: "Break information into chunks of 3-4 items"

**Narration:**
"So here's what to do. First, break information into chunks of three to four items. When giving instructions, don't list seven steps—group them into three or four stages. When presenting, limit each slide to three to four key points. Work with working memory limits, not against them."

### FRAME 10: Takeaway 2 (2:19-2:32, 13 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Large number "2" in gold
- Text: "When overwhelmed, externalize: write it down"

**Narration:**
"Second, when you're overwhelmed, externalize your working memory. Write things down. You're not just creating a record—you're freeing up mental slots. Once something is written, you don't have to hold it in working memory anymore. Writing, diagrams, lists—these are tools for extending your four-slot limit."

---

### FRAME 11: Takeaway 3 & Close (2:32-2:50, 18 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Large number "3" in gold
- Text: "Design for limited working memory"
- Applications: presentations, instructions, interfaces, teaching
- Bottom text: "Miller (1956) & Cowan (2001) | Working Memory Limits"

**Narration:**
"Third, design for limited working memory. If you're teaching, don't introduce five new concepts at once—introduce two or three, let students chunk them, then add more. If you're designing an interface, don't require users to remember six pieces of information—design so they only need to hold two or three. Working memory is small. Respecting that limit makes everything clearer."
