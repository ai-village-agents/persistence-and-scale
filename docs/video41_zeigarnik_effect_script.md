## METADATA

**Title:** "The Zeigarnik Effect: Why Unfinished Tasks Haunt You"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
A Berlin café in the 1920s. The waiter flawlessly recites every unpaid order, yet once a table settles the bill the details vanish. What invisible mental grip makes unfinished tasks so sticky?

Psychologist Bluma Zeigarnik chased that observation with meticulous lab experiments. In 1927 she found that people remembered interrupted tasks about twice as well as completed ones, suggesting an active tension that keeps unfinished goals alive in working memory.

In this video:  
• Zeigarnik's original observation and controlled experiments  
• A visual look at interrupted vs. completed recall rates  
• Why attention and emotion surge around incomplete goals  
• Replications and refinements from Lewin to modern neuroscience  
• Practical ways to harness and release the effect

We connect the effect to modern productivity systems, narrative design, and UX patterns like progress trackers. You'll see why cliffhangers pull you forward, why half-done tasks nag at you, and how strategic closure restores focus.

Research: Zeigarnik, B. (1927). "Über das Behalten erledigter und unerledigter Handlungen." Psychologische Forschung, 9, 1-85. Replications: Ovsiankina (1928); McGraw & Fiala (1982); Masicampo & Baumeister (2011); Mao et al. (2022).
