## Research Authority

- One of the most cited theories in social psychology (10,000+ citations)
- 65+ years of replication across domains: beliefs, attitudes, behavior, decision-making
- Explains rationalization, belief persistence, buyer's remorse, effort justification

## Core Concept
**Cognitive Dissonance:** When you hold two conflicting beliefs, or when your behavior contradicts your beliefs, you experience psychological discomfort (dissonance). To reduce this discomfort, you change one of the beliefs, add new beliefs to justify the conflict, or reduce the importance of the conflict. Explains why smokers rationalize ("my grandfather smoked and lived to 90"), why people justify bad purchases, and why effort increases perceived value.

## Section 1: The Classic Study (0:15-0:55)
**Visual 1 (Frame 1):** Title card
- Large text: "COGNITIVE DISSONANCE"
- Subtext: "Festinger, 1957"

**Visual 2 (Frame 2):** Festinger & Carlsmith 1959 study design
- Title: "The $1 vs. $20 Experiment"
- Three groups:
  * All participants: "Complete boring task (turn pegs for 1 hour)"
  * Group A: "Paid $1 to tell next person task was interesting"
  * Group B: "Paid $20 to tell next person task was interesting"
  * Group C: "Control - not asked to lie"
- Question: "How interesting did you find the task?"

**Visual 3 (Frame 3):** Results - the dissonance effect
- Bar chart: "How Interesting Was the Task?"
- Three bars:
  * **$1 group**: High rating (electric blue, tallest) - "Actually found it interesting"
  * **$20 group**: Low rating (gray) - "Still found it boring"
  * **Control group**: Low rating (gray) - "Found it boring"
- Label: "$1 group changed their belief to justify their behavior"

**Narration:**
"In 1959, Festinger and colleague James Carlsmith ran a brilliant study. Participants did a boring task—turning pegs on a board for an hour. Then they were asked to lie to the next participant, saying the task was interesting. Some were paid $20 to lie. Others were paid just $1. Later, everyone rated how interesting the task actually was. The $20 group said it was boring—they had enough money to justify the lie. But the $1 group said the task was actually interesting. They changed their belief to match their behavior. One dollar wasn't enough to justify lying, so they convinced themselves they weren't lying. That's cognitive dissonance."

## Section 2: How Dissonance Reduction Works (0:55-1:35)
**Visual 4 (Frame 4):** Three paths to reduce dissonance
- Title: "REDUCING DISSONANCE"
- Center: "Conflicting beliefs or belief vs. behavior" (white box with tension lines)
- Three resolution paths radiating outward:
  1. **Change one belief** (electric blue): "I'll quit smoking"
  2. **Add justifying belief** (white): "My grandfather smoked and lived to 90, I'm probably fine"
  3. **Reduce importance** (gray): "Everyone dies of something anyway"

**Narration:**
"When you experience dissonance, you have three ways to reduce it. First, change one of the conflicting elements. If smoking conflicts with health, quit smoking. That's the healthiest option, but often the hardest. Second, add new beliefs that justify the conflict. 'My grandfather smoked and lived to 90, so I'm probably fine.' You haven't quit, but you've reduced the discomfort. Third, reduce the importance of the conflict. 'Everyone dies of something anyway, health isn't everything.' Your behavior stays the same, but the dissonance decreases."

**Visual 5 (Frame 5):** Effort justification example
- Title: "EFFORT JUSTIFICATION"
- Two scenarios:
  * **Easy initiation**: "Join group with no effort" → "Group seems okay" (medium rating)
  * **Difficult initiation**: "Join group after hazing/hard work" → "Group is amazing!" (high rating, electric blue)
- Bottom: "High effort → high value (to justify the effort)"

**Narration:**
"Cognitive dissonance explains effort justification. If you join a group easily, you rate it as fine. But if you went through a difficult initiation—hazing, hard work, sacrifice—you rate the group as amazing. Why? Because you need to justify the effort. 'I worked so hard to get in, so this group must be special.' The harder you work for something, the more you convince yourself it's valuable. That's dissonance reduction."

## Section 3: Real-World Applications (1:35-2:10)
**Visual 6 (Frame 6):** Domains where dissonance appears
- Title: "WHERE DISSONANCE SHOWS UP"
- Four examples:
  1. **Buyer's Remorse:** Justify expensive purchase after the fact
  2. **Relationship Commitment:** Justify staying in flawed relationship ("I've invested 5 years...")
  3. **Political Beliefs:** Ignore contradictory evidence to maintain worldview
  4. **Health Behaviors:** Rationalize unhealthy choices ("I'll start tomorrow")

**Narration:**
"Dissonance is everywhere. Buyer's remorse: you overpaid for something, so you convince yourself it's higher quality than it is—justifying the expense. Relationships: you stay in a flawed relationship because leaving would mean admitting the last five years were a mistake—that's sunk-cost dissonance. Politics: when evidence contradicts your worldview, you ignore or reinterpret it, because changing your entire belief system creates massive dissonance. Health: you skip the gym and rationalize—'I'll start tomorrow, and besides, rest is important.' Dissonance reduction is constant mental negotiation."

## Section 4: Practical Framework (2:10-2:35)
**Visual 7 (Frame 7):** Using dissonance awareness
- Title: "WORK WITH DISSONANCE"
- Three-step action framework:
  1. **NOTICE** when you're rationalizing (adding justifications post-hoc)
  2. **ASK** "Am I changing my belief to match my behavior, or vice versa?"
  3. **CHOOSE** dissonance reduction path consciously (don't let autopilot decide)

**Narration:**
"You can't eliminate dissonance, but you can manage it consciously. First, notice when you're rationalizing. If you're suddenly generating reasons why something you did was actually fine, that's dissonance reduction in action. Second, ask which direction you're moving—are you changing your belief to match your behavior, or changing your behavior to match your belief? Third, choose your path consciously. If you're rationalizing smoking, acknowledge you're reducing dissonance by adding justifications. That awareness alone can shift your choice."
