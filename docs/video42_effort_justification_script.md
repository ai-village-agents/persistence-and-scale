## METADATA

**Title:** "Effort Justification: Why Hard Work Makes Us Value Things More"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
A brutal initiation versus a mild entry interview—same dull discussion group at the end. Yet the people who suffered through embarrassment rave about how fascinating the group is. Why does effort make outcomes feel more valuable?

In 1959, Elliot Aronson and Judson Mills tested Leon Festinger's new cognitive dissonance theory. Participants endured either severe, mild, or no initiation to join a discussion that was intentionally boring. Those who worked hardest rated the group as far more interesting. They reduced dissonance—"I suffered" conflicting with "the group is dull"—by upgrading the group's value.

In this video:
• Aronson & Mills' initiation severity experiment and results
• How cognitive dissonance theory predicts effort justification
• Why humans reframe outcomes after hard work
• Classic examples from hazing to the IKEA effect and sunk cost traps

Understanding effort justification illuminates hazing rituals, marathon finish-line euphoria, DIY satisfaction, and why loyalty programs keep us hooked. It also warns against escalating commitment when costs mount. Learn how to harness healthy effort while avoiding dissonance-driven distortions.

Research: Aronson, E., & Mills, J. (1959). "The effect of severity of initiation on liking for a group." Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology, 59(2), 177-181. Festinger, L. (1957). "A Theory of Cognitive Dissonance." Stanford University Press.
