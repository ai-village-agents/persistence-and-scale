## METADATA

**Title:** "Why You Can't Stop After You've Already Started"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
You've invested time, money, or effort into something that isn't working. The rational choice is to cut your losses. But you keep going anyway, because you've "already come this far."

In 1985, psychologists Hal Arkes and Catherine Blumer demonstrated this powerful bias in a series of experiments. In one study, people who paid more for a theater season ticket were more likely to attend performances—even bad ones—just because they'd paid. Past costs that can't be recovered were irrationally influencing future decisions.

In this video:
• Arkes & Blumer's theater ticket experiments
• Why past costs shouldn't influence future decisions
• How we irrationally escalate commitment
• Why emotional investment compounds the fallacy

This fallacy drives everything from bad relationships to failed business ventures. We throw good money after bad, good time after wasted time, because we can't accept that our past investment is gone. Understanding the sunk cost fallacy is essential for rational decision-making.

Research: Arkes, H. R., & Blumer, C. (1985). "The psychology of sunk cost." Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes, 35(1), 124-140.

### FRAME 3: The Irrational Logic (0:26-0:42, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Timeline showing:
  - PAST (left side, grayed out): "$15 paid | Can't get it back"
  - PRESENT (center, bright): "Decision point"
  - FUTURE (right side): "Go or stay home?"
- Arrow showing past cost incorrectly influencing present decision
- Label: "Past cost (sunk) → Should NOT affect → Future decision"

**Narration:**
"Here's what's irrational about this. The fifteen dollars is gone. It's sunk. Whether you attend the show or not, you can't get that money back. The rational question is: given where you are now, what's the best choice? But we don't think that way."

### FRAME 4: Escalation of Commitment (0:42-0:58, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Ascending staircase or bar chart showing increasing investment
- Each step labeled:
  - "Initial investment"
  - "More investment to justify first"
  - "Even more to justify both"
  - "Can't stop now..."
- Arrow pointing upward labeled "Escalation"

**Narration:**
"We escalate commitment. You invest a thousand dollars in a failing stock. Instead of cutting your losses, you invest another thousand to 'average down.' Then another. Each investment makes it harder to quit because now you've invested even more. The hole gets deeper."

### FRAME 6: The Emotional Component (1:16-1:32, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Brain with two competing signals:
  - Logic side: "Evaluate: future costs vs future benefits"
  - Emotion side: "But I've already invested so much! It can't be for nothing!"
- Emotion side visually larger/brighter
- Label: "Emotional investment compounds the fallacy"

**Narration:**
"The fallacy is partly emotional. Admitting that your past investment was wasted feels like admitting failure. It feels like loss. So we keep investing, hoping to transform that past waste into eventual success. We're trying to make it 'not be for nothing.' But this just creates more waste."

---

### FRAME 7: The Justification Trap (1:32-1:46, 14 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Speech bubbles with common justifications:
  - "But I've already come this far..."
  - "I can't let that time go to waste..."
  - "I've invested too much to quit now..."
- All connected to central text: "Justifying with past costs"

**Narration:**
"Listen to your own justifications. If you're saying 'I've already come this far' or 'I've invested too much to quit now,' that's sunk cost thinking. You're justifying a future decision with a past cost. That's the trap."

---

### FRAME 8: The Rational Alternative (1:46-2:02, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Two decision paths:
  - PATH A (crossed out): "Past investment → Should I continue?"
  - PATH B (highlighted): "Ignore sunk costs → Starting fresh today, what's best?"
- Large text: "The right question"

**Narration:**
"The rational approach is to ignore sunk costs completely. Ask yourself: if I were starting fresh today, knowing everything I know now, would I choose this? If the answer is no, then quit. It doesn't matter what you've already invested. That's gone regardless."

### FRAME 10: Takeaway 2 (2:15-2:28, 13 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Large number "2" in gold
- Text: "Ask: Would I start this today knowing what I know?"
- Visual: Reset button or fresh start symbol

**Narration:**
"Second, ask the reset question. Would I start this project today knowing what I know? Would I enter this relationship now? Would I make this investment fresh? If the answer is no, then the fact that you already started is irrelevant. Quit."

---

### FRAME 11: Takeaway 3 & Close (2:28-2:50, 22 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Large number "3" in gold
- Text: "Focus on future costs and benefits, not past"
- Visual: Forward-pointing arrows, future timeline highlighted
- Bottom text: "Arkes & Blumer (1985) | The Psychology of Sunk Cost"

**Narration:**
"Third, deliberately focus on the future. What will it cost to continue? What are the benefits if you continue versus if you quit? Ignore what you've already spent. It's psychologically hard to let go of that investment. But the investment is gone either way. The only question is: do you want to add more waste on top of it, or cut your losses now and move forward?"
