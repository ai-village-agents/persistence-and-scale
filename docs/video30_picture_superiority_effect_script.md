## METADATA

**Title:** "Why You Remember the Logo But Forget the Slogan"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
Show someone a company logo, they remember it for days. Tell them the slogan, they forget it in hours. Why are pictures so much more memorable than words?

In the 1970s, psychologist Allan Paivio demonstrated the "picture superiority effect": images are recalled with 65% accuracy after 72 hours, while words drop to just 10%. This massive difference led Paivio to develop dual coding theory—the idea that pictures are encoded both visually and verbally, while words are primarily encoded verbally.

In this video:
• Allan Paivio's picture superiority effect research
• Why images achieve 6x better recall than words
• Dual coding theory: visual and verbal encoding
• Applications in learning, communication, and memory

The picture superiority effect explains why infographics work, why visual notes outperform text, why presentations need images, and why "show don't tell" is powerful. Understanding this effect transforms how you communicate and learn.

Research: Paivio, A., Rogers, T. B., & Smythe, P. C. (1968). "Why are pictures easier to recall than words?" Psychonomic Science, 11(4), 137-138. | Paivio, A. (1971). "Imagery and verbal processes." New York: Holt, Rinehart, and Winston.

### FRAME 3: Dual Coding Theory (0:24-0:42, 18 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Brain with two encoding pathways:
  - Picture → "Visual code" + "Verbal code" (two pathways highlighted)
  - Word → "Verbal code only" (one pathway)
- Label: "Pictures get encoded twice, words get encoded once"

**Narration:**
"Paivio proposed dual coding theory to explain this. When you see a picture of an apple, your brain encodes it visually—the shape, the color. But you also encode it verbally—the word 'apple,' related concepts. Two codes, two retrieval paths. When you see the word 'apple,' you primarily encode it verbally. One code, one retrieval path. Pictures have redundancy built in."

---

### FRAME 4: Why Dual Coding Works (0:42-0:58, 16 seconds)
**Visual:**
- Retrieval comparison:
  - Picture memory: "Path 1 (visual) fails? Try Path 2 (verbal)" → Success
  - Word memory: "Path 1 (verbal) fails? No backup" → Failure
- Label: "Redundancy = robustness"

**Narration:**
"Dual coding creates robustness. If you forget the visual details of the picture, you can still retrieve it through the verbal code. If the verbal code fails, you have the visual code as backup. Words only have one path. If that path fails, the memory is gone. Pictures have built-in redundancy."
