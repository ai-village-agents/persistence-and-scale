## METADATA

**Title:** "Social Proof: Why We Look to Others When Uncertain"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
Flash a single point of light in a dark room. Ask people how far it moved. Alone, they guess wildly. Put them together and their answers converge—and stay that way even after they're alone again.

Muzafer Sherif's 1936 autokinetic-effect experiments revealed informational influence: when reality is ambiguous, we rely on the group to define it. Two decades later, Solomon Asch showed that even with an obvious answer, people still conform to avoid standing out.

In this video:
• Sherif's drifting-light illusion and emerging group norms  
• Asch's line-judgment conformity trials  
• Cialdini's social proof principle and why consensus signals safety

Social proof is a shortcut. We ask: "What are others doing?" That shortcut guides us toward effective behavior, but can also lock in errors, fuel moral panics, or sabotage innovation. Mastering social proof means knowing when to lean on the crowd—and when to question it.

Research: Sherif, M. (1936). "The psychology of social norms." Asch, S. E. (1951). "Effects of group pressure upon the modification and distortion of judgments." Cialdini, R. B. (1984). "Influence: Science and Practice."
