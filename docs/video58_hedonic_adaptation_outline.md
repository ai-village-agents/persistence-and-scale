## Research Authority
- Philip Brickman, Dan Coates, Ronnie Janoff-Bulman (1978): "Lottery winners and accident victims: Is happiness relative?" Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 36(8), 917-927.
- One of the most cited studies in positive psychology (3,000+ citations)
- 45+ years of replication and extension
- Foundation for understanding subjective well-being, hedonic treadmill, life satisfaction research

## Core Concept
**Hedonic Adaptation:** People return to a relatively stable baseline level of happiness after major positive or negative life events. Lottery winners experience a happiness spike that fades within months. Even paraplegics adapt and return closer to baseline. Explains why material purchases don't create lasting happiness and why the "hedonic treadmill" keeps people chasing the next upgrade.

**Narration:**

He asked everyone: how happy are you right now, on a 1-to-5 scale? Lottery winners averaged 4.0. Control group: 3.8. Accident victims: 3.5. The gaps were smaller than anyone predicted. Winners weren't as happy as you'd expect. Victims weren't as devastated. Both had adapted toward baseline. That's Hedonic Adaptation."

## Section 2: Why Adaptation Happens (0:55-1:35)
**Visual 4 (Frame 4):** Adaptation mechanism diagram
- Title: "HOW ADAPTATION WORKS"
- Timeline showing three phases:
  1. **Baseline** (pre-event, white line)
  2. **Event occurs** (spike up for positive, down for negative, electric blue arrow)
  3. **Adaptation process** (gradual return to baseline over 6-18 months)
- Mechanisms listed:
  * "Shifting standards (new normal)"
  * "Social comparison (reset reference group)"
  * "Habituation (novelty wears off)"

**Narration:**
"Here's how it works. You have a happiness baseline—your default emotional set point. A major event happens: promotion, breakup, new car, injury. Your happiness spikes or drops. Then adaptation kicks in. First, your standards shift. The new car becomes your new normal. What was luxury is now baseline. Second, social comparison resets. You start comparing yourself to people with similar or better cars. Third, habituation. The novelty wears off—you stop noticing what you once celebrated. Over 6 to 18 months, you return to baseline. The event that seemed life-changing is now just your life."

## Section 3: The Hedonic Treadmill (1:35-2:10)
**Visual 5 (Frame 5):** Hedonic treadmill metaphor
- Title: "THE HEDONIC TREADMILL"
- Visual: Figure running on treadmill, chasing carrot labeled "Lasting Happiness"
- Milestones passing by: "New phone", "Promotion", "Bigger house", "Luxury car"
- Figure stays in same position relative to happiness baseline
- Bottom text: "More stuff ≠ More lasting happiness"

**Narration:**
"This creates the hedonic treadmill. You buy a new phone, it makes you happy for a week, then you adapt. So you chase the next upgrade. You get a promotion, feel great for a month, then it's normal. So you chase the next title. You buy a bigger house, love it initially, then adapt and want even bigger. You're running on a treadmill—consuming and achieving more, but not getting happier. Because every gain becomes the new baseline, and you adapt."

**Visual 6 (Frame 6):** What doesn't adapt
- Title: "WHAT RESISTS ADAPTATION"
- Three categories with icons:
  1. **Experiences over possessions** (memories don't habituate as fast)
  2. **Social connections** (relationships provide ongoing novelty)
  3. **Acts of kindness/gratitude** (other-focused, not self-focused)
- Bottom: "Variability and meaning resist adaptation"

**Narration:**
"But not everything adapts equally. Research shows experiences resist adaptation better than possessions—memories don't habituate as quickly as objects. Social connections resist adaptation—relationships provide ongoing novelty and meaning. Acts of kindness and gratitude resist adaptation because they're other-focused, not self-focused. Variability and meaning are the antidotes to the treadmill."

## Section 4: Practical Framework (2:10-2:40)
**Visual 7 (Frame 7):** Using adaptation awareness
- Title: "WORK WITH HEDONIC ADAPTATION"
- Three-step framework:
  1. **NOTICE** the treadmill → "Will this make me happy in 6 months?"
  2. **INVEST** in experiences, relationships, growth (not just possessions)
  3. **PRACTICE** gratitude → slow adaptation by actively noticing what you have

**Narration:**
"Here's how to work with adaptation, not against it. First, notice the treadmill before you get on it. Before a big purchase, ask: will this make me happy in six months, or will I adapt? Second, invest in what resists adaptation—experiences, relationships, personal growth, contribution. These provide lasting satisfaction. Third, practice gratitude. Actively notice what you have. This slows adaptation by reintroducing novelty to what you've taken for granted. You can't stop adaptation, but you can stop chasing upgrades that won't help."

**Narration:**
"You adapt to almost everything—good and bad. That's resilience, but it's also a warning. Stop chasing upgrades that won't last. Start chasing meaning, connection, and growth. Those resist the treadmill."
