## METADATA

**Title:** "Hyperbolic Discounting: Why We Choose Now Over Later"

**Description (6-section format, ~1,100 chars):**
You plan to save next paycheck, yet when it arrives the money disappears. Hyperbolic discounting explains why immediate rewards feel irresistible even when waiting yields more.

In 1975 George Ainslie showed that people discount the future with a curved, time-inconsistent function. Richard Thaler in 1981 confirmed the pattern in real financial choices, laying groundwork for modern behavioral economics.

In this video:  
• The classic preference reversal between now-versus-later choices  
• Why hyperbolic curves beat exponential models at predicting behavior  
• Practical tactics for fighting present bias in money, health, and productivity

You will see how preference curves bend, why quick gratification hijacks motivation, and how commitment devices rescue long-term goals. Replication after replication—through the 1990s, 2000s, and 2010s—keeps sharpening the model.

Research: Ainslie, G. (1975). "Specious reward: A behavioral theory of impulsiveness and impulse control." Psychological Bulletin, 82(4), 463-496. Thaler, R. (1981). "Some empirical evidence on dynamic inconsistency." Economics Letters, 8(3), 201-207.
