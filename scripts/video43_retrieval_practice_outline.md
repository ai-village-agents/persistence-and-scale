## Research Authority

**Primary Researchers:** Henry Roediger III & Jeffrey Karpicke (2006)
**Core Finding:** Testing beats restudying even when both take same time; after 1 week, testing group retains 50% more than study group
**Replication Status:** 15+ years intensive research, builds on 100+ years of testing effect research (Gates 1917, Spitzer 1939)
**Key Mechanism:** Retrieval practice strengthens memory traces more than re-exposure

### Frame 2: The Study (0:20-0:52, 32s)
**Visual:** Roediger & Karpicke 2006 experimental design
- All participants read prose passages
- Condition 1: Study 4 times (SSSS)
- Condition 2: Study 3 times, test 1 time (SSST)
- Condition 3: Study 1 time, test 3 times (STTT)
- Results graph: 5 minutes later vs 1 week later
  - 5 min: SSSS = 81%, STTT = 75% (study advantage short-term)
  - 1 week: SSSS = 40%, STTT = 56% (testing advantage long-term, +40%)
**Audio:** "Roediger and Karpicke gave people prose passages to learn. Some studied repeatedly. Others studied once, then tested themselves. Five minutes later, the study group remembered more—81% versus 75%. But one week later, the pattern reversed. The study group dropped to 40%. The testing group retained 56%—a 40% advantage. Testing creates durable learning. Repeated studying creates the illusion of learning."

### Frame 3: Why It Happens - Retrieval as Memory Modifier (0:52-1:24, 32s)
**Visual:** Memory trace visualization
- Studying: Strengthens input pathway (modest thickening)
- Retrieval: Strengthens output pathway + modifies memory structure (major thickening, branching connections)
- Key label: "Retrieval = active reconstruction, not playback"
- Comparison: Passive input vs Active reconstruction
**Audio:** "Retrieval practice works because memory isn't playback—it's reconstruction. When you study, you strengthen the input pathway. When you retrieve, you actively rebuild the memory from cues. That reconstruction process strengthens the memory trace more than re-exposure does. It also reveals gaps in your knowledge, forcing deeper processing. Retrieval is effortful, and that effort is what builds durable memory."

### Frame 4: Retrieval Practice vs Mere Testing (1:24-1:56, 32s)
**Visual:** Comparison chart showing effective vs ineffective testing
- Effective (green): No-feedback quizzing, flashcards (fill-in), free recall, explain-aloud
- Ineffective (yellow): Multiple choice with answers visible, recognition with hints, passive review
- Key principle: Must actively generate answer from memory, not just recognize
**Audio:** "But not all testing works. Effective retrieval practice requires generating the answer from memory. Flashcards where you recall the answer work. Multiple choice where you recognize among options? Weak benefit. Free recall—writing everything you remember—is powerful. Explaining concepts aloud from memory works. The key is active generation without cues. Recognition feels like retrieval, but it's too easy—it doesn't force the reconstruction that strengthens memory."

### Frame 5: Practical Implementation (1:56-2:26, 30s)
**Visual:** Study transformation guide
- OLD: Highlight → re-read → summarize notes (passive)
- NEW: Read → close book → write what you remember → check gaps → test again (active retrieval)
- Schedule: Immediate retrieval (after studying), spaced retrieval (1 day, 1 week, 1 month)
- Combination: Retrieval practice + spacing effect = optimal learning
**Audio:** "Transform your study method. Instead of highlighting and re-reading, read once, close the book, and write everything you remember. Check your gaps, then test again. Schedule retrieval: immediately after studying, one day later, one week later. Combine retrieval practice with spacing for maximum effect. Flashcards work if you generate answers without looking. Practice tests work if you don't peek. The difficulty of retrieval is the point—that's what creates learning."

### Frame 6: Actionable Takeaways (2:26-2:50, 24s)
**Visual:** Three-step framework with icons:
1. 🔍 "NOTICE" - Eye icon with study vs test distinction
2. ❓ "ASK" - Question mark with "Can I recall this?"
3. 🧠 "REMEMBER" - Brain with active retrieval
**Audio:** "Here's how to use it. One: Notice when you're reviewing passively. Re-reading feels productive but builds weak memory. Two: Test yourself immediately. After every study session, close your materials and recall everything you can. Three: Remember that forgetting is part of the process. Successful retrieval after difficulty builds stronger memory than easy recognition."
