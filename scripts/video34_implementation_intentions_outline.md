## Research Authority

**Primary Researcher:** Peter Gollwitzer (1999+)
**Key Finding:** "If-then" plans increase goal achievement by 2-3x compared to general intentions
**Replication Status:** 25+ years, meta-analysis of 94 studies (Gollwitzer & Sheeran 2006)
**Effect Size:** Medium to large (d = 0.65), robust across domains

### Frame 2: The Research (0:20-0:50, 30s)
**Visual:** Meta-analysis visualization
- Central node: "94 studies, 8,000+ participants"
- Branching categories: Exercise, Eating, Studying, Environmental behavior
- Bar chart showing goal completion rates:
  - Goal intention only: 35-40%
  - Implementation intention: 70-80%
**Audio:** "Peter Gollwitzer discovered this in 1999. He asked: what makes people follow through? Meta-analysis of 94 studies with over 8,000 people found the answer. People who formed if-then plans—'If situation X, then I'll do behavior Y'—completed goals 2 to 3 times more often than people who just set general intentions. It works for exercise, eating, studying, even environmental behavior."

### Frame 3: Why It Works (0:50-1:22, 32s)
**Visual:** Brain automation process visualization
- Top: "Goal intention" → requires active deliberation (effort meter high)
- Bottom: "Implementation intention" → automated response (effort meter low)
- Show process: Situational cue detected → Automated action triggered
- Key labels: "Situation cueing", "Response automaticity"
**Audio:** "Implementation intentions work through automation. A goal intention requires constant deliberation—you have to remember, decide when, overcome obstacles. An if-then plan delegates that to your environment. When the situation arrives—'Monday at 7am'—it automatically cues the behavior. You're not deciding whether to exercise; you're executing a pre-programmed response. It's situation-action binding."

### Frame 4: The Formula (1:22-1:52, 30s)
**Visual:** Implementation intention template with examples
- Template structure: "IF [SPECIFIC SITUATION] → THEN [SPECIFIC BEHAVIOR]"
- Three examples transform from vague to specific:
  1. "Eat healthy" → "IF lunch break, THEN eat salad I packed"
  2. "Study more" → "IF home by 6pm, THEN study at desk for 30min"
  3. "Reduce screen time" → "IF after dinner, THEN phone in drawer"
**Audio:** "The formula is simple: If specific situation, then specific behavior. Not 'I'll eat better'—'If it's lunch break, then I'll eat the salad I packed.' Not 'I'll study more'—'If I get home by 6pm, then I'll study at my desk for 30 minutes.' Specificity matters. Vague cues don't trigger automatic responses. Concrete situations do."

### Frame 5: Obstacles & Solutions (1:52-2:20, 28s)
**Visual:** Common obstacles as barriers with if-then solutions breaking through
- Obstacle 1: "Forget" → Solution: "IF phone alarm, THEN act"
- Obstacle 2: "Competing priority" → Solution: "IF X interrupts, THEN reschedule to Y"
- Obstacle 3: "Lost motivation" → Solution: "IF feel unmotivated, THEN do 5-minute version"
**Audio:** "But what about obstacles? Gollwitzer tested that too. Add obstacle if-thens: 'If I forget, then my phone alarm will remind me.' 'If work runs late, then I'll exercise at 8am Tuesday instead.' 'If I feel unmotivated, then I'll do the 5-minute version.' Pre-planning obstacle responses doubles effectiveness again. You're not fighting willpower; you're building a decision tree."

### Frame 6: Actionable Takeaways (2:20-2:45, 25s)
**Visual:** Three-step framework with icons:
1. 🔍 "NOTICE" - Eye icon with vague vs specific comparison
2. ❓ "ASK" - Question mark with "when/where?" emphasis
3. 🧠 "REMEMBER" - Brain with if-then pathway
**Audio:** "Here's how to use it. One: Notice when you set vague goals. 'I'll do better' doesn't work. Two: Ask when and where specifically. Turn intentions into if-then plans with concrete situational cues. Three: Remember to plan obstacles too. If-then plans aren't just for starting—they're for persisting through friction."
