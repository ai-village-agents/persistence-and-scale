## Research Authority

**Primary Researchers:** George Ainslie (1975), David Laibson (1997)
**Core Finding:** Present rewards disproportionately valued over future rewards; preference reversal when both options are delayed
**Key Example:** Prefer $100 now over $110 tomorrow, but prefer $110 in 31 days over $100 in 30 days
**Replication Status:** 50+ years, foundational to behavioral economics, confirmed across species and cultures

---

Now most people pick $110. It's the same one-day wait, the same $10 difference—but your preference reverses. Welcome to hyperbolic discounting."

**Modality:** Choice scenarios shown visually with simple icons ($ and calendar), audio explains paradox.

### Frame 2: The Study (0:22-0:55, 33s)
**Visual:** Graph showing discount curves
- X-axis: Time delay (0 days, 1 day, 7 days, 30 days, 90 days, 1 year)
- Y-axis: Subjective value (% of actual value)
- Two curves: 
  1. Exponential (consistent, gradual decline) - RATIONAL
  2. Hyperbolic (steep near-term drop, shallow long-term) - ACTUAL HUMAN BEHAVIOR
- Highlight where curves diverge at near-term decisions
**Audio:** "George Ainslie discovered this in 1975. We don't discount the future consistently. Rational discounting would be exponential—every week matters the same. Human discounting is hyperbolic—the present is massively overweighted, but once everything's in the future, patience returns. That's why a one-day delay matters enormously when it's today vs. tomorrow, but barely matters when it's day 30 vs. 31."

### Frame 3: Why It Happens (0:55-1:28, 33s)
**Visual:** Brain with competing systems
- Limbic system (emotional, immediate) lights up for "NOW" rewards
- Prefrontal cortex (planning, rational) handles "LATER" rewards
- When reward is immediate: Limbic dominates (bright glow)
- When both rewards are future: Prefrontal evaluates rationally (balanced glow)
**Audio:** "Your brain has competing valuation systems. The limbic system screams for immediate rewards—it evolved for environments where tomorrow was uncertain. The prefrontal cortex plans rationally for the future. When a reward is available now, limbic activation spikes and overwhelms deliberation. When both options are in the future, the limbic system stays quiet and your prefrontal cortex compares rationally."

### Frame 4: Real-World Consequences (1:28-2:00, 32s)
**Visual:** Four examples in quadrant layout:
1. "Save tomorrow, spend today" (retirement procrastination)
2. "Study later, watch now" (deadline cramming)
3. "Exercise Monday, relax today" (gym procrastination)
4. "Quit next week, smoke now" (addiction cycles)
**Audio:** "This explains procrastination, undersaving, deadline panic, and relapse. Your future self always prefers patience—exercise tomorrow, save next month, quit next week. But when tomorrow becomes today, hyperbolic discounting kicks in again. You're not weak-willed; you're mathematically inconsistent. The person deciding 'tomorrow' isn't the person executing 'today.'"

### Frame 5: Commitment Devices (2:00-2:30, 30s)
**Visual:** Three commitment strategies as tools:
1. "Remove NOW option" - Lock savings, block distractions
2. "Increase LATER salience" - Visual goals, pre-commitment
3. "Automate decision" - Default contributions, scheduled workouts
- Each tool shown as barrier between "NOW temptation" and action
**Audio:** "The solution is commitment devices. Remove the now option: lock your savings so you can't withdraw, block tempting websites during work. Increase later salience: make future consequences vivid with photos, charts, pre-commitments. Best: automate decisions before hyperbolic discounting activates—schedule workouts, default retirement contributions. Decide when you're rational; execute automatically."

### Frame 6: Actionable Takeaways (2:30-2:55, 25s)
**Visual:** Three-step framework with icons:
1. 🔍 "NOTICE" - Eye icon with "now vs. later" comparison
2. ❓ "ASK" - Question mark with reversed timeline
3. 🧠 "REMEMBER" - Brain with lock/commitment icon
**Audio:** "Here's how to use it. One: Notice when immediate options feel irrationally attractive. Ask yourself: would I choose this if both options were next month? Two: Use time-reversal—if you prefer patience when it's future-future, lock that choice in now. Three: Remember that your future self has the same bias. Don't rely on willpower. Pre-commit while you're still rational."
