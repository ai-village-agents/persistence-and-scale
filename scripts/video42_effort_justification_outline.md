## Research Authority

**Primary Researchers:** Leon Festinger (1959) - Cognitive Dissonance Theory; Elliot Aronson & Judson Mills (1959) - Severity of Initiation Study
**Core Finding:** Difficult initiation increases group attraction; effort creates perceived value independent of objective worth
**Replication Status:** 65+ years, robust across domains (Alessandri et al. 2008 confirmation with IKEA effect, Cooper 1980 review)
**Key Study:** Severe initiation → group rated 8.2/10; Mild initiation → group rated 6.3/10 (same boring discussion)

### Frame 2: The Study (0:20-0:52, 32s)
**Visual:** Aronson & Mills experimental design
- Female students told they're joining "sex discussion group"
- Condition 1: Severe initiation (read embarrassing sexual passages aloud to male experimenter)
- Condition 2: Mild initiation (read boring non-sexual words)
- Condition 3: No initiation (control)
- All listen to same deliberately boring group discussion (contraception in lower animals)
- Results: Severe 8.2/10, Mild 6.3/10, Control 6.1/10
**Audio:** "In 1959, Aronson and Mills recruited women for a sex discussion group. Some went through severe initiation—reading embarrassing sexual passages aloud. Others had mild initiation or none. Everyone then listened to the same boring discussion. The severe initiation group rated it significantly more interesting and valuable than the others. They convinced themselves the boring discussion was worthwhile because they'd worked hard to get there."

### Frame 3: Why It Happens - Cognitive Dissonance (0:52-1:24, 32s)
**Visual:** Brain resolving dissonance
- Dissonance equation: "I worked hard" + "This is boring" = psychological tension
- Three resolution paths shown:
  1. "Reject effort" (difficult - past can't be changed)
  2. "Reject experience" (threatens ego)
  3. "Revalue experience" (easiest - make it seem valuable)
- Arrow showing path 3 as chosen resolution
**Audio:** "This is cognitive dissonance. You have two conflicting beliefs: 'I worked really hard for this' and 'This is boring.' That creates psychological tension. You can't undo the effort—it's in the past. Admitting you wasted effort threatens your self-image. The easiest resolution? Increase the value of the experience. Your brain retroactively decides it must have been worthwhile, or you wouldn't have worked so hard for it."

### Frame 4: Real-World Consequences (1:24-1:54, 30s)
**Visual:** Four domains as cards:
1. "Hazing in fraternities/military creates loyalty"
2. "Expensive products seem higher quality (regardless of actual quality)"
3. "IKEA effect: Self-assembled furniture valued 63% more"
4. "Difficult diets/programs create adherence through sunk effort"
**Audio:** "This explains hazing rituals—the harder the initiation, the stronger the group loyalty, even when the group is mediocre. It's why expensive products seem better: the price is effort, and effort creates perceived quality. The IKEA effect: people value furniture they assembled themselves 63% more than identical pre-assembled furniture. Difficult diet or fitness programs create adherence not because they work better, but because the effort demands justification."

### Frame 5: Recognition & Application (1:54-2:20, 26s)
**Visual:** Two perspectives - defensive vs strategic
- Defensive: Recognize when you're justifying past effort (sunk cost awareness)
- Strategic: Design onboarding with meaningful effort to increase engagement
- Balance scale: Too little effort = low commitment; Too much = dropout; Optimal = engagement
**Audio:** "Two ways to use this. Defensively: recognize when you're valuing something because of past effort, not actual worth. That's sunk cost thinking. Your effort doesn't make something valuable—it just makes you think it is. Strategically: meaningful onboarding effort increases engagement. Make users work slightly—tutorials, setup, customization—to increase perceived value. But too much effort causes dropout. Find the sweet spot."

### Frame 6: Actionable Takeaways (2:20-2:45, 25s)
**Visual:** Three-step framework with icons:
1. 🔍 "NOTICE" - Eye icon with effort-value distortion
2. ❓ "ASK" - Question mark with objective value check
3. 🧠 "REMEMBER" - Brain with effort ≠ worth
**Audio:** "Here's how to use it. One: Notice when you're defending a choice based on effort invested. 'I spent so much time on this' doesn't mean it's good. Two: Ask what objective value is, separate from your effort. Would you choose this if you found it today? Three: Remember that effort creates perceived value, not actual value. Don't let past effort dictate future decisions."
