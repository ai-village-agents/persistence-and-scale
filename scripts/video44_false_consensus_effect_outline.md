## Research Authority

**Primary Researchers:** Lee Ross, David Greene, Pamela House (1977)
**Core Finding:** People overestimate agreement with their views by 20-40 percentage points
**Replication Status:** 45+ years, robust across cultures, topics, and political orientations (Mullen et al. 1985 meta-analysis, Marks & Miller 1987)
**Classic Study:** "Eat at Joe's" sign experiment - estimates of agreement differed by 30+ percentage points

## Hook & Opening (0:00-0:18, 18s)

**Visual:** Survey question with estimate vs reality bars
**Audio:** "Quick question: What percentage of people share your political views? If you guessed 60%, the actual number is probably closer to 35%. We systematically overestimate how many people think like us by 20 to 40 percentage points. Lee Ross called this the false consensus effect—your brain assumes your views are normal."

**Modality:** Percentage bars shown visually (estimate vs reality), audio explains the gap.

### Frame 2: The Study (0:18-0:50, 32s)
**Visual:** Ross et al. "Eat at Joe's" study visualization
- College students asked: "Would you walk around campus wearing an 'Eat at Joe's' sign?"
- Group A: Said YES → estimated 62% of others would also say yes
- Group B: Said NO → estimated 67% of others would also say no
- Reality: 50/50 split
- Key: ~30 percentage point difference in estimates based solely on own choice
**Audio:** "In 1977, Lee Ross asked students if they'd wear a sandwich board sign saying 'Eat at Joe's' around campus. Some said yes, some said no. Then he asked them to estimate what percentage of others would agree with their choice. People who said yes estimated 62% would agree. People who said no estimated 67% would agree. Both groups thought their view was the majority. Reality was a 50-50 split."

### Frame 3: Why It Happens (0:50-1:22, 32s)
**Visual:** Brain with three cognitive mechanisms
1. "Selective exposure" - surround yourself with similar people (echo chamber icon)
2. "Availability heuristic" - your view is most accessible example (brain pathway)
3. "Motivated reasoning" - want to believe you're normal (comfort/validation icon)
**Audio:** "False consensus comes from three sources. First, selective exposure: you surround yourself with similar people, so your sample is biased. Your friends mostly agree with you. Second, availability: your own view is the most accessible example in memory, so it feels common. Third, motivated reasoning: believing you're in the majority is psychologically comforting. It validates your choices and reduces uncertainty."

### Frame 4: Consequences (1:22-1:52, 30s)
**Visual:** Four problematic domains as cards:
1. "Political polarization: Both sides think they're the majority"
2. "Risk assessment: Overestimate support for risky decisions"
3. "Pluralistic ignorance: Everyone thinks everyone else believes X"
4. "Social bubbles: Echo chambers reinforce false consensus"
**Audio:** "False consensus fuels political polarization. Both sides believe they're the silent majority, which breeds contempt for the 'minority' opposition. It distorts risk assessment—you think risky decisions have more support than they do. It creates pluralistic ignorance: everyone privately disagrees but thinks everyone else agrees. Social media echo chambers amplify this—your feed shows agreement, hiding the diversity of actual opinion."

### Frame 5: Breaking the Illusion (1:52-2:20, 28s)
**Visual:** Three correction strategies:
1. "Seek disconfirming views" - actively find disagreement (diverse sources icon)
2. "Base rate check" - look up actual opinion data (survey/poll icon)
3. "Steel man opposition" - genuinely understand other side (strong argument icon)
- Before/after: "Feel 70% agree" → "Discover 45% agree" → recalibrate
**Audio:** "Breaking false consensus requires active effort. One: Seek disconfirming views. Actively expose yourself to people who disagree. Two: Check base rates. Look up actual polling data—your gut feeling is probably wrong. Three: Steel man the opposition. Genuinely try to understand why smart people disagree with you. If you can't explain their view charitably, you're in a bubble."

### Frame 6: Actionable Takeaways (2:20-2:45, 25s)
**Visual:** Three-step framework with icons:
1. 🔍 "NOTICE" - Eye icon with agreement overestimation
2. ❓ "ASK" - Question mark with diverse sample
3. 🧠 "REMEMBER" - Brain with bubble awareness
**Audio:** "Here's how to use it. One: Notice when you assume your view is normal. Ask: am I overestimating agreement? Two: Test your assumptions. Find people who disagree and understand their reasoning. Check actual data. Three: Remember that your bubble isn't reality. The people who surround you aren't a representative sample—they're selected for similarity."
