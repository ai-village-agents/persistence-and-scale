## Research Authority

**Primary Researchers:** Slamecka & Graf (1978)
**Key Finding:** Self-generated information recalled 30-40% better than passively read information
**Replication Status:** 45+ years, widely replicated across age groups and materials
**Supporting Research:** Bertsch et al. (2007) confirmed across diverse learning contexts

### Frame 2: The Study (0:18-0:48, 30s)
**Visual:** Experimental design layout
- Top row: "Generate" condition shows partial words: "F__T" (foot), "D__K" (dark)
- Bottom row: "Read" condition shows complete words: "FOOT", "DARK"
- Clock showing delay
- Results: Bar chart showing 65% vs 45% recall
**Audio:** "In 1978, Slamecka and Graf showed people word pairs. One group generated: saw 'F-O-blank-T' and filled in 'foot.' The other just read 'foot' complete. Both groups studied the same amount of time. During recall, the generate group remembered 65% of words. The read group? 45%. Generating the answer yourself created a 40% retention advantage."

### Frame 3: Why It Works (0:48-1:20, 32s)
**Visual:** Brain processing visualization with effort indicators
- Passive reading: Shallow processing path (thin line), minimal effort meter
- Generation: Deep processing path (thick, branching lines), higher effort meter, multiple connections forming
- Key labeled: "Elaborative encoding" with network nodes lighting up
**Audio:** "Generation forces elaborative encoding. When you fill in the blank, you're not just receiving information—you're retrieving related knowledge, making predictions, confirming matches. That effort creates stronger, more connected memory traces. Passive reading skips that work. Your brain literally builds better pathways for information it had to work for."

### Frame 4: Real-World Proof (1:20-1:50, 30s)
**Visual:** Four application examples as quadrants:
1. "Flashcards: Fill-in > Multiple choice"
2. "Notes: Your words > Copy paste"
3. "Coding: Build it > Watch tutorial"
4. "Writing: Outline first > Template fill"
**Audio:** "This explains why blank flashcards beat multiple choice. Why taking notes in your own words beats copying slides. Why building a project teaches more than watching a tutorial. Why outlining before writing creates better essays than filling templates. The generation effect isn't about intelligence—it's about engaging retrieval and construction, not just recognition."

### Frame 5: The Limits (1:50-2:15, 25s)
**Visual:** Effectiveness chart showing diminishing returns
- X-axis: "Difficulty of generation task"
- Y-axis: "Memory benefit"
- Curve rises then falls
- Three zones marked: "Too easy" (low benefit), "Optimal" (peak), "Too hard" (frustration, low retention)
**Audio:** "But generation has limits. The task can't be too easy—if the blank is obvious, you're not really generating. And it can't be impossible—if you have no idea, you're just guessing, which doesn't help. The sweet spot is desirable difficulty: challenging enough to require effort, doable enough to succeed."

### Frame 6: Actionable Takeaways (2:15-2:45, 30s)
**Visual:** Three-step framework with icons:
1. 🔍 "NOTICE" - Eye icon with active/passive split
2. ❓ "ASK" - Question mark with blank space
3. 🧠 "REMEMBER" - Brain with construction tools icon
**Audio:** "Here's how to use it. One: Notice whether you're consuming or generating. Are you reading complete information or testing yourself? Two: Ask questions before answers. Create blanks in your notes. Use flashcards that require recall, not recognition. Three: Remember that harder feels better for learning. If studying feels too easy, add generation. Make yourself work for it."
