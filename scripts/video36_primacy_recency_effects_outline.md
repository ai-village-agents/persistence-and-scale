## Research Authority

**Primary Researcher:** Bennet Murdock Jr. (1962), building on Hermann Ebbinghaus
**Key Finding:** First and last items in a list recalled 2-3x better than middle items (serial position effect)
**Replication Status:** 60+ years, one of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology
**Meta-analysis:** Confirmed across age groups, list types, and presentation modalities

### Frame 2: The Research (0:20-0:50, 30s)
**Visual:** Classic serial position curve
- X-axis: Position in list (1-15)
- Y-axis: Recall probability (0-100%)
- Curve shows: High at position 1 (primacy), dips in middle, rises at position 15 (recency)
- Annotations: "70% recall" at first item, "30% recall" in middle, "65% recall" at last item
**Audio:** "In 1962, Bennet Murdock showed people lists of words and tested immediate recall. First items? Recalled 70% of the time—that's primacy. Last items? 65%—that's recency. Middle items? Just 30%. The serial position curve is one of the most replicated findings in psychology. Your memory favors the edges."

### Frame 3: Why It Happens (0:50-1:22, 32s)
**Visual:** Brain with two memory systems
- Primacy: Early items → rehearsal loop → long-term memory (stable storage)
- Recency: Recent items → working memory (temporary, still active)
- Middle items: Crowded out by new input, insufficient rehearsal (weak encoding)
**Audio:** "Primacy happens because early items get more rehearsal. When you hear the first word, your working memory is empty, so you repeat it mentally. Each new word gets less rehearsal because your cognitive load increases. First items transfer to long-term memory. Recency happens because last items are still in working memory—they haven't been displaced yet. Middle items get squeezed: insufficient rehearsal for long-term storage, already displaced from working memory."

### Frame 4: Real-World Applications (1:22-1:54, 32s)
**Visual:** Four practical examples as cards:
1. "Presentations: Strong opening + closing" (audience attention curve)
2. "Interviews: First impression + final question"
3. "Menus: Profitable items at top/bottom"
4. "Arguments: Lead and close with strongest points"
**Audio:** "This is everywhere. Presenters put key messages at the start and end because middle content gets forgotten. Interviewers know first impressions and final questions matter most. Restaurant menus place high-margin items at the top and bottom. Lawyers lead with their strongest point, bury weaker arguments in the middle, and close strong. If you want something remembered, don't bury it in the middle."

### Frame 5: Breaking the Pattern (1:54-2:24, 30s)
**Visual:** Strategies to overcome the dip
- Strategy 1: "Chunk the middle" (group items into clusters)
- Strategy 2: "Add distinctiveness" (make middle items stand out)
- Strategy 3: "Pause and review" (reset the serial position)
- Graph showing middle recall improving with each strategy
**Audio:** "But you can fight the curve. Chunk the middle—group items into meaningful clusters so each cluster gets primacy and recency. Add distinctiveness—make middle items unusual or surprising. Pause and review—create multiple short lists instead of one long list, giving each section its own edges. The pattern is powerful, but it's not absolute."

### Frame 6: Actionable Takeaways (2:24-2:50, 26s)
**Visual:** Three-step framework with icons:
1. 🔍 "NOTICE" - Eye icon with edge emphasis
2. ❓ "ASK" - Question mark with position awareness
3. 🧠 "REMEMBER" - Brain with strategic placement
**Audio:** "Here's how to use it. One: Notice that position matters. If you're communicating something important, don't put it in the middle. Two: Ask where your key points fall. Restructure so critical information hits the edges. Three: Remember that learning works this way too. Break long study sessions into shorter chunks so more material gets edge effects."
