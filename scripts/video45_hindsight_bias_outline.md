## Research Authority

**Primary Researcher:** Baruch Fischhoff (1975)
**Core Finding:** After learning outcome, people believe they "would have predicted it" - overestimate by 50%+ 
**Replication Status:** 50+ years, robust across domains and cultures (Roese & Vohs 2012 review, Christensen-Szalanski & Willham 1991 meta-analysis)
**Classic Example:** Nixon's China trip - after outcome, participants "remembered" predicting it even when their written predictions said opposite

### Frame 2: The Study (0:18-0:50, 32s)
**Visual:** Fischhoff's Nixon China trip study
- 1972: Nixon planning visit to China and Russia
- Participants asked to estimate probabilities of 15 possible outcomes (e.g., meeting with Mao, trade agreement)
- After trip: Participants asked what they originally predicted
- Results: Participants "remembered" predicting actual outcomes at 2x the rate they actually did
- Key: Written predictions vs remembered predictions showed 50%+ inflation
**Audio:** "In 1972, Baruch Fischhoff asked people to predict outcomes of Nixon's upcoming China trip. He recorded their predictions. After the trip, he asked what they'd predicted. People consistently remembered predicting the actual outcomes at twice the rate they actually had. If they'd assigned 30% probability to an outcome that happened, they remembered predicting 60%. Their brains rewrote history to make the outcome seem foreseeable."

### Frame 3: Why It Happens - Memory Reconstruction (0:50-1:22, 32s)
**Visual:** Brain timeline showing memory process
- Stage 1: "Make prediction" - uncertain future (wide possibility cone)
- Stage 2: "Learn outcome" - integrate new knowledge (outcome highlighted)
- Stage 3: "Recall prediction" - brain reconstructs from current knowledge (outcome feels inevitable)
- Key label: "Memory isn't retrieval—it's reconstruction"
**Audio:** "Hindsight bias happens because memory is reconstruction, not playback. When you learn the outcome, your brain integrates that knowledge into your memory of the past. When you later try to recall your original prediction, you're reconstructing it from your current state of knowledge. The outcome feels inevitable because you now know it happened. You can't mentally 'unknow' it to access your original uncertainty."

### Frame 4: Consequences (1:22-1:52, 30s)
**Visual:** Four problematic domains as cards:
1. "Medical malpractice: 'Should have seen it coming' (20/20 hindsight)"
2. "Historical analysis: Events seem inevitable in retrospect"
3. "Performance reviews: 'Should have known' evaluations"
4. "Learning impairment: Can't identify what was actually difficult to predict"
**Audio:** "Hindsight bias distorts judgment. Medical malpractice cases: juries see missed diagnoses as obvious in hindsight, when they were ambiguous in real time. Historical analysis: past events seem inevitable, when contemporaries saw genuine uncertainty. Performance reviews punish decisions that were reasonable with available information. Learning suffers: if you think you 'knew it,' you don't identify what actually made prediction difficult."

### Frame 5: Countermeasures (1:52-2:20, 28s)
**Visual:** Three correction strategies:
1. "Record predictions before outcomes" (written documentation icon)
2. "Consider alternative outcomes" (multiple paths visualization)
3. "Outcome independence: Judge decision quality by process, not result" (decision tree icon)
- Example: Good decision + bad outcome ≠ bad decision
**Audio:** "Fighting hindsight bias requires external records. One: Write down predictions before outcomes. You can't rewrite what's documented. Two: Consider alternative outcomes actively. What else could have happened with the same information? Three: Judge decisions independently of outcomes. A good decision with a bad outcome was still good. A bad decision with a good outcome was still bad. Separate process from result."

### Frame 6: Actionable Takeaways (2:20-2:45, 25s)
**Visual:** Three-step framework with icons:
1. 🔍 "NOTICE" - Eye icon with "I knew it" warning
2. ❓ "ASK" - Question mark with pre-outcome uncertainty
3. 🧠 "REMEMBER" - Brain with documentation
**Audio:** "Here's how to use it. One: Notice when you think 'I knew it.' Ask: did I actually predict this, or does it just feel obvious now? Two: Restore pre-outcome uncertainty. What did things look like before you knew the answer? Three: Document predictions. Keep decision journals. Record your reasoning before outcomes, so hindsight can't rewrite history."
