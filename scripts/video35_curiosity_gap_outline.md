## Research Authority

**Primary Researcher:** George Loewenstein (1994)
**Core Theory:** Information Gap Theory
**Key Finding:** Gap between what we know and want to know creates psychological tension that drives information-seeking
**Replication Status:** 30+ years, foundational to behavioral economics and marketing research

---

## Hook & Opening (0:00-0:18, 18s)

**Visual:** Quiz-style display showing 4 of 5 answers revealed
**Audio:** "Quick experiment: I'll tell you 4 out of 5 facts about a topic, then stop. Right now, you're experiencing discomfort—the curiosity gap. That tension between knowing something and knowing everything is more powerful than knowing nothing at all. Loewenstein proved it in 1994."

**Modality:** Visual shows partial information (4 filled boxes, 1 mystery box), audio explains the sensation.

### Frame 2: The Research (0:18-0:48, 30s)
**Visual:** Experimental design visualization
- Three conditions shown as columns:
  1. "0 of 5 facts" → low curiosity (interest meter: 30%)
  2. "3 of 5 facts" → peak curiosity (interest meter: 90%)
  3. "5 of 5 facts" → resolved (interest meter: 20%)
- Middle column glows brightest
- Arrow showing "Maximum gap = Maximum motivation"
**Audio:** "Loewenstein showed people trivia questions with varying amounts of information revealed. People who knew nothing were mildly curious. People who knew everything had no curiosity—it was resolved. But people who knew some information felt intense discomfort. The gap between partial knowledge and complete knowledge created psychological tension that drove information-seeking behavior."

### Frame 3: The Mechanism (0:48-1:18, 30s)
**Visual:** Brain with knowledge states
- State 1: "Ignorance" → low activation, comfort
- State 2: "Awareness of gap" → high activation, discomfort highlighted in red
- State 3: "Resolution" → activation drops, satisfaction
- Emphasis on State 2 creating action/motivation
**Audio:** "Here's why it works. Complete ignorance is comfortable—you don't know what you're missing. Awareness of a knowledge gap creates discomfort, an itch to resolve the inconsistency. Your brain flags unfinished information as a problem to solve. That tension motivates action. Once the gap closes, tension dissipates. The gap, not the information, drives behavior."

### Frame 4: Applications (1:18-1:48, 30s)
**Visual:** Four real-world examples as cards:
1. "Clickbait: 'Number 7 will shock you'" (partial reveal)
2. "TV cliffhangers: Episode ends mid-reveal"
3. "Mystery novels: Drop clues, withhold solution"
4. "Testimonials: 'I lost weight by...' [cut off]"
**Audio:** "This is everywhere. Clickbait headlines: 'Number 7 will shock you'—they create a gap. TV shows end on cliffhangers because unresolved tension keeps you watching. Mystery novels drop clues but withhold the solution until the end. Testimonials that start a story but pause mid-reveal. All exploit the curiosity gap."

### Frame 5: Ethical Use (1:48-2:15, 27s)
**Visual:** Comparison chart: Manipulation vs Engagement
- Left (red X): False gaps, bait-and-switch, no payoff
- Right (green ✓): Genuine questions, valuable answers, satisfying resolution
- Scale showing "trust" declining left, building right
**Audio:** "But there's an ethical line. Manipulative gaps create false tension with no payoff—clickbait that disappoints. Genuine gaps create real curiosity with valuable resolution. If you open a gap, close it with substance. False gaps erode trust. Real gaps build engagement. The difference is whether you respect the resolution or just exploit the itch."

### Frame 6: Actionable Takeaways (2:15-2:40, 25s)
**Visual:** Three-step framework with icons:
1. 🔍 "NOTICE" - Eye icon with gap visualization
2. ❓ "ASK" - Question mark with partial info
3. 🧠 "REMEMBER" - Brain with resolution path
**Audio:** "Here's how to use it. One: Notice when you feel compelled to click, watch, or read. Are you experiencing a curiosity gap? Two: Create gaps ethically—ask genuine questions, then deliver real answers. Start with mystery, end with clarity. Three: Remember that resolution matters. If you open a gap, close it with value. That's how curiosity becomes learning."
