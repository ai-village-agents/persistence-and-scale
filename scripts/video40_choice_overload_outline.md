## Research Authority

**Primary Researchers:** Sheena Iyengar & Mark Lepper (2000)
**Core Finding:** 24 jam varieties → 3% purchase rate; 6 varieties → 30% purchase rate (10x difference)
**Replication Status:** 25+ years, meta-analysis by Chernev, Böckenholt & Goodman (2015) - effect strongest with high task difficulty and low prior preference
**Key Theory:** Paradox of Choice (Barry Schwartz 2004 synthesis)

---

## Hook & Opening (0:00-0:20, 20s)

**Visual:** Two jam tasting booth setups side by side
**Audio:** "Two grocery stores set up jam tasting booths. Store A offers 24 varieties. Store B offers 6. More choice should mean more sales, right? Store A: 3% of tasters bought jam. Store B: 30% bought jam. Ten times more sales with fewer options. Welcome to choice overload."

**Modality:** Booth setups shown visually with purchase rate numbers, audio explains the paradox.

### Frame 2: The Study (0:20-0:52, 32s)
**Visual:** Iyengar & Lepper experimental design
- Condition 1: 24 jam varieties, attractive display
- Condition 2: 6 jam varieties, identical presentation
- Results table:
  - Attraction: 60% stopped (24 jams) vs 40% stopped (6 jams)
  - Purchase: 3% bought (24 jams) vs 30% bought (6 jams)
- Key insight: More choice attracts but paralyzes
**Audio:** "In 2000, Sheena Iyengar and Mark Lepper ran this study in a California grocery store. The 24-jam booth attracted more initial interest—60% of shoppers stopped to taste. But only 3% bought anything. The 6-jam booth attracted fewer browsers—40% stopped—but 30% bought. More variety increased browsing but killed decisions. Too much choice creates decision paralysis."

### Frame 3: Why It Happens (0:52-1:24, 32s)
**Visual:** Brain processing load visualization
- Small choice set: Comparison manageable (low cognitive load meter)
- Large choice set: Comparison overwhelming (high cognitive load meter)
- Three factors highlighted:
  1. "Comparison difficulty" - too many attributes to evaluate
  2. "Anticipated regret" - fear of missing the best option
  3. "Opportunity cost" - every choice means rejecting alternatives
**Audio:** "Choice overload happens through three mechanisms. First, comparison difficulty: evaluating 24 options exhausts your cognitive capacity. Second, anticipated regret: with so many options, you fear making the wrong choice. Third, opportunity cost: every jam you pick means rejecting 23 others. The mental cost of choosing outweighs the benefit of the choice itself."

### Frame 4: When It Happens (1:24-1:56, 32s)
**Visual:** Conditions chart showing when choice overload is strongest
- HIGH overload: Complex products (insurance, investments), unfamiliar domains, no clear preference
- LOW overload: Simple products (socks), familiar domains, clear goals
- Examples as cards:
  1. "401k plans: 10 options = 75% participation, 60 options = 60% participation"
  2. "Dating apps: More profiles = lower satisfaction"
  3. "Restaurant menus: 10 pages = decision anxiety"
**Audio:** "Choice overload is strongest when decisions are complex, you lack expertise, and preferences aren't clear. Retirement plan study: employers with 10 investment options had 75% employee participation. Employers with 60 options? 60% participation. More choice literally made people not participate at all. Dating apps, restaurant menus, insurance plans—complexity kills action."

### Frame 5: Solutions (1:56-2:26, 30s)
**Visual:** Three strategies as tools:
1. "Curate ruthlessly" - Limit options to 5-7 best
2. "Satisfice, don't maximize" - Good enough > perfect
3. "Embrace constraints" - Fewer choices = easier decisions
- Before/after comparison: 20 options → decision paralysis; 6 options → confident choice
**Audio:** "The fix is intentional constraint. One: Curate ruthlessly. If you're offering choices, limit to 5-7 best options. Two: Satisfice, don't maximize. Pick good enough instead of searching for perfect. Three: Embrace constraints. Fewer choices aren't limiting—they're liberating. Satisficers are happier than maximizers. Constraints reduce regret and increase satisfaction."

### Frame 6: Actionable Takeaways (2:26-2:50, 24s)
**Visual:** Three-step framework with icons:
1. 🔍 "NOTICE" - Eye icon with option overload
2. ❓ "ASK" - Question mark with "good enough?" threshold
3. 🧠 "REMEMBER" - Brain with constraint as freedom
**Audio:** "Here's how to use it. One: Notice when you're stuck choosing. Are too many options creating paralysis? Two: Ask what's good enough. Set your threshold before searching, then stop at the first option that meets it. Three: Remember that constraints reduce regret. Fewer options don't limit you—they free you to actually decide."
