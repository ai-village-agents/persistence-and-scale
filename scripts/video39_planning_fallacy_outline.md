## Research Authority

**Primary Researchers:** Daniel Kahneman & Amos Tversky (1979)
**Key Finding:** People systematically underestimate task completion time by 25-50%, even when aware of past delays
**Replication Status:** 45+ years, Nobel Prize work, replicated across domains
**Supporting Research:** Buehler, Griffin & Ross (1994) - shown to persist even with experience

---

## Hook & Opening (0:00-0:18, 18s)

**Visual:** Project timeline showing estimate vs. reality
**Audio:** "You think this task will take 2 hours. It takes 4. You're not bad at planning—you're experiencing the planning fallacy. Kahneman and Tversky found that people underestimate completion time by 25 to 50 percent, even when they know their own history of delays."

**Modality:** Timeline shown visually with gap between estimate and actual, audio explains the systematic bias.

### Frame 2: The Research (0:18-0:50, 32s)
**Visual:** Classic planning fallacy study visualization
- Students asked: "When will you finish your thesis?"
- Three estimates collected: Best case (30 days), Realistic (49 days), Worst case (67 days)
- Actual average completion: 56 days
- Comparison: Only 30% finished by their "realistic" estimate
**Audio:** "Kahneman and Tversky asked students to predict thesis completion time. Average realistic estimate: 49 days. Average actual completion: 56 days. Only 30% finished by their realistic estimate. Even the average worst-case scenario—67 days—was optimistic for 25% of students. People consistently underestimate, even when explicitly asked for realistic and pessimistic forecasts."

### Frame 3: Why It Happens (0:50-1:24, 34s)
**Visual:** Brain with competing perspectives
- Inside view: Your plan, ideal scenario, controlled factors (tunnel vision)
- Outside view: Past similar projects, base rates, typical obstacles (broader perspective)
- Planning process shows: Inside view dominates, outside view ignored
- Labels: "Optimism bias", "Singular focus on plan"
**Audio:** "The fallacy comes from inside-view thinking. You focus on your specific plan: the steps, your intentions, your effort. You ignore the outside view: how long similar tasks actually took, typical obstacles, historical base rates. You imagine the best path, not the typical path. Optimism bias amplifies it—you assume this time will be different, even when past projects always ran over."

### Frame 4: Real-World Consequences (1:24-1:56, 32s)
**Visual:** Four major domains as examples:
1. "Construction: Sydney Opera House—4 years → 14 years" (massive overrun)
2. "Software: 'It'll be done Friday' → Ships 3 Fridays later"
3. "Personal: 'Quick errand' → Lost afternoon"
4. "Writing: 'One hour to write' → Misses deadline"
**Audio:** "This isn't just personal—it's systemic. The Sydney Opera House was estimated at 4 years, took 14. Software projects routinely double their timelines. Your 'quick errand' becomes a lost afternoon. Writers miss deadlines, renovations blow budgets, meetings run over. The fallacy affects experts as much as novices. Experience doesn't fix it—everyone thinks this project is special."

### Frame 5: The Fix - Outside View (1:56-2:26, 30s)
**Visual:** Correction strategy as process:
1. "Ignore your plan temporarily" (set aside inside view)
2. "Find similar past projects" (reference class forecasting)
3. "Use actual average time" (base rate as starting point)
4. "Adjust only for major differences" (small corrections, not optimism)
- Formula: Base rate + buffer = realistic estimate
**Audio:** "The fix is outside-view forecasting. Step one: ignore your plan temporarily. Step two: find similar past projects—yours or others. Step three: use the actual average time as your baseline. Your last three similar tasks took 5, 6, and 7 hours? Start with 6. Step four: adjust only for major differences. Your plan isn't a major difference—everyone has a plan. Use historical averages, add a buffer."

### Frame 6: Actionable Takeaways (2:26-2:50, 24s)
**Visual:** Three-step framework with icons:
1. 🔍 "NOTICE" - Eye icon with inside vs outside view
2. ❓ "ASK" - Question mark with reference class
3. 🧠 "REMEMBER" - Brain with historical data
**Audio:** "Here's how to use it. One: Notice when you're estimating. Are you imagining your plan or consulting past reality? Two: Ask what happened last time. Find a reference class—similar tasks by you or others. Three: Remember that your plan feels special, but it's not. Start with base rates, multiply by 1.5, and you'll be closer to reality."
