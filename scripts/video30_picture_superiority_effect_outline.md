## Research Authority

**Primary Researcher:** Allan Paivio (1971, 1986)
**Core Theory:** Dual Coding Theory
**Key Finding:** Pictures recalled with 65% accuracy vs 10% for words after 72 hours
**Replication Status:** 50+ years, foundational to multimedia learning (Mayer 2001, Clark & Paivio 1991)
**Meta-analysis:** Nelson, Reed & Walling (1976) confirmed across 150+ studies

## Frame Breakdown

### Frame 1: Opening (0:00-0:20, 20s)
**Visual:** Dark navy background, split-screen layout
- Left: 4 famous logos (Apple, Nike, McDonald's, Target) fade in sequentially (0.5s each)
- Right: Corresponding slogans as white text (small, hard to read intentionally)
- After 3s, logos glow slightly, slogans fade to 20% opacity
**Audio:** [Hook text above]
**Key Principle:** Show don't tell - viewers experience the effect before learning about it

### Frame 2: The Study (0:20-0:45, 25s)
**Visual:** Simple experimental design visualization
- Split screen: Left = word list (10 common words), Right = picture list (10 simple icons)
- Clock icon showing "72 hours" between top and bottom sections
- Bottom: Empty slots with "?" marks
**Audio:** "In 1971, psychologist Allan Paivio showed people lists of common words. Some saw text: 'CAT, TREE, HOUSE.' Others saw simple drawings. After three days, people who saw pictures remembered 65% of them. People who saw words? Just 10%. Same information, six times the retention."

### Frame 3: Why It Works - Dual Coding (0:45-1:15, 30s)
**Visual:** Brain diagram (simplified, not anatomical)
- Two pathways light up: "Visual System" (image icon) and "Verbal System" (text icon)
- For picture: BOTH pathways light up and connect
- For word: ONLY verbal pathway lights up
- Connections between systems shown as glowing lines
**Audio:** "Paivio called this dual coding. When you see a picture, your brain encodes it twice: once as a visual memory, once as a verbal label. Two retrieval paths instead of one. When you see a word, you get one encoding. The picture has backup—the word doesn't."

### Frame 4: Real-World Proof (1:15-1:40, 25s)
**Visual:** Three examples appear as split comparison cards:
1. Road signs: "STOP" text vs red octagon (universally recognized)
2. IKEA instructions: text manual vs pictographic steps
3. Data presentation: spreadsheet numbers vs bar chart
**Audio:** "This isn't just lab curiosity. It's why road signs use shapes and colors, not paragraphs. Why IKEA instructions work across 50 countries with zero translation. Why executives ask for charts, not tables. Your brain processes images 60,000 times faster than text."

### Frame 5: The Catch - Not All Pictures (1:40-2:05, 25s)
**Visual:** Comparison grid showing effective vs ineffective pictures
- Top row (green checkmarks): Simple icon, clear diagram, relevant photo
- Bottom row (red X): Decorative clip art, unrelated stock photo, overly complex infographic
- Middle shows brain with "Processing Load" meter rising for bottom row
**Audio:** "But here's the catch: decorative pictures backfire. A random stock photo doesn't help—it distracts. Overly complex infographics create cognitive overload. The picture has to be relevant, simple, and directly linked to the concept. Paivio's effect isn't about pretty—it's about encoding."

### Frame 6: Actionable Takeaways (2:05-2:45, 40s)
**Visual:** Three-step framework appears as vertical progression with icons:
1. 🔍 "NOTICE" - Eye icon with image symbol
2. ❓ "ASK" - Question mark with relevance arrows
3. 🧠 "REMEMBER" - Brain with dual pathways
**Audio:** "Here's how to use it. One: Notice when you're presenting information. Are you using text where a picture would work? Two: Ask if the image is relevant. Does it encode the actual concept, or is it decoration? Three: Remember dual coding—the best materials combine simple visuals with clear verbal explanation. That's why this video has graphics you see and narration you hear, not text I'm reading to you."
