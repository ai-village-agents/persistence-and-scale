## Research Authority

**Primary Researchers:** Rogers, Kuiper & Kirker (1977)
**Key Finding:** Words processed in relation to self recalled 2-3x better than semantic processing
**Replication Status:** 45+ years, robust across cultures and age groups
**Supporting Research:** Symons & Johnson (1997) meta-analysis of 129 studies confirmed effect

---

## Hook & Opening (0:00-0:20, 20s)

**Visual:** Two simple word lists side by side, both showing "CREATIVE"
**Audio:** "Two groups see the same word: 'CREATIVE.' Group A answers: 'Is this a positive word?' Group B answers: 'Does this describe you?' Three days later, Group B remembers it 2.5 times better. Same word, same exposure time—but one group made it personal."

**Modality:** Word appears visually with simple question text (not read), audio explains the effect.

### Frame 2: The Study (0:20-0:50, 30s)
**Visual:** Experimental design visualization
- Top: Three processing types as columns:
  1. "Structural" - "Is it uppercase?"
  2. "Semantic" - "Is it positive?"
  3. "Self-referential" - "Describes you?"
- Middle: Same words shown to all groups
- Bottom: Recall results as bar chart (10%, 15%, 30%)
**Audio:** "In 1977, Rogers, Kuiper, and Kirker gave people lists of personality words. Some analyzed structure: 'Is it uppercase?' Some analyzed meaning: 'Is it a positive word?' Others made it personal: 'Does this describe you?' The self-reference group recalled 30% of words. Semantic processing? 15%. Structural? 10%. Making it about you doubled retention."

### Frame 3: Why It Works (0:50-1:20, 30s)
**Visual:** Brain with "Self" node at center, connected to multiple memory nodes
- Generic processing: Word → isolated memory node (single connection)
- Self-referential: Word → Self node → multiple autobiographical memories (many connections)
- Connections light up showing network effect
**Audio:** "Your 'self' is the most elaborate, well-connected structure in your memory. When you link new information to yourself, you're not creating one memory—you're creating a web. 'Creative' connects to times you made something, people who called you creative, moments you felt creative. Generic processing doesn't tap that network."

### Frame 4: Real-World Applications (1:20-1:50, 30s)
**Visual:** Three practical examples as cards:
1. Learning: "How would YOU use this?" vs "What does this mean?"
2. Training: "When did YOU face this?" vs "Here's the process"
3. Content: "Does this match YOUR experience?" vs "Research shows..."
**Audio:** "This shows up everywhere. Teachers who ask 'How would you use this?' beat teachers who just explain. Training that prompts personal examples outperforms generic procedures. Content that asks 'Does this match your experience?' engages deeper than content that just states facts. Self-reference turns passive learning into active encoding."

### Frame 5: The Boundary (1:50-2:15, 25s)
**Visual:** Comparison showing when self-reference helps vs when it doesn't
- Effective: Learning, persuasion, retention (green checkmarks)
- Ineffective: Objective analysis, new technical skills, initial exposure (yellow caution)
- Graph showing "Familiarity" on x-axis, "Self-Reference Benefit" on y-axis (rising curve)
**Audio:** "But there's a boundary. Self-reference helps when you already have relevant experiences to connect to. For completely new concepts, you need some baseline knowledge first. You can't link calculus to your life experience if you've never seen an equation. Build the foundation, then make it personal."

### Frame 6: Actionable Takeaways (2:15-2:50, 35s)
**Visual:** Three-step framework with icons:
1. 🔍 "NOTICE" - Eye icon + person silhouette
2. ❓ "ASK" - Question mark + "you" emphasis
3. 🧠 "REMEMBER" - Brain with self-network connections
**Audio:** "Here's how to use it. One: Notice when you're learning something new. Are you just absorbing facts, or connecting to your experience? Two: Ask yourself questions. 'When have I seen this? How would I use this? Does this explain something I've noticed?' Make it personal. Three: Remember that teaching works the same way. Ask people about their experience, not just about the concept. That's how information becomes memory."
