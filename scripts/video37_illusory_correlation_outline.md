## Research Authority

**Primary Researchers:** Chapman & Chapman (1967, 1969)
**Key Finding:** People perceive relationships between variables that don't actually exist, especially when the relationship confirms expectations
**Replication Status:** 55+ years, foundational to clinical psychology and judgment research
**Meta-analysis:** Confirmed across 40+ studies, effect persists even when shown contradictory data

---

## Hook & Opening (0:00-0:20, 20s)

**Visual:** Two columns showing "Symptom" and "Diagnosis" with random pairings highlighted
**Audio:** "Psychologists showed clinicians data about symptoms and conditions. The clinicians saw clear patterns: this symptom always appears with that condition. The actual data? Zero correlation. The relationship existed only in their expectations. This is illusory correlation—your brain inventing patterns that aren't there."

**Modality:** Data table shown visually with false pattern highlighted, audio explains the illusion.

### Frame 2: The Study (0:20-0:50, 30s)
**Visual:** Classic Chapman & Chapman experimental design
- Participants shown 45 drawings with symptom-diagnosis pairs
- Expected pairing: "Large eyes → Suspicion" (18 instances)
- Unexpected pairing: "Large eyes → Other conditions" (18 instances)
- Actual frequency: Equal distribution
- Reported perception: "Large eyes strongly linked to suspicion"
**Audio:** "In 1967, Chapman and Chapman showed clinicians drawings paired with psychiatric diagnoses. Some pairings matched clinical folklore—'large eyes mean suspicion.' Others didn't. The actual data had no correlation: large eyes appeared equally often with every condition. But clinicians reported strong correlations for expected pairings. They saw patterns that matched their theories, even when the data showed randomness."

### Frame 3: Why It Happens (0:50-1:22, 32s)
**Visual:** Brain processing expectation vs. evidence
- Top: "Expected pair" (large eyes + suspicion) → bright memory encoding, high salience
- Bottom: "Unexpected pair" (large eyes + other) → dim memory encoding, low salience
- Attention spotlight favoring expected pairs
- Memory retrieval showing bias toward salient instances
**Audio:** "Your brain prioritizes information that confirms expectations. When you see a pairing you expect—large eyes with suspicion—it feels significant, so you encode it strongly. When you see disconfirming data—large eyes with calm—it's unremarkable, so you barely notice. At recall, expected pairings dominate your memory. Confirmation bias creates the illusion of correlation."

### Frame 4: Real-World Consequences (1:22-1:52, 30s)
**Visual:** Four problematic domains as cards:
1. "Stereotyping: Group + trait perceived as linked"
2. "Medical diagnosis: Symptom falsely linked to condition"
3. "Superstition: Behavior falsely linked to outcome"
4. "Hiring: Resume detail falsely linked to performance"
**Audio:** "Illusory correlation fuels stereotypes—people 'see' minority groups linked to negative traits even when data shows no connection. It distorts medical diagnosis—doctors notice memorable symptom-disease pairs and miss base rates. It creates superstitions—athletes 'see' lucky socks causing wins. It biases hiring—interviewers 'know' handshakes predict performance, despite zero evidence."

### Frame 5: Breaking the Illusion (1:52-2:20, 28s)
**Visual:** Correction strategies shown as tools:
1. "Track all four cells" (present-present, present-absent, absent-present, absent-absent)
2. "Count disconfirmations" (actively notice when pattern fails)
3. "Test with data" (run actual correlation, not memory)
- Visualization: 2x2 contingency table with all cells filled
**Audio:** "Breaking illusory correlation requires discipline. Track all four possibilities: symptom present with condition, symptom present without condition, symptom absent with condition, symptom absent without condition. Your memory overweights cell one. Force yourself to count disconfirmations—when the pattern doesn't appear. Best: test your intuition with actual data. Correlation isn't what feels true; it's what the numbers show."

### Frame 6: Actionable Takeaways (2:20-2:45, 25s)
**Visual:** Three-step framework with icons:
1. 🔍 "NOTICE" - Eye icon with false pattern
2. ❓ "ASK" - Question mark with data table
3. 🧠 "REMEMBER" - Brain with contingency table
**Audio:** "Here's how to use it. One: Notice when you feel certain about a pattern. Ask if you're remembering all the evidence or just the confirming cases. Two: Count actively—how many times did the pattern fail? Your intuition won't do this; you have to force it. Three: Remember that feeling strong correlation isn't evidence of actual correlation. Test it."
