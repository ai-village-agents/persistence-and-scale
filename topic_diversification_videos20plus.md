## CATEGORY 1: SOCIAL PSYCHOLOGY

### 1. Mere Exposure Effect (Zajonc, 1968)
- **Concept:** Repeated exposure to stimuli increases liking
- **Research:** Robert Zajonc's landmark studies on familiarity and preference
- **Actionable takeaway:** Why first impressions aren't everything; consistency builds trust
- **Visual potential:** HIGH - show frequency/preference graphs, real-world applications
- **Estimated quality:** 4.7/5 (well-established, highly relatable)

### 2. Fundamental Attribution Error (Ross, 1977)
- **Concept:** Overestimate personality factors, underestimate situational factors in explaining others' behavior
- **Research:** Lee Ross, classic social psychology
- **Actionable takeaway:** Consider context before judging; avoid snap character judgments
- **Visual potential:** HIGH - scenarios comparing dispositional vs. situational explanations
- **Estimated quality:** 4.7/5 (highly applicable to daily life)

### 3. Confirmation Bias (Wason, 1960)
- **Concept:** Tendency to search for, interpret, and recall information confirming pre-existing beliefs
- **Research:** Peter Wason's selection task, subsequent meta-analyses
- **Actionable takeaway:** Actively seek disconfirming evidence; test rather than validate
- **Visual potential:** HIGH - Wason selection task visualization, belief-evidence diagrams
- **Estimated quality:** 4.8/5 (universally relevant, strong research base)

### 4. Planning Fallacy (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979)
- **Concept:** Underestimating time, costs, and risks of future actions
- **Research:** Kahneman/Tversky, Buehler et al. follow-up studies
- **Actionable takeaway:** Use outside view; reference class forecasting
- **Visual potential:** MEDIUM - timeline comparisons, prediction vs. reality
- **Estimated quality:** 4.7/5 (practical, relatable)
- **Note:** Still Kahneman, but distinct from other queue items

### 5. Halo Effect (Thorndike, 1920)
- **Concept:** One positive trait influences perception of other unrelated traits
- **Research:** Edward Thorndike's original research, Nisbett & Wilson extensions
- **Actionable takeaway:** Evaluate traits independently; avoid "beautiful = good" assumptions
- **Visual potential:** HIGH - trait correlation diagrams, practical examples
- **Estimated quality:** 4.6/5 (clear concept, strong applications)
- **Note:** Video 20 materials already exist but abandoned due to audio issue - could remake

## CATEGORY 2: BEHAVIORAL ECONOMICS (Beyond Kahneman)

### 6. Mental Accounting (Thaler, 1985)
- **Concept:** People treat money differently depending on its source or intended use
- **Research:** Richard Thaler's groundbreaking work
- **Actionable takeaway:** Money is fungible; recognize arbitrary mental categories
- **Visual potential:** HIGH - budget buckets, spending scenarios
- **Estimated quality:** 4.7/5 (Thaler's Nobel-winning work, very practical)

### 7. Hyperbolic Discounting (Ainslie, 1975)
- **Concept:** People value immediate rewards disproportionately over delayed rewards
- **Research:** George Ainslie, Frederick et al.
- **Actionable takeaway:** Pre-commit to long-term goals; recognize present bias
- **Visual potential:** HIGH - discount curves, marshmallow test references
- **Estimated quality:** 4.6/5 (strong concept, clear visualization)

### 8. Sunk Cost Fallacy (Arkes & Blumer, 1985)
- **Concept:** Continuing endeavor because of previously invested resources
- **Research:** Arkes & Blumer, subsequent behavioral economics literature
- **Actionable takeaway:** Make decisions based on future value, not past investment
- **Visual potential:** HIGH - investment/value timelines, decision trees
- **Estimated quality:** 4.8/5 (universally applicable, clear concept)

### 9. Status Quo Bias (Samuelson & Zeckhauser, 1988)
- **Concept:** Preference for current state; resistance to change
- **Research:** Samuelson & Zeckhauser, default effects literature
- **Actionable takeaway:** Question defaults; periodically re-evaluate baselines
- **Visual potential:** MEDIUM - choice comparisons, default effects
- **Estimated quality:** 4.6/5 (practical but less visceral than other biases)

## CATEGORY 3: LEARNING SCIENCE (Extended)

### 10. Interleaving (Rohrer & Taylor, 2007)
- **Concept:** Mixing different types of practice improves retention vs. blocking
- **Research:** Doug Rohrer, Bjork lab studies
- **Actionable takeaway:** Mix up practice problems; alternate topics in study sessions
- **Visual potential:** HIGH - study schedule comparisons, performance graphs
- **Estimated quality:** 4.7/5 (counterintuitive, strong evidence)

### 11. Desirable Difficulties (Bjork, 1994)
- **Concept:** Introducing challenges during learning improves long-term retention
- **Research:** Robert Bjork's seminal work
- **Actionable takeaway:** Embrace struggle in learning; avoid false fluency
- **Visual potential:** MEDIUM-HIGH - performance curves, difficulty/retention relationship
- **Estimated quality:** 4.7/5 (Bjork's authority, practical for students)

### 12. Generation Effect (Slamecka & Graf, 1978)
- **Concept:** Information generated by oneself is better remembered than information read
- **Research:** Slamecka & Graf, Roediger extensions
- **Actionable takeaway:** Create, don't just consume; practice retrieval
- **Visual potential:** MEDIUM - memory performance comparisons
- **Estimated quality:** 4.6/5 (clear concept, actionable)

### 13. Zeigarnik Effect (Zeigarnik, 1927)
- **Concept:** Uncompleted tasks are remembered better than completed ones
- **Research:** Bluma Zeigarnik's original studies, modern replications
- **Actionable takeaway:** Use open loops for motivation; manage mental clutter with closure
- **Visual potential:** MEDIUM - task memory diagrams, practical scenarios
- **Estimated quality:** 4.5/5 (interesting but less robust replication)

## CATEGORY 4: MEMORY & COGNITION

### 14. Levels of Processing (Craik & Lockhart, 1972)
- **Concept:** Depth of mental processing affects memory retention
- **Research:** Craik & Lockhart's framework, extensive follow-up
- **Actionable takeaway:** Process information deeply; ask "why" and "how"
- **Visual potential:** HIGH - processing depth hierarchy, retention comparisons
- **Estimated quality:** 4.7/5 (foundational memory research)

### 15. Working Memory Limits (Miller, 1956 / Cowan, 2001)
- **Concept:** "Magical number 7 ± 2" (Miller) refined to ~4 chunks (Cowan)
- **Research:** George Miller, Nelson Cowan's updates
- **Actionable takeaway:** Chunk information; externalize working memory with notes
- **Visual potential:** HIGH - capacity demonstrations, chunking examples
- **Estimated quality:** 4.7/5 (classic cognitive psychology)

### 16. Serial Position Effect (Ebbinghaus, 1885)
- **Concept:** Primacy and recency effects in memory recall
- **Research:** Ebbinghaus's original work, modern extensions
- **Actionable takeaway:** Structure important info at beginning/end; review midpoints
- **Visual potential:** HIGH - recall curves, practical applications
- **Estimated quality:** 4.6/5 (clear pattern, practical)

## CATEGORY 5: JUDGMENT & DECISION-MAKING

### 17. Framing Effects (Tversky & Kahneman, 1981)
- **Concept:** Presentation of information affects decisions
- **Research:** Asian disease problem, gain/loss framing
- **Actionable takeaway:** Reframe problems in multiple ways before deciding
- **Visual potential:** HIGH - side-by-side framings, decision shifts
- **Estimated quality:** 4.8/5 (powerful demonstrations, Kahneman/Tversky authority)
- **Note:** Kahneman again, but such a strong concept it may warrant inclusion

### 18. Hindsight Bias (Fischhoff, 1975)
- **Concept:** "I knew it all along" effect after outcomes are known
- **Research:** Baruch Fischhoff's landmark work
- **Actionable takeaway:** Document predictions; acknowledge uncertainty
- **Visual potential:** MEDIUM - before/after probability assessments
- **Estimated quality:** 4.6/5 (relatable, clear implications)

### 19. Dunning-Kruger Effect (Kruger & Dunning, 1999)
- **Concept:** Incompetent individuals overestimate their competence
- **Research:** Kruger & Dunning's studies across domains
- **Actionable takeaway:** Seek feedback; competence correlates with recognizing limits
- **Visual potential:** HIGH - competence/confidence graphs (inverse U or cross)
- **Estimated quality:** 4.7/5 (widely known, strong engagement potential)
- **Caveat:** Ensure nuanced treatment; avoid meme-level simplification
